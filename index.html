US Adds 130,000 Jobs in January, Unemployment Falls to 4.3% Brew

Despite warnings from the White House, the January jobs report exceeded expectations with the strongest month of employment gains since December 2024, fueled by healthcare, construction, and professional services.

Healthcare added 82,000 jobs, the most in a month since 2020. Construction gained 33,000 jobs, professional and business services added 34,000, and manufacturing posted 5,000 new positions—the sector's first monthly gain in more than a year.

The upbeat report makes it more likely that the Fed will continue to hold interest rates steady, at least until Jerome Powell's term as Fed chair ends in May. The number of people working part-time for economic reasons fell by 453,000.

Looking back: The BLS adjusted job numbers downward for the past two years. For 2024, the US added 1.5 million jobs, down from the previously estimated 2 million.

Who Will You Be?

Throughout his life, Abraham Lincoln—born on this day in 1809—could not seem to please his father. Even though his son was brilliant and clearly cut out for something other than subsistence farming, Thomas Lincoln resented his son's talent. He hated that he was always reading. He rented him out as a laborer. He nearly wore him down. In the end, Thomas died more or less estranged from his son, then a well-known politician and successful lawyer.

Lincoln, with his own children, went the other direction. He bound them to him by the cords of affection. He never used corporal punishment. He loved to play with them. He took them to work with him. He embraced their craziness. "It is my pleasure that my children are free," Lincoln said, "happy and unrestrained by parental tyranny."

Given his own childhood, it makes sense. It's sweet. He loved his boys. He also knew that life—especially back then—was precarious and short. Only one of his sons would live to adulthood, and Lincoln himself would die tragically.

Which parent would you rather be, then? Imperious and impossible to please? Or fun and proud and loving? You know the answer.

AI Rundown

China's Z.ai Launches GLM-5, New Open-Source Frontier Model

China's Z.ai launched GLM-5, a 744B-parameter open-weights model that sits just behind Claude Opus 4.6 and GPT-5.2 on benchmarks, further closing the gap with Western AI leaders.

GLM-5 scored 50 on Artificial Analysis' Intelligence Index, surpassing closed models like Gemini 3 Pro and Grok 4 as well as open-source ones like Kimi K2.5. On Humanity's Last Exam, it hit 50.4 with tools, beating Opus 4.5, Gemini 3 Pro, and GPT-5.2.

The model uses DeepSeek's Sparse Attention architecture with just 40B active parameters, runs inference on Chinese chips including Huawei Ascend, and is available under an MIT license at $1 per million input tokens.

Why it matters: The gap with the West isn't closed yet, but with open weights, competitive pricing, and domestic chip support, it's narrowing faster than ever.

AI Regulation Becomes Electoral Issue as PACs Pour $145M Into Midterms

The AI industry's policy debate has spilled into electoral politics. Anthropic's $20 million donation to Public First directly counters the $125 million war chest assembled by OpenAI executives and Andreessen Horowitz.

Public First is already running ads backing Sen. Marsha Blackburn's gubernatorial bid in Tennessee and supporting Sen. Pete Ricketts in Nebraska, both Republicans who favor stricter AI oversight and chip-export controls.

OpenAI Launches Ads in ChatGPT as $100B Burn Rate Looms

OpenAI finds itself in an uncomfortable position: spending like a tech giant while earning like a startup. The company expects to burn through $100 billion over the next four years on computing infrastructure.

OpenAI has begun serving advertisements inside ChatGPT's free tier, targeting the 94% of its 800 million users who don't pay for subscriptions. The company has hired hundreds of employees from Meta and X to build its ad infrastructure.

The deeper risk is reputational: Anthropic already ran a Super Bowl ad mocking the move, and users who chose ChatGPT partly for its clean interface may reconsider if answers start arriving with commercial baggage.

International Brew

Switzerland to Vote on Population Cap at 10 Million

The Swiss government scheduled a June referendum on whether to cap the country's population at 10 million—a measure that could limit immigration and force withdrawal from EU treaties.

Switzerland's population grew 10% to 9.1 million in the past decade—five times faster than that of the EU. Promoted by a right-wing party, the ballot measure would restrict newcomers once the population reaches 9.5 million.

At 10 million, Switzerland would start withdrawing from international treaties like its free movement accord with the EU. A November poll showed 48% support, 41% opposed, and 11% undecided.
