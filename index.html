Google's AI Cracks Nine Decades-Old Math Problems in One Day

A day after OpenAI claimed its own Erdős breakthrough, Google DeepMind quietly did them eight better — autonomously solving nine open problems considered among the hardest unsolved questions in mathematics.

Google DeepMind's AlphaProof Nexus solved nine open Erdős problems — including two unsolved for 56 years — spanning combinatorics and graph theory. The system paired a large language model with Lean, a formal proof assistant, to generate machine-verified proofs at a cost of just a few hundred dollars per problem.

The AI also proved 44 open conjectures from the Online Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences. A simpler version of the agent matched results at higher cost, and problems requiring entirely new mathematical constructions remained out of reach.

This comes just one day after OpenAI claimed its AI disproved an 80-year-old Erdős conjecture — months after walking back an earlier claim of solving 10 novel problems. Google's formal verification approach changes the game: the system generates proofs, checks them in Lean, and repeats until one passes. Over time, this enables researchers to make novel discoveries at machine speed.

Columnist | Daily Dad

You Never Know Where They'll Find Their Thing

Nobody knows where your kids will find that thing that lights them up — the thing that makes them think, for the first time, I want to do that with my life.

It could happen at school. It could happen on that trip where you took them out of school. It could happen while they're home sick, watching TV. It could happen at a museum or a music store. At the zoo. During a surfing lesson, stumbling across a fossil, or while reading a book. You don't know — nobody does — where your kids will find their thing.

That's probably why the great Robert Greene (in his book Mastery) calls it our "life's task." The life's task of a parent, we might say, is to help our kids find their life's task.

Which is why we have to cast a wide net. We have to travel with them, read to them, watch things with them, take them to hear and see stuff. Be deliberate — and deliberately allow serendipity into their lives. Think about how random it was when you found your own thing. What a freak occurrence. What a miracle. We have to do the work to recreate that for them. It's not something we can give them, but it is a process we can facilitate, encourage, and most of all, never stifle.

Build an AI Secretary That Plans Your Day

Use Codex or Claude Code to build an AI taskmaster that checks Slack, Gmail, and your calendar every morning — turning the mess into a prioritized to-do list that gets better every day you use it.

  1. Create a folder, open Claude Code inside it, and ask it to create a skill that looks at your Slack, Gmail, and calendar daily, prioritizes tasks high to low, and puts them at the top of MonoNote.md with date, feedback, and status checkboxes.
  2. Tell the agent to create MonoNote.md and task-rules.md, and add any high/low priority rules you have per task into task-rules.md.
  3. Run the skill and review the list. MonoNote.md should have today's date at the top, grouped tasks, source links, and checkboxes you can use all day.
  4. After the first run, ask the agent to create the automation. It will review yesterday's feedback, roll over bumped tasks, update the rules, then generate the new list.

Pro tip: Create a weekly audit skill that scans your task list, finds repeated tasks, and suggests which ones you could automate with AI.

The Rockefeller Approach to Productivity: Clockwork Over Speed

Rockefeller wasn't the fastest or smartest man in the room. He was slow, methodical, and relentlessly focused on winning long-term while everyone else chased speed.

His approach, detailed in Ron Chernow's Titan: build a schedule that fits you and stick to it for years, not weeks, so every action compounds. Plan like chess — mapping every counter-move before committing. Once you execute, never look back. Treat work as a calling, not a 9-to-5 obligation. He approached his entire life with what one writer called "clockwork regularity" — and it never felt like a grind.
