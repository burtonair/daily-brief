Miami Is Now More Expensive to Live In Than New York City

For the first time ever, the Miami metro has surpassed greater New York in total cost of living — a stunning reversal driven by soaring home prices, sky-high insurance, and restaurant bills that now dwarf Manhattan’s.

Consumer prices for Miamians have risen 36% since 2019 — more than any other US metro area except Tampa. Home prices have surged 79% since the pandemic, and while Miami’s per-square-foot prices remain below New York’s, the average total housing cost is now 5% higher than in the tri-state area. Morning Brew

Property taxes have skyrocketed 62% since 2019, per Attom. Residents in older buildings now face mandatory costly safety upgrades following the 2021 Surfside condo collapse, on top of the steepest homeowners’ insurance rates in the country. An average restaurant check in Miami hit $94 last year versus $79 in New York, per OpenTable.

Miami workers earn $1,000 less per year than the median American, with white-collar wages also trailing New York’s. The transplants who fled New York for cheaper living are now paying more — while billionaires like Citadel’s Ken Griffin (who bought a $107 million Miami estate this year), Zuckerberg, and Google co-founders Brin and Page aren’t sweating the dinner bill. Columnist  |  Daily Dad

You Will Wish You Said It

It’s not like you never said it. It’s just that you were busy. It’s just that they rolled their eyes when you did. It’s just that you didn’t want to spoil them. It’s just that it wasn’t a good time.

Of course, you love them. Of course, you are proud of them. Of course, some part of you thinks this should be obvious. But it isn’t obvious enough. They need to hear it again. They need to hear it more. Because someday, you may realize you missed your chance to say the thing they needed from you most.

Mark Twain felt profound sorrow after losing his daughter Susy. Deep in mourning, he wrote of how much he regretted not being more expressive to and about her. “And I felt like saying, ‘You marvelous child!’ but never said it; to my sorrow I remember it now.”

Don’t wait. Don’t hold back. If there’s something positive you want to say, say it while you can. Tell them. Tell them again. Put it in writing. Put it to them directly. Call them out of the blue. Take them out to lunch. Make a habit of it. Maybe they won’t remember every word. But you’ll never have to remember that you didn’t say it.

Jensen Huang’s Shifting Number: AI Data Center Costs Have Tripled — and Markets May Not Have Priced It In

At GTC Taipei, Nvidia’s CEO mentioned in passing that the cost to build a one-gigawatt AI “factory” has gone from $20–30 billion to $50–60 billion — and will soon hit $80–100 billion. He delivered the tripling as an aside. The market treated it as bullish.

That cost-per-gigawatt figure is the hinge on which the entire AI sector’s trillion-dollar arithmetic turns. Analysts multiply it by forecast demand to size the market. Everyone works from the same equation — they simply now disagree, by a factor of three, about one of its key terms.

The question raised: is the market actually pricing in how fast AI data center costs keep rising? When the most bullish voice in the room quietly triples a core assumption on his way to a different point, it’s worth pausing on the valuations built around those numbers.

Poolside’s Laguna S 2.1: Strongest US Open-Source Coding Model Yet — But China Still Leads

Poolside launched Laguna S 2.1, an open-weights coding model that tops Western open-source benchmarks while fitting on a single desktop GPU. It still trails China’s Kimi K3 significantly, but signals that American open-source is building momentum.

S 2.1 uses just 8B of its 118B parameters at inference time, running on one Nvidia DGX Spark desktop. Weights are free on Hugging Face. Training took under nine weeks. Co-CEO Jason Warner told Forbes the aim is “the most capable open model in the West.”

The release follows Mira Murati’s TML releasing Inkling last week. Two US open-source releases in a week don’t erase China’s deep lead — Kimi K3 is still lapping the field — but they show American open-source has momentum instead of just a gap.
