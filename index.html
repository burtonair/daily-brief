Retail Investors Go From Meme to Mainstream

The class of investors that chose a dog wearing a tie as its avatar during the GameStop days now looks a lot more like your parents—and they're beating the S&P 500 for a third straight year.

Last year, individual investors outperformed the S&P 500, relegating the "dumb money" stereotype to the pandemic-era zeitgeist for good. The Goldman Sachs 2025 "retail favorites" basket gained 30.5% on the year, compared to the S&P 500's 16.4% rise.

Twenty-somethings are increasingly moving significant amounts of money from checking accounts to investing accounts. More than 33% of 25-year-olds did so in 2024, up from just 6% in 2015, according to JPMorgan data. Their favorites: Nvidia, Amazon, Reddit, AppLovin, Palantir, and Tesla.

But the hidden world of SPVs raises questions. At least 170 special purpose vehicles related to SpaceX have been set up since 2020, with investors sometimes not knowing how far removed they are from the actual asset. "We've never had people in a more gambling mood than now," Warren Buffett warned at Berkshire's annual meeting.

The $16.5 Million Pikachu and the Booming Collectibles Market

Vintage video games, trading cards, rare wines, and dinosaur fossils are setting new records as the wealthy hedge volatility with nostalgia.

In February, Logan Paul sold a rare Pikachu Illustrator card for $16.5 million, smashing the previous Pokémon card record he set five years earlier. A 1945 bottle of Domaine de la Romanée-Conti sold for a record $812,500 in March.

In 2021, a sealed copy of the 1985 Super Mario Bros. sold for $2 million. And a yearning for the Jurassic period has the wealthy spending exorbitant amounts on dinosaur bones—since 2020, three nearly complete fossils have fetched over $30 million apiece.

The booming collectible market stands in sharp contrast to the sputtering art market, which reported just 4% global sales growth last year after declining the previous year.

Wealth Morning Brew

Family Offices Are How the Ultrarich Roll—and They're Multiplying

More ultrawealthy families than ever are establishing private companies solely focused on managing their cash, and they're projected to surpass hedge funds by 2030.

Family offices employ professional wealth managers who execute financial strategies set by family members—and sometimes manage rich families' chores, from taxes to yacht purchases and travel.

The number of family offices was projected to exceed 9,000 last year, managing $3.1 trillion. Deloitte predicts that figure will hit $5.4 trillion by 2030. They're increasingly moving into private dealmaking and real estate, and some families are pooling resources into multifamily offices to combine financial firepower.

Women's Sports: From Underdog to Billion-Dollar Club

The WNBA's Golden State Valkyries just became the first women's team in any sport valued at $1 billion—up from a $50 million purchase price in 2023.

From 2022 to 2024, women's sports grew 4.5 times faster than men's sports, according to McKinsey. By 2030, US women's sports are projected to generate at least $2.5 billion, up from about $1 billion in 2024.

Fans are breaking attendance records, and the WNBA and NWSL's combined sponsorships jumped nearly 33% last year.
