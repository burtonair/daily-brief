What Will the Ternus Era at Apple Look Like?

With Tim Cook stepping aside in September, incoming CEO John Ternus—a mechanical engineer and hardware lifer—faces the defining question: can the guy who built AirPods and led the Mac chip transition steer Apple through the AI revolution? Morning Brew

Ternus has worked at Apple since 2001 and served as Senior VP for hardware engineering. Employees reportedly love him: he’s decisive, focused, a good collaborator, and has risen above internal politics. He helped develop AirPods, facilitated the Mac’s switch to Apple silicon, and pushed the MacBook Neo—the cheaper, colorful laptop that sold out almost immediately.

Critics argue Apple has been slow on AI, falling behind competitors. Fans counter that Apple wisely let others dump hundreds of billions into data centers and LLMs that Apple can simply run on its devices. Ternus recently reorganized Apple’s hardware engineering department to prepare for faster AI product development.

Looking ahead: After product flops like the Vision Pro and the autonomous car, Apple has its sights set on big AI-powered launches: a more chatbot-like Siri, wearables, smart home devices, and a foldable iPhone.

This Is Your Best?

We’ve all said it. In moments of frustration. In fights. We’ve heard our own parents say it. “Hey, I’m doing my best.” But are you? Look at your screen time app right now. That was your best effort at being present with your kids over the last week? Think about the times you lost your temper. That was you at your best? Really?

Of course, this parenting thing is hard. It’s exhausting. None of us are perfect. Which is why we have to try really hard. Which is why we have to be consciously trying to do our best, not just paying lip service to the idea.

If we want to do better, we have to notice—we have to be present. If we want to grow, we have to reflect. Not just when we blow it. Not just when there’s a crisis. That’s why a daily practice matters. If you want to stop repeating what you swore you’d never repeat, good intentions aren’t enough. You need a practice you can return to, day after day.

Begin today. Keep the practice, and let it keep you.

Economy • Supply Chain

Condom Prices to Spike 20–30% Because of Iran War Morning Brew

Karex, the world’s largest condom producer (one in every five condoms worldwide), plans to raise prices 20–30% due to supply chain disruptions.

Production costs are up 25–30% since strikes on Iran began, amid shortages of oil-derived condom materials. Global freight disruptions have doubled Karex’s European and US shipping times from one month to two.

Compounding the crunch: since the US slashed USAID funding, condom stockpiles in some developing nations have dwindled. Karex said its outlook is hazy beyond two to three months and can’t rule out further increases.
