Columnist | Daily Dad

What Your Children Learn From Your Anger

Your son knocks over his milk at dinner — the third time this week. Your daughter dawdles getting ready for school, making everyone late again. Your teenager puts a huge scratch on the driver's side of your car. What happens next matters more than you might realize.

In these seemingly small moments, you are teaching your children one of life's most important lessons: how to respond when things don't go as planned. George Washington, a student of Stoic philosophy, insisted on viewing challenges "in the calm light of mild philosophy." This wasn't weakness — this was the discipline that helped him lead a revolution and build a nation.

The wise don't fly off the handle. They don't say the first thought that enters their mind. They pause. They reflect. They choose their response. Emperor Augustus used an alphabet exercise to master his temper. Seneca warned that anger distorts us beyond recognition. The ability to pause before reacting isn't just good parenting advice — it's the secret to success and happiness that great leaders throughout history have practiced.

Your children aren't just inheriting your features — they're inheriting your responses. The parent who masters their reactions gives their children a priceless gift: the knowledge that emotions are to be felt, but not always acted upon.

Audit Claude's Context of You and Your Work — In 15 Minutes

Claude builds up assumptions about you over time — your role, priorities, workflows, even old side projects it still treats as active. Here's how to surface and clean up what it thinks it knows, so your AI stays sharp instead of subtly off-track.

  1. Prompt Claude: "Audit your context and memory assumptions about me. Put them in a table: what you believe, why you believe it, your confidence level, and whether each item is confirmed. Cover my role, priorities, KPIs, tools, workflows, and anything you may be over-weighting from old chats or projects."
  2. Review the table for stale assumptions, dead side projects, one-off tests, or personal questions Claude may be treating like real work.
  3. Turn the audit into an interview: "Now interview me about the assumptions, outdated items, and unknowns. Ask in rounds using MCQs wherever possible. After each round, summarize what changed."
  4. Answer the questions, tell Claude to update its memory, and save the report with next steps to improve your AI workflows.

Pro tip: Ask Claude to turn this audit-interview-report process into a reusable skill. Rerun every quarter so its context stays aligned with your actual priorities.

Community workflow from Curtis B. in Raleigh, NC: "I took a cute picture of my almost 2-year-old son and my dad in a canoe together, and had AI convert it into a coloring page. I used ChatGPT to engineer the prompt, then Gemini's Nano Banana for image generation. After a couple of iterations: simple bold lines, but the faces were still recognizable. Printed it out, had my toddler go to town with his crayons. My dad loved it, and the whole family was asking how I did it."

OpenAI Model Disproves an 80-Year Math Belief

Sam Altman called it "a kinda big milestone" — a rare case of a tech CEO underselling a headline. An internal general-purpose reasoning model autonomously disproved a conjecture tied to Erdős' 1946 unit distance problem, what OpenAI is calling a first for AI in novel mathematical discovery.

The proof draws on algebraic number theory — a different branch of math entirely — and was verified by experts including Tim Gowers, Noga Alon, and Thomas Bloom. Crucially, this came from a general-purpose model being released soon — not a math-specific system like DeepMind's AlphaProof.

OAI's Alex Wei put it plainly: "Math is a leading indicator of what is to come." If a general-purpose model can autonomously disprove an 80-year argument with its own solution, that's the early look of "Level 4" AI — systems making original contributions across fields, not just accelerating existing work. Note: OpenAI previously walked back a 2025 claim that GPT-5 solved 10 Erdős problems, which turned out to be literature finds, not discoveries. This one was independently verified.

How Anthropic Got to Profitability First

Anthropic will post $559M in operating income on $10.9B in projected Q2 revenue — more than double Q1's $4.8B. But the profitability line is the lagging indicator. The cost curve is the leading one.

Compute cost per revenue dollar fell from 71¢ to 56¢ in a single quarter — a 21% improvement on a base already low vs. peers. The key factors: Anthropic runs TPUs from Google and Trainium from Amazon, both cheaper than Nvidia's frontier silicon; it has a small consumer surface unlike ChatGPT, avoiding the cost of hundreds of millions of free users; its revenue boom is concentrated in enterprise coding tools where it has clear category leadership; and it has been far more measured than OpenAI in locking up multi-year compute capacity.

The frame has shifted. The question is no longer whether AI unit economics work — but who else can run Anthropic's cost stack. When OpenAI's prospectus arrives, possibly as soon as Friday, it will need to answer for compute anchored in Nvidia and forward commitments tens of billions deeper than Anthropic's.
