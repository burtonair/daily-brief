Jury Tosses Musk’s Case Against OpenAI — But the Trial Transcript Lives Forever

Morning Brew The Rundown AI 1% Better

After three weeks of blockbuster testimony, a nine-member jury dismissed Elon Musk’s $100B+ lawsuit against OpenAI in under two hours — finding the case filed years too late. Musk called it a “calendar technicality” and vowed to appeal.

The suit alleged that Sam Altman “stole a charity” by converting OpenAI from a nonprofit to a for-profit entity. But the jury found the three-year statute of limitations had expired — OpenAI went for-profit in 2019, and Musk didn’t sue until 2024. Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers immediately dismissed the case. Microsoft, named as co-defendant for its multibillion-dollar backing of OpenAI, was also cleared. Musk posted on X that he will appeal.

The verdict was decisive — but the trial produced something more durable: a federal docket full of sworn testimony that didn’t previously exist. Ilya Sutskever testified that Altman exhibits a “consistent pattern of lying.” Brockman confirmed his OpenAI stake is worth nearly $30 billion. Internal Microsoft emails about the 2023 board crisis are now on the public record. And Altman, who told Congress he had no direct OpenAI stake, acknowledged roughly $2 billion in adjacent equity in companies doing business with the lab.

OpenAI’s PBC restructure — valued at $852 billion, with both California and Delaware AGs issuing statements of no objection — was already legally settled before the trial began. Wall Street read Monday’s verdict as a green light for a potential $1 trillion IPO. But as 1% Better notes: “The verdict closed Musk’s case. The transcript is the part everyone else now gets to read for free.”

Columnist • Daily Dad

They Need To Hear It Again

The most highly paid athletes in the world — people who have trained their whole lives and have every incentive to perform — still need their coaches to repeat the basics. Over and over.

But your kid? You assume they should get it because “you already told them?” If the pros need to hear it a thousand times, your kids probably need to hear it again. And by “it,” we mean everything — every lesson, every rule, every instruction. And by “again,” we mean essentially an infinite number of times.

Not just for sports, but for all the things you want them to understand. They’re going to need to hear it explained over and over. They’re going to need it simplified, boiled down to its essence. They’re going to need it put up on the wall, told via stories and examples, called out when it’s done well — and reviewed when it isn’t.

That’s the only way they’ll learn it. That’s the only way they — or anyone — can live up to it.

How To • The Rundown AI

3D Model Anything with Claude and Blender

Connect Blender to Claude Code via MCP, then use plain English to create and edit a full 3D scene — no 3D experience required.

  1. Download and install Blender and its MCP extension. In Blender, go to Edit > Preferences, search MCP, and enable the extension.
  2. In Terminal, open Claude Code in your project folder. Connect Blender with: claude mcp add blender -- uvx blender-mcp-server
  3. Ask Claude to verify setup: “Connect Claude Code to Blender and confirm the MCP config is set up correctly. Check Blender is running the MCP server, confirm the host and port, and verify the connection before editing.”
  4. Test it: “Use Blender MCP to model my name in 3D. Add disco balls and lighting, reflective materials, and a camera angle to make it like an event poster.”

Pro tip: Download models from BlenderKit, open them in Blender, and ask Claude to arrange objects, adjust lighting, and prepare a render.

AI • Coding

Cursor’s Composer 2.5 Hits Frontier Performance for Under $1 per Task

The Rundown AI

Cursor released Composer 2.5, an in-house coding model built on Moonshot’s Kimi K2.5 that benchmarks near Anthropic’s Claude 4.7 and OpenAI’s GPT-5.5 — at a fraction of the cost.

An average CursorBench task costs under $1 with Composer 2.5, compared to up to $11 per task for Opus 4.7 or GPT-5.5 at similar performance levels — a nearly 10% step up from its predecessor. Composer 2.5 was partially trained on xAI’s Colossus 2 compute cluster. Cursor is currently training a larger model with 10x more compute. With xAI’s compute now behind Cursor, the next model could be the one that takes over the frontier.

AI • World Models

Odyssey Drops First Real-Time Multimodal World Model — and the First Multiplayer One

The Rundown AI

Odyssey introduced Starchild-1 — the first real-time multimodal world model — and Agora-1, which lets up to 4 players interact inside the same AI-generated world stream simultaneously.

Starchild-1 generates synchronized audio and video on the fly while adjusting to user inputs with no fixed generation length. Agora-1 was demoed via a GoldenEye-style simulation where every pixel is produced live — tracking each agent’s position and health as actions change the shared world state.

Many of the sharpest minds in tech view world models as the industry’s next frontier. Going from rendered clips to live, adjustable shared streams opens whole new avenues for gaming, storytelling, robotics, and AI agent training in simulation.

Lululemon Fires Back at Founder Chip Wilson: “Misguided” and “Outdated”

Morning Brew

More than a decade after leaving, Lululemon founder Chip Wilson is trying to overhaul the company’s board — prompting the company’s first public response calling his views outdated and urging shareholders to back existing board members.

Lululemon’s revenue tripled in the nine years after Wilson left the board in 2015 — but its stock is now down ~75% from a 2023 peak amid competition from Vuori and Alo, tariffs, and a consumer spending pullback.
