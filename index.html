Ted Turner, CNN Founder and Media Mogul, Dies at 87

The brash Atlanta entrepreneur who launched CNN and created the 24-hour news cycle has died after a long battle with Lewy body dementia. He leaves behind a legacy that reshaped how America consumes news.

Ted Turner, the founder of CNN, creator of the 24-hour news cycle, and one of America's most colorful business figures, died yesterday at his Florida estate. He was 87. His family confirmed he passed peacefully. Turner earned the nickname "Captain Outrageous" while building one of the largest cable TV empires in the country. Morning Brew 1% Better

At age 24, Turner took over his family's Atlanta billboard business and ultimately turned it into a media powerhouse. He bought local radio stations and a struggling Atlanta TV station, and by 1976, jumped on satellite technology to broadcast nationally. In 1980, he introduced CNN and the idea of around-the-clock news.

Beyond CNN, Turner built a cable empire spanning TBS, TNT, Cartoon Network, and Turner Classic Movies, then sold to Time Warner in 1996 for $7.34 billion. He owned an estimated 2 million acres of land across eight states, owned the Atlanta Braves, captained the 1977 America's Cup winner, and donated $1 billion to the United Nations.

Columnist Daily Dad

Could You Go 30 Days Without Yelling at Your Kids?

It's tough to make it through a whole day without getting upset with your kids, without raising your voice. Even people with professional training struggle. Samantha Mann, a licensed behavioral analyst who works with dysregulated children, wrote recently about how she found herself constantly yelling at her 6-year-old son at home, despite being calm with kids at work.

So Mann set a goal: go 30 days without yelling. She made behavior charts—one for her son and one for herself—with rewards for intervals of time without behavioral issues. Each day, it became easier. The chart and her desire to change kept bringing her back to regulation, no matter how her son was behaving. In the end, the challenge helped her "react more thoughtfully and feel more in control" of her emotions and her parenting.

When a group of fathers tried the same challenge, the stories were remarkable. One dad made a practice of checking in with his kids after a long day, always ending with "I love you." Another pulled back on yelling, only to notice his wife falling into a "bad cop" role—which led to a conversation that changed how they parented together. Another dad audited how many times he hugged his daughter in a day, and after realizing it wasn't enough, made an effort to hug her at least three times daily.

Emotional regulation, while more difficult for some than others, is a habit like anything else. The more you practice it, the more you are aware of it, the more you reflect on it, the better you'll get at it.
