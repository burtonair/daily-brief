Iran Strikes Dubai Airport as Gulf Safety Bubble Bursts

An Iranian drone struck Dubai International Airport, causing a fire and halting flights for hours at the world's busiest international hub — shattering the city-state's reputation as a conflict-proof haven for the global wealthy. Morning Brew

Since the war in Iran began, Dubai tried to project business-as-usual — influencers sipping gold-flaked cocktails while drone attack videos circulated online. That facade cracked when a drone struck Dubai International Airport on Monday, sparking a fire. No injuries were reported, but flights were paused for hours at what is, in peacetime, the world's busiest international airport, connecting 291 cities and serving 95 million passengers last year.

Iranian attacks have now killed four people in the UAE as Iran retaliates against the US-Israel campaign throughout the region. Shrapnel damaged several luxury landmarks. Emirates, the UAE's flagship carrier, has already cut US-bound Airbus A380 flights by 51% in March. Passengers posted videos of eerily empty planes and photos of Dubai's usually bustling beaches and markets looking like ghost towns.

The deeper worry is capital flight. Lawyers and asset managers are telling the Wall Street Journal that wealthy clients have begun inquiring about moving money out of Dubai. Urbanist Richard Florida argued in the New York Times that the city is uniquely vulnerable because nearly nine in ten residents lack citizenship — meaning little rootedness in a crisis. Dubai has over 80,000 millionaires who can pack their bags at a moment's notice.

UAE authorities have banned posting photos of damage from Iranian attacks, and the country's president visited a luxury mall in the conflict's early days to project normalcy. Meanwhile, the US has reported 13 troops killed and roughly 200 wounded since the conflict began February 28. At least 1,825 people have died in Iran, including over 1,200 civilians.

Nvidia's GTC 2026: Chips, Agents, Games, and Space Data Centers Rundown AI Morning Brew

Jensen Huang delivered one of the most expansive tech keynotes in recent memory at GTC 2026, positioning Nvidia as "the first vertically integrated but horizontally open company" — spanning chips, AI agents, photorealistic gaming, and robotics.

The headline announcements included NemoClaw, an open-source security and privacy stack for OpenClaw AI agents, and the Vera Rubin platform with seven new chips powering AI training and inference. Huang also unveiled DLSS 5, which uses AI to add photorealistic lighting and materials to games in real time — with Bethesda, Capcom, and Ubisoft as launch partners.

Huang teased space-based data centers, introduced the Groq 3 language-processing unit designed for inference (not just training), and announced new AI platforms for autonomous vehicles and physical robotics.

Why It Matters
  • Nvidia is no longer just a chip company — GTC showed it owning the full stack from silicon to enterprise software to game engines
  • NemoClaw signals Nvidia wants to be the security layer for the entire OpenClaw agent ecosystem
  • $1 trillion in projected chip sales through 2027 suggests Huang sees demand as structural, not a bubble

Pokémon Go Data Is Now Training Delivery Robots Morning Brew

Ten years after players were catching Pokémon in the streets, all those smartphone camera scans of real-world landmarks are being used to help sidewalk robots navigate cities. The internet is not thrilled.

Niantic Spatial — an AI company spun off from Pokémon Go developer Niantic — built a model using 30+ billion images captured by Pokémon Go users that can reportedly geolocate down to the centimeter. Now, a Sam Altman-backed company called Coco Robotics will use this tool to teach its 1,000-bot fleet to better navigate Los Angeles, Chicago, and other cities — especially in urban canyons where GPS signals degrade.

The controversy: A 2020 Pokémon Go feature called "Field Research" incentivized users to scan real-world landmarks for in-game rewards. Many players may not have realized their scans would fuel a commercial robotics dataset. Niantic only announced its plan to build a navigation model from the data in 2024. The long-term goal: a live, centimeter-accurate map of the entire world.

OpenAI Delays ChatGPT "Adult Mode" After "Sexy Suicide Coach" Warning 1% Better

OpenAI shelved its planned erotic chatbot feature after its own advisory council raised alarms, including a warning that ChatGPT could inadvertently become what one adviser called a "sexy suicide coach."

The feature, originally planned for Q1 2026, was pulled after the company discovered its age-verification system was misclassifying minors as adults roughly 12% of the time. The advisory council warned the feature risked fostering emotional overreliance and that the misclassification rate put vulnerable users at risk.

OpenAI confirmed the adult feature is still planned, saying it remains "committed to treating adult users as adults" while building stronger safeguards first. The delay comes as AI companionship products like Character.ai face intensifying regulatory scrutiny over their effects on teenagers.

The Buy-Borrow-Die Wealth Strategy 1%
