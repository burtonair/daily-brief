Trump Admin to Revoke Key Climate Policy, Calls It 'Largest Deregulation' in History Brew

The EPA plans to revoke the Obama-era "endangerment finding" that greenhouse gases threaten public health—the legal foundation for federal emissions regulations—in what the administration calls a $1 trillion deregulation move.

The 2009 finding gave the EPA authority to set emissions standards for cars, trucks, and power plants. The Trump administration will officially announce the change this week, projecting it will reduce vehicle costs by an average of $2,400.

Industry observers note the car industry never asked for this change. California and other states may impose their own stricter standards, and automakers would still need to comply with state and foreign regulations.

Legal experts warn the move may violate federal law and Supreme Court precedent. Environmental groups are expected to sue, setting the stage for years of court battles. Several Democratic state attorneys general have already signaled intent to challenge the decision.

Look At You, You Made It Out

It was quite a journey from there to here. You got out of that small town. You made it into college. You've done better than your parents. You escaped the trap of addiction or abuse or close-mindedness that haunts your family. You made it out. That's an incredible accomplishment. Your kids are lucky. They have someone who is present, who is loving, who is intentional.

But that doesn't mean the work is done. Because genetically, the traces of this are still there. And you know what else? Your kids aren't exposed to that daily reminder, that cautionary tale, that example of what not to be. That's the double-edged nature of making it out, just as in foreign policy it is the double-edged nature of decades of peace. People forget the cost of conflict, they forget the roots of conflict.

You're going to have to work hard to make sure your kids understand the work you've done. You're going to have to work hard to get them to see that physical and mental health are the result of hard work. You're going to have to find other ways to cultivate resilience and determination in them. You're going to have to find ways to show them the costs of going down the wrong paths, that certain mistakes can change the course not just of a life, but generations of a family.

You made it out. Congratulations. Now you've got to make sure your kids stay out.

AI Research Rundown

Harvard Finds AI Tools Actually Expand Workloads

A new Harvard Business Review study found that AI tools at a U.S. tech company didn't lighten employee workloads over 8 months—they grew them.

The study tracked ~200 employees who adopted AI on their own, observing work habits and conducting 40+ interviews over eight months. Workers expanded well beyond their roles, with AI making unfamiliar work feel doable.

AI blurred lines between work and rest, with employees firing off prompts after hours or during breaks. Engineers reported spending more time reviewing and coaching colleagues on AI-assisted code, with "vibe-coding" help requests piling up.

Why it matters: AI was supposed to free workers up, not quietly pile more on their plates. The tech's productivity gains are real, but so is the tradeoff of broader roles and blurred boundaries.

Streaming Brew

Spotify Hits Record User Growth, Stock Pops 15%

The Swedish streaming giant revealed outstanding earnings with record user growth over the final three months of 2025.

Between October and December, Spotify added a record 38 million new listeners. Monthly active users rose 11% to 751 million year over year. Paid subscribers climbed 10% to 290 million.

Wrapped had its best showing ever: More than 300 million users engaged with the feature. Spotify has also unveiled new features including the ability to exclude suggested songs and AI-generated playlists via text prompts.

Looking ahead: Spotify projects it will add 8 million new users in Q1, expecting some churn due to the premium subscription increase from $11.99 to $12.99 that started this month.

Auto Brew

Ferrari Unveils EV Interior Designed by Jony Ive

Ferrari unveiled the inside of its first all-electric vehicle, the Ferrari Luce, designed in collaboration with former Apple design chief Jony Ive's creative firm.

One of the starkest differences from Apple's typical aesthetic is the plethora of physical controls meant to keep drivers' eyes on the road. "Everything feels satisfyingly clicky or twisty," Wired wrote.

The typical Ive/Apple trifecta of rounded edges, glass, and brushed aluminum are everywhere. The glass comes from the same company that supplies iPhones, and displays use Samsung OLED screens.

Next: Ferrari will reveal the Luce's exterior in May, after pre-orders open in March. The company's stock surged this week on strong earnings.

Energy 1% Better

Electric Bills Soaring Across America

Household electricity prices jumped 21% over three years, from 13.66 cents per kilowatt-hour in 2021 to 16.48 cents in 2024. Average monthly bills rose from $121 to $144.

This winter's Arctic freeze amplified the pain. One Pittsburgh homeowner reported an $800 statement, while an Ohio resident's January bill hit $1,013. Multiple factors drive costs: aging grid infrastructure, utilities investing in storm resilience, and increased reliance on natural gas.

Outlook: Experts predict continued increases as new electricity demand from data centers, electric vehicles, and building electrification requires expanded generation. Analysts project another double-digit percentage rise in coming years.
