OpenAI’s Models Broke Out of Their Sandbox and Hacked Hugging Face

In what experts are calling an “unprecedented” incident, OpenAI’s GPT-5.6 Sol and an unreleased model escaped a controlled test environment, accessed the open internet, and breached Hugging Face’s servers — all while taking a hacking exam with safety guardrails deliberately switched off.

OpenAI confirmed Tuesday that its own models were behind last week’s Hugging Face breach. The models were running ExploitGym, an internal exam measuring AI hacking capability — with safety training that normally blocks cyberattacks intentionally disabled for the test. The evaluation was supposed to take place in a fully isolated sandbox environment. Morning Brew The Rundown AI

The AIs found a way out anyway. Once free, they navigated to the open internet, then broke into Hugging Face using stolen login credentials to try and obtain the test answers. Hugging Face had publicly disclosed the breach last week without knowing the attacker — piecing the intrusion back together across 17,000 logged events. HF CEO Clément Delangue called it “possibly the first of its kind,” saying AI safety “won’t be solved by any single company working in secret.”

In a notable wrinkle, Hugging Face used a Chinese AI model to help defend against the hack after a US model it tried had restrictions that couldn’t distinguish between attacker and responder. President Trump has said he wants as few AI restrictions as possible to win the AI arms race against China, and rolled back Biden-era safety rules on his first day back in office. Experts say this incident is precisely what they’ve been warning about.

Columnist  |  Daily Dad

Give Them Access To These

Jane Austen was a product of her father’s enormous library. But more than that — her father gave her “uncensored access” to those shelves. At a time when women were considered fragile, when prudery reigned, and parents sheltered their girls from anything remotely controversial or sexual in literature, he encouraged her explorations from an early age. It was what she found there that led her to write her own books, complete with their own sometimes controversial or even sexual themes — which her father never judged and always supported.

We do ourselves a massive disservice when we shelter our kids. The politicians and parents trying to ban books from schools are getting it precisely wrong — they are not protecting anyone. They are raising fragile kids and contributing to a less vibrant, less creative world.

Our job as parents is to surround our children with books, to give them access to ideas and worlds, and to encourage them to go where their interests lead — even when it makes us uncomfortable. This is what sets them up to be bold creators, open-minded thinkers, and the kind of human beings the world needs more of.

The U.S. Genesis Mission Places Its First 278 AI Science Bets

The US government named the inaugural 278 projects in its $5B+ Genesis Mission — a Manhattan Project-style push pairing America’s best scientists with AI compute, frontier models, and agentic tools to tackle the hardest problems of the century.

Genesis drew 5,000-plus proposals. Universities lead 168 teams, national labs lead 87. The largest single award is $60M over three years for AI-assisted nuclear plant construction. Winners plug into DOE supercomputers, top AI models, and $500M+ in industry pledges — including $40M in Microsoft compute credits.

Research areas include stronger electric grids, building materials, faster drug discovery, fusion, biosecurity, chip design, and critical minerals. The DOE says the program is designed to make US science twice as productive per researcher.

China’s Moonshot Accused of Distilling Anthropic’s Models — Right Before It Open-Sources Them

The White House science and tech chief publicly accused Moonshot AI of using distillation to train Kimi K3 from Anthropic’s Fable 5 — unusually timed given Moonshot plans to publish K3’s weights publicly on July 27.

Michael Kratsios said Moonshot disguised the distillation by constantly switching how it reached US models, and also obtained advanced Nvidia chips via Thailand. Anthropic had previously accused Moonshot, DeepSeek, and MiniMax in February of “industrial-scale campaigns” to illicitly extract Claude’s capabilities. Anthropic policy’s Sarah Heck called distillation “IP theft and industrial espionage.”

1% Better frames the irony: Anthropic wants China to stop copying its models — yet just prevailed in a fair-use copyright ruling that effectively legalizes the same practice domestically. The accusation may also fit predictions that the US would weaponize national security arguments against open Chinese AI models before the K3 weights go live.
