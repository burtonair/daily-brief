USPS Changes Could Make Last-Minute Tax Filing Too Late Brew

Due to cutbacks at the US Postal Service, mailing your tax return on April 15 may not guarantee a same-day postmark, potentially making you late for the deadline.

Unlike soup, tax returns are not always better a day later. The USPS is cutting back on how often it picks up mail from local post offices to save money, making same-day postmarking less common.

The agency still stamps the day's date on your letter when it reaches a processing facility, but your mail might take longer to actually make it there. This affects all time-sensitive mail, including bills and ballots.

Alternative options: Ask someone at the post office to manually postmark your tax return, deliver documents via UPS or FedEx, or file taxes electronically.

Finance Brew

The Great Millionaire Tax Debate

Several states are considering hiking taxes on their wealthiest residents, but questions remain about whether the rich will actually leave.

New York Mayor Zohran Mamdani is lobbying state leaders to raise taxes on the richest New Yorkers to fund campaign proposals like free childcare and bus service. Several state legislatures, including Virginia's, Washington's, and Rhode Island's, are mulling their own millionaire income tax hikes.

Californians might soon vote on a ballot measure that would impose a one-time 5% tax on the wealth of residents worth $1 billion or more. Google co-founder Larry Page already left California in response to the state's proposed billionaire tax, while venture capitalist David Sacks personally left for Austin, TX.

Massachusetts case study: The state's millionaire tax, which adds a 4% surcharge on income over $1 million, has brought in almost $6 billion over two years. Supporters claim wealthy residents haven't rushed to leave, citing the rising number of millionaires in the state, perhaps because the state's high quality of life outweighs the tax burden.

But the Boston-based libertarian think tank Pioneer Institute insists that local wealth growth is largely due to ballooning asset values and notes that counting Mass-based millionaires doesn't reflect the number of affluent residents moving to lower-tax destinations like Florida and New Hampshire.

Big picture: Evidence from across the US is mixed. Several studies show that millionaires don't move en masse in response to tax hikes, and when they do, it's not as significant as headlines might imply, though some research pushes back, saying that a not-insignificant number of high earners do leave over time.

Sports & Finance Brew

Pro Athletes Follow the Tax-Free Money

Professional athletes have a powerful incentive to play in states without income taxes, where huge contracts are worth millions more.

Hockey: Mikko Rantanen spent the first 10 years with the Colorado Avalanche (4.4% state tax), then was traded to Carolina (4.25% tax). Three months later, he was traded to the Dallas Stars in tax-free Texas, where he signed an eight-year, $96 million extension—saving roughly $4 million compared to his previous teams.

Basketball: NBA player Grant Williams left Boston for Dallas in 2023 on a $54 million contract. Massachusetts has a 5% state tax plus a 4% surtax on income above $1 million. "In Boston, it's really like $48 million with the millionaire's tax, so $54 million in Dallas is really like $58 million in Boston," Williams said.

Football: NFL player Tyreek Hill chose the Miami Dolphins over the New York Jets in free agency. When asked why, Hill said, "Those state taxes, man." By living in Florida instead of New Jersey, he saved $9 million in income taxes over his three-year, $90 million contract.

Individual sports: Pro golfers and tennis players can call anywhere home. Many choose Florida, Texas, and Nevada due to friendly tax laws and warm weather.

College competition: Arkansas became the first state to make the name, image, and likeness (NIL) money that athletes receive tax exempt. Five other states—Georgia, Illinois, Louisiana, New Jersey, and South Carolina—are considering similar measures.
