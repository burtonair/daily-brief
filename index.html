Do You Support Them in This?

You might think that every little boy would want to be king, but Marcus Aurelius didn't. He supposedly wept when he found out he would be emperor—because he knew how many bad kings there had been in history, and more sweetly, since he was just a boy, he did not want to move out of his mother's house.

Although Marcus Aurelius had lost his father at age 3, it's clear that he came from good stock and that his parents and grandparents had done a good job raising him. He was wary of power and did not lust for it. He wanted a quieter life. He did not prize fame or attention or wealth.

Why is it that so many of us try to impress ambition on our kids? Why are we trying to push them to become famous or powerful? Have we not seen the people who tend to get to these positions? Have we not seen how it works out for them?

There is nothing wrong with humility. There is nothing wrong with having a quiet life and a modest, fulfilling job. The goal should be happiness, whatever that might look like for our kids. Let's not pressure them into a different direction for our own reasons, but support them on the path they choose for themselves.

Markets Brew

Wall Street Embraces the "Anti-AI Trade" with HALO Stocks

As AI hype slows down, HALO ("heavy assets, low obsolescence") businesses—companies relatively immune to automation like McDonald's or John Deere—are becoming the new safety stocks.

Josh Brown of Ritholz Wealth Management coined HALO to describe any company that can't be disrupted by typing something into a prompt. Delta Air Lines has jumped 13% while Expedia has stayed flat. Goldman Sachs introduced an everything-but-AI index for investors who want to buy everything except AI in one package.

AI Rundown

New AI Model Learns Any Computer Task by Watching Videos

Standard Intelligence introduced FDM-1, a "computer action" model trained on 11M hours of screen footage that can do CAD modeling, find software bugs, and even drive a real car through San Francisco.

FDM-1 is trained on 550,000x the largest open dataset, with AI that reverse-engineers what actions produced each frame. The model can process nearly two hours of continuous screen activity at once—50x the visual context of existing models. Demos range from building gears in Blender to driving a real car via arrow keys with under an hour of training data.

Dark Sky Creators Launch Acme Weather App

The team behind Dark Sky is back with Acme Weather, an app that wants to improve forecast accuracy by embracing their inherent uncertainty.

The app shows multiple lines denoting primary and alternate predictions—the closer the lines, the higher the chance of accuracy. Community-reported conditions (think Waze, but for humidity and rainbows) appear on your map. It's $25 per year for iOS, with Android coming soon.
