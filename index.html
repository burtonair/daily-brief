US Employers Unexpectedly Shed 92,000 Jobs in February

A jarring reversal from January's gains sends the unemployment rate to 4.4%, rattling markets already strained by the Iran war and surging oil prices. Brew

Economy watchers may feel like someone waking up with a low-grade fever after hoping rest would cure their emergent sniffles. US employers unexpectedly shed 92,000 jobs in February, according to data released Friday, quashing expectations of a stabilizing job market. That's a sharp reversal from January when the economy added 126,000 jobs, and it caused the unemployment rate to creep up to 4.4%.

Part of February's employment dip was due to strikes reducing healthcare employment, as well as arctic weather stalling hospitality and construction work. But payrolls also shrank in government, manufacturing, and information technology. Revised data also revealed the US shed jobs in December, correcting that month from a gain of 48,000 to a loss of 17,000.

The weak jobs report reflects a reality before the war in Iran disrupted industries. Since then, gas prices have jumped by more than 30 cents a gallon this week after Iran began hindering shipments of Middle Eastern oil to global markets, and experts warn the disruptions could make other products pricier too.

Accelerating price growth and weak hiring is a double whammy for the Fed. Investors still expect the Fed to keep interest rates steady during their upcoming meeting this month, with some central bank officials saying it would take more economic alarm bells to prompt rate cuts.
