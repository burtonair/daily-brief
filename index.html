World Cup Eve and Everyone's Mad at FIFA

Nearly 180,000 tickets for the 2026 World Cup remain unsold — the result of FIFA's first-ever dynamic pricing experiment, which made these the most expensive matches in tournament history and sparked legal investigations across multiple US states.

Fans may not have been this universally angry at FIFA since Diego Maradona scored with his hand. From October 2025 through April, FIFA hiked prices for the vast majority of games by an average of 35%. At the extreme end, best tickets to the final tripled to ~$33,000 in May. Even the cheapest seats are more than three times as expensive as Qatar 2022's cheapest tickets.

Around 4,400 of those unsold tickets are for the US team's opening match against Paraguay this Friday in Los Angeles. FIFA president Gianni Infantino has defended the prices as reflecting North American demand — but median resale prices have fallen 20% since last month, and most resellers are set to lose money.

Financially, FIFA may still win: it's projected to generate $11 billion in overall revenue from 2026, with $3 billion from ticket sales alone — versus just ~$930 million from the 2022 Qatar World Cup. But several states have launched investigations into allegations of artificial ticket scarcity and price deception. "Had the tickets been priced more reasonably all along, they would all be sold out," wrote soccer reporter Henry Bushnell for The Athletic.

Life Biosciences has started the first FDA-approved human clinical trial to reverse cellular aging — arguably the biggest tangible step yet for the longevity industry, which has historically overpromised and underdelivered.

Scientists injected their ER-100 drug into the eye of a glaucoma patient, attempting to rejuvenate optic nerve cells and restore sight using cellular reprogramming — a technique the company hopes to apply to other organs like the liver and, eventually, the brain.

Temper expectations: the trial is in early stages, and cellular reprogramming caused cancerous cells in some mouse research. Architecture / Culture Morning Brew

Sagrada Família Is Almost Done After 144 Years

Pope Leo XIV visited Barcelona today to inaugurate the 566-foot Tower of Jesus Christ, completing the world's tallest church — exactly 100 years after architect Antoni Gaudí's death.

Gaudí took over the project shortly after construction began in 1882, envisioning ornate towers with natural motifs as a stone embodiment of the Bible. Construction was delayed for decades by the Spanish Civil War and funding shortfalls. It now stands as Spain's most recognizable landmark, powering the country's tourism boom.

Not everyone's celebrating: some locals say the neighborhood has become too touristy, and residents oppose a facade plan that would require demolishing their homes. The broader anti-tourism backlash blames Barcelona's hospitality industry for rising housing costs and eroding the city's character.

Can't make it to Barcelona? Lego is releasing a 12,060-piece version of the basilica — hopefully not a 144-year build.

AI / Research The Rundown AI

Perplexity + Harvard Map How AI Agents Shift Knowledge Work

A study comparing 10,000 identical queries sent to Perplexity Search vs. its Computer agent found that agents don't just make workers faster — they change what workers ask for.

Search is initially quicker (33 seconds vs. Computer's 26-minute average run), but leaves "doing" to the user — the equivalent full workflow takes 269 minutes without the agent, vs. 36 with it. Half of what users asked the agent to do involved creating something new, double the Search rate. Work requested outside a user's own field climbed 9 points to 59%.

The more interesting finding: agentic users were more likely to request cognitively complex, creative outputs and pull across multiple fields — suggesting the subtle draw of AI agents may be users acting with more ambition, not just more efficiency.
