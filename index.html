Stocks just had their best quarter since 2020 — and chips led the way Brew

The S&P 500 closed Q2 up 14.9% — its biggest quarterly jump since the post-Covid rally of 2020. AI hardware stocks drove the surge: the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index posted its best quarter in history at a nearly 92% return.

The market has had the vibe of a Ford Ranger with six Roman candles duct-taped to the back: shooting up farther than anyone expected, on an unstable base. The S&P is up 9.55% since January despite geopolitical turbulence including US and Israeli attacks on Iran. Investors bet tensions were easing and the Strait of Hormuz would remain open — markets rewarded that bet. The Dow ended H1 up 8.85%, its best six-month stretch since 2021. The Nasdaq added 12.8%.

Chip and memory stocks like Sandisk, Micron, and Intel are the biggest drivers. The Russell 2000 surged over 20% since January — its best performance since 1991 — though 16 of the 50 top performers are chip-adjacent names pulling the index higher. Small caps are riding AI coattails.

The rally has cracks. OpenAI is reportedly weighing pushing its IPO to 2027. Nvidia’s semi-stagnant growth signals chip-maker returns may have peaked. Gold posted its worst quarter since 2013, hurt by a strong dollar. Bitcoin had a dismal half. Fed rate increases expected later this year could cool the run. Today also marks Bobby Bonilla Day — the annual reminder that the retired Mets third baseman still collects $1.19M from New York every July 1 through 2035, the greatest personal finance flex in sports history.

Give Them What You Didn’t Get

Few of us felt completely understood as kids. Our parents didn’t quite get what we wanted, how scared we were when alone, how overwhelming the world was as teenagers. And now as adults, we struggle too — maybe our parents still can’t really hear us when we try to talk about wounds from the past. It’s painful. It’s lonely. It’s rough.

Epictetus said there are things that are up to us and things that are not. Being understood is never going to be fully up to us — it depends on other people, our busy, overwhelmed, imperfect parents. That ship has sailed. We can’t go back in time and get the understanding we missed.

But if being understood is not in our control, there is something quite wonderful that is: understanding. We can give what we didn’t have. Most importantly, we can do this for our children right now. We can try to really see them, really listen. Our own pain teaches us the value of being understood — how much it means, how even a little of it can go a long way.

Instead of turning inward because we haven’t gotten all we needed, why not channel that energy toward being a positive difference-maker for our kids? We can’t always be understood, but we can always strive to understand.

Labor & AI Brew

New study: AI-forward companies are hiring more, not less

A study by Ramp and Revelio Labs found that companies that heavily adopted AI expanded headcount by 10.2% in the 24 months after adoption — while companies with modest AI use saw no noticeable employment change at all.

Entry-level hiring grew at the same rate as senior roles at AI-forward firms. The most aggressive adopters had a higher share of junior employees than non-AI firms — the opposite of the automation-kills-entry-level-jobs fear.

The authors caution it’s too early to generalize long-term effects. But some economists now believe AI could create jobs by making companies more efficient, boosting consumption of their products and services. Enroll in clowning school only if you truly love it.
