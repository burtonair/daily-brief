Cover Story

The Beauty Industry's Sweeping Makeover

From men's skincare to elementary school makeup hauls, K-beauty's US surge, and AI deepfakes peddling dubious creams — the $500B+ global beauty industry is undergoing a radical transformation that's reshaping who buys, who sells, and who you can trust. Morning Brew

Close your eyes and picture someone at the store buying makeup. If you're not picturing a five-year-old kid or a grown man, you're living in the past. The beauty industry has gotten a makeover in recent years, driven by changing social attitudes and ambitious brands eager to explore untapped markets. Read the full Morning Brew feature here.

Men are increasingly embracing cosmetics: in 2019, more than 90% of US males said they never wore makeup. By 2024, that figure had dropped to 75%. Meanwhile, the share of Gen Z men using facial skincare products jumped 68% from 2022 to 2024, per market intelligence firm Mintel.

Children are a newly courted demographic too. Klee Naturals has built a multimillion-dollar business selling mineral eye shadows and lip glosses to girls ages 5 to 7, while Evereden reported $100 million in global sales in 2024 with products aimed at children ages 3 and up. K-beauty — Korean cosmetics — is also surging in the US, with NielsenIQ data showing US sales more than doubled from 2023 to 2025.

And then there's the murkier side: AI-generated deepfakes flooding social media to hawk beauty products under stolen influencer identities, threatening the $32 billion human influencer industry and raising real questions about health and safety.

Beauty & Tech

AI Deepfakes Are Eroding Trust in the Beauty Influencer Industry Morning Brew

Social media is saturated with fraudulent AI-generated personas hawking beauty products through affiliate links — threatening the $32 billion global human influencer industry and raising serious health concerns.

Scammers are exploiting the cachet of real influencers and prominent beauty professionals by deploying AI versions of their likenesses to promote products they never endorsed. Beverly Hills plastic surgeon Dr. Andrew Cohen had his AI avatar used to recommend supplements — a development he says undermines professional trust and endangers patients' health.

Some fraudulent accounts invent entirely new AI personas. The TikTok account "Holistic Health Finds," for example, features an AI deepfake claiming to be the wife of South Korea's highest-paid plastic surgeon and a Victoria's Secret model — all to push batana oil hair growth products.

Not all AI influencers are scams: Aitana Lopez, developed by talent agency The Clueless, has nearly 400,000 Instagram followers and legitimate brand collaborations with Olaplex. But the line between authentic and fabricated is becoming harder to draw. A recent viral trend had users flooding feeds with AI-generated images mimicking Hailey Bieber's Rhode brand aesthetic, confusing thousands of fans.

Some plastic surgeons report patients are now requesting surgeries to resemble AI-altered faces — a troubling new chapter in beauty's long history of impossible standards.

Celebrity Business

How Celebrity Beauty Brands Became Billion-Dollar Empires Morning Brew

For Hollywood's elite, launching a beauty brand is no longer a vanity project — it's a legitimate path to becoming a billionaire business mogul, and the numbers are staggering.

Elf Beauty acquired Hailey Bieber's Rhode for $1 billion last year — remarkable for a brand founded just four years prior. Selena Gomez's pandemic-born Rare Beauty now carries a $2.7 billion valuation, with its signature blush commanding over 26% of all blush category sales at Sephora, per YipItData.

The trailblazer remains Rihanna, who revolutionized the industry in 2017 by launching Fenty Beauty with 40 foundation shades — double the industry standard at the time. The brand generated $100 million in its first two months. Her 50% stake is now worth approximately $700 million, cementing her status as a businesswoman who eclipsed her music career financially.

Kylie Jenner sold a majority stake in Kylie Cosmetics to Coty in 2019 for $600 million, though she reportedly explored buying back the brand in 2023 — signaling just how valuable these ventures have become.

Men's Health & Travel

Turkey's Booming Hair Transplant Industry Has Become a Global Destination Morning Brew

Steep US prices and slick all-inclusive packages have turned Turkey into the world's foremost destination for hair restoration surgery, drawing nearly 1 million visitors in 2022 alone.

A single hair graft in the US can cost between $18,000 and $24,000. In Turkey, the same procedure typically runs $3,000 to $9,000, often bundled with luxury hotel accommodations, airport transfers, translators, and even sightseeing tours. The Turkish Health Tourism Association reported that approximately 1 million people traveled to Turkey for hair transplants in 2022, spending around $2 billion in total.

Social media has supercharged demand: TikTokers showing off dramatic before-and-after results have inspired legions of followers to book their own transplant trips abroad.

But experts urge caution. The International Society of Hair Restoration Surgery warns against so-called black-market clinics that transplant too aggressively in a single session. Aftercare is also a challenge — many US doctors are reluctant to treat patients whose procedures were performed overseas.

Cosmetic Procedures

C-Suite Executives Are Getting Fat Injections From Cadavers Morning Brew

The latest cosmetic trend among wealthy business leaders involves filling out body curves using purified fat from donors — a procedure that takes under two hours and skips the general anesthesia of traditional augmentation.

Half a dozen plastic surgeons told Business Insider they administered these cadaveric fat injections roughly 75 times last year, frequently to C-suite executives who often return directly to the office after the procedure. Unlike traditional augmentation surgeries — which require general anesthesia, lengthy recovery, and either artificial fillers or liposuction to harvest the patient's own fat — this newer technique avoids all of that.

GLP-1 medications like Ozempic are reportedly fueling demand. These drugs tend to deflate users' hips, buttocks, and chest — leaving patients with less of their own fat to harvest for conventional cosmetic procedures. Cadaveric fat fills that gap, quite literally.

The procedures can cost tens of thousands of dollars per session, firmly placing them in the realm of luxury medicine — but demand from the executive class is growing steadily.
