Travel & Economy Morning Brew

The K-Shaped Economy Has Arrived at the Airport

Airlines, hotels, and destinations are all following the same script: double down on the wealthy and let everyone else fight over the scraps. The travel industry's split personality is now impossible to ignore.

At Delta, premium ticket sales surpassed economy for the first time ever last year — business and first-class rose 9% while main cabin seats dropped 7%. The airline has been deliberately directing investment toward premium products, and the math is working. United and American are following the same playbook.

The divergence is sharper in hotels. From November 2024 to November 2025, luxury properties posted a 2.9% gain in revenue per available room while midscale dropped 2.6% and economy properties fell 4.1%. Meanwhile, Spirit Airlines abruptly shut down earlier this month, specifically citing fuel costs driven in part by the Iran war.

That said, two-thirds of Americans still plan to take a domestic trip this summer. They're just doing it differently — hunting "destination dupes" (Brussels instead of Paris, Naples instead of Rome), leaning on loyalty points, and spending lunch breaks refreshing deal sites.

What Happened in Vegas Is Staying in the Statistics

Annual trips to Las Vegas dropped 7.5% last year — outside Covid, the worst decline the Las Vegas Convention and Visitors Authority has recorded since it started tracking visits in the 1970s. But the casinos don't care: they made more money anyway.

The theories for the decline range from geopolitical headwinds (Mayor Shelley Berkley publicly begged Canadians to return) to GLP-1 drugs reducing gambling and drinking urges, to Gen Z's declining alcohol consumption. But the strongest case is simple economics: casinos tweaked odds against gamblers in roulette, raised blackjack minimums, and piled on resort fees, inflated food prices, and eliminated free parking — all to chase higher-income visitors.

It's working financially: despite fewer visitors, casinos posted record revenue of roughly $8.8 billion. An LVCVA survey found 44% of visitors now have household incomes of $150,000 or more. For everyone else, Atlantic City awaits.

Six Overlooked Destinations Worth the Trip This Summer

Not one to follow the herd? Morning Brew's travel edition rounds up genuinely nonobvious picks — from a Revolutionary War battlefield in upstate New York to a 800-year-old camphor tree in Nagasaki.

Fort William Henry, Lake George, NY — America's semiquincentennial is the time to visit. The site of a decisive Revolutionary War battle is now a history museum surrounded by natural beauty; hike Prospect Mountain for views of Lake George.

La Grange, Kentucky — The quaint town 30 minutes outside Louisville has a CSX freight train running along its main street. Best spotted from the Train Observation Tower, alongside a 1929 dining car and a caboose at the railroad museum.

Burns, Oregon — Drive the High Desert Discovery Scenic Byway through eastern Oregon for desert vistas, spectacular birdwatching at Malheur National Wildlife Refuge, and a soak at Crane Hot Springs.

Kyrgyzstan — Nomadic practices, mountainous terrain on horseback, yurt sleeping, and kok boru — a polo-like sport played with a goat carcass. Morning Brew's Macy Gilliam traversed the country and reported back.

Tallinn, Estonia — Cobbled streets, a medieval old town, and UNESCO-recognized sauna culture. Visit in June for the Nordic-style summer solstice festival at the Estonian Open Air Museum.

Nagasaki, Japan — Beyond the Peace Park, there's an 800-year-old camphor tree and a dessert shop that's been operating since 1624.

The $1.4 Billion Suitcase Wars: Away, Béis, Rimowa, and Everyone Else

DJ John Summit's viral post — kicking, hammering, and setting a Rimowa on fire after repeated breakdowns — unleashed a luggage brand pile-on that reveals just how crowded the premium suitcase market has become.

Away, which launched in 2015 as a prettier-than-Samsonite but cheaper-than-Rimowa option, hit a $1.4 billion valuation in four years and opened the D2C suitcase floodgates. Shay Mitchell's Béis launched in 2018 and has since collaborated with Rare Beauty. Casetify — yes, the phone case company — launched its first suitcase in 2024.

Legacy brands Samsonite (25% market share) and LVMH-owned Rimowa still dominate the carousel, but the upstarts are chipping. "We're all fighting to steal market share from Samsonite," the co-founder of Australian brand July told Fast Company. The next trendy suitcase, they say, better come with a built-in television.
