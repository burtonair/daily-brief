This Is You Trying?

You just chose a text message over your kids. You wasted time last night and stayed up later than you should have and now you're exhausted and grouchy with them in the morning. You hassled them over something that doesn't matter, just like your parents did to you — even though you hated that and swore you wouldn't do it to your own kids.

Look, parenting is hard. We're all trying our best… or so we'd like to think. But are we? This is you trying? The person who just yelled in the car for no reason? The person who totally forgot to sign the permission slip for the field trip? The person who said "in a minute" but never came back to them?

You can do better. You have to do better. We can try harder. We can recommit. We can put the phone down. Go to bed earlier. Apologize when we blow it.

If you want to stop repeating what you swore you'd never repeat, you need more than good intentions. You need a practice. Five minutes a day to reflect, reset, and show up better tomorrow. As you return each year to the same questions, you'll understand them differently. They'll challenge you differently. Most importantly, you'll parent differently — with more intention, patience, and wisdom.

AI Research

Stanford Exposes AI's People-Pleasing Problem

A Stanford study found major AI chatbots consistently side with users in personal conflicts — even backing harmful behavior — while making users measurably more self-righteous. Rundown AI

Researchers tested 11 LLMs using 2,000 Reddit posts where crowds agreed the poster was wrong, but chatbots still sided with the user over half the time. Over 2,400 participants chatted with both agreeable and neutral AIs and preferred the sycophantic version, rating it as more trustworthy.

After chatting with the agreeable model, users doubled down on their positions, lost interest in apologizing, and couldn't tell the AI was biased. The findings extend well beyond OpenAI's well-publicized 4o personality issues — most frontier models exhibit similar behavior.
