The Cheaper AI Got, The More It Cost

Companies that adopted AI fastest are now the ones rationing it. Jevons Paradox — a 19th-century coal observation — explains why cheaper tokens are producing bigger bills.

A new status game has taken hold in Silicon Valley. Engineers at the largest technology companies have begun competing to consume as much AI as they possibly can — a practice the industry has christened "tokenmaxxing." At Meta, an internal leaderboard called Claudeonomics ranked its roughly 85,000 employees by tokens burned; one 30-day window logged more than 60 trillion before the company quietly took it down.

But the theater is the trivial version of the problem. Consider Uber, which rolled out Claude Code to its engineers in December. By March, about 84% had adopted it; roughly 70% of code committed today originates with AI, and about 11% of live backend updates ship with no human in the loop. Individual engineers were running between $500 and $2,000 a month in tokens. None of that is gaming — it is the tool working. And in April, Uber's CTO told The Information that the company had exhausted its entire 2026 AI coding budget in just four months. "I'm back to the drawing board," he said.

Microsoft reached the same wall from the opposite direction — in mid-May it began cancelling most internal Claude Code licenses inside its Experiences and Devices division, pointing engineers toward GitHub Copilot, a cheaper coding tool it already owns. (That Microsoft holds a stake in Copilot's maker, OpenAI, and was trimming a competitor while steering toward its own, need not be the only reason for the decision to be one of them.)

The paradox belongs to William Stanley Jevons, who observed in 1865 that England's coal consumption climbed as steam engines grew more efficient. Substitute tokens for coal and the rhyme is exact. Gartner reckons inference costs will fall ~90% between 2025 and 2030 — but Goldman Sachs projects agentic AI could push consumption up 24-fold, to 120 quadrillion tokens a month. A 90% price cut against a 24x volume surge doesn't net to savings. It nets to a bill more than twice the size of today's. AI was sold as the conversion of expensive headcount into cheap tokens. Under a meter, the more capable and widely adopted the tool, the larger the line item it creates — until the savings it was supposed to deliver become the thing finance reaches for first.

Find The Things Where They're Happy To Be With You

They're embarrassed of you. They don't want to be seen going in with you. They ignore you. They'd rather look at their phone than have a conversation with you. This is what kids do. This is what teenagers do.

The comedian Paul Scheer recently made a heartwarming little documentary interviewing parents outside a Taylor Swift concert. One mom put it perfectly: "I think everybody knows most 16-year-old girls don't want to hang out with their parents, but our daughter is very happy to be with us today. Tomorrow, she'll pretend she doesn't know us again, but tonight, she's our best friend."

Look for those things that would make them happy to be with you — and use them as an excuse to spend time together. Maybe your kid likes Taylor Swift. Maybe they like metal. Maybe ballet or anime or monster trucks. Whatever it is, get into it to be with them.

You'll also be showing them that you care. That you're interested in what they enjoy. You'll be learning how they like to spend their time, making plans together — and in the process, getting closer.

Startup Cleans Your NYC Apartment for Free — In Exchange for AI Training Data

German startup MicroAGI's Shift app opened a free home-cleaning service where cleaners wear head-mounted cameras. The footage trains autonomous robot housekeepers.

A vetted cleaner arrives wearing what co-founder Bercan Kilic calls a "magic hat," filming the roughly two-hour job point-of-view style. Despite covering the cost, the human footage is worth more to robot makers for training — letting Shift cover the bill and still profit. The platform has already paid $5M+ in Q1 to people in 15 countries filming everyday chores at $20/hour.

GM Harry Kilberg said the NYC launch drew "thousands and thousands of bookings," with London, Munich, and Zurich next. The company promises to blur sensitive details. If you're worried your home is too messy: don't — Shift calls messy homes "challenging cleaning environments" and says they're especially useful.

Why it matters: As we've seen with DoorDash paying couriers to capture task data, the next AI dataset is coming from ordinary human work instead of the internet. Shift pushes that model deeper into the home — where people are simultaneously the customers, the workforce, and the training material for their own eventual automation.

Personal Finance Morning Brew

Home Insurance Is Denying Claims at Record Rates — Hurricane Season Starts Today

The top 5 insurers didn't pay on more than 44% of claims last year, up from 36% in 2015. Predictive AI risk models, soaring reinsurance costs, and extreme weather are all to blame.

According to a Wall Street Journal analysis, Allstate, Farmers, Liberty Mutual, State Farm, and USAA together denied more than 44% of resolved claims last year. Insurers are increasingly using predictive models and drone footage to calculate "risk scores" rather than historical claims data — driving up costs even for homes with clean records. Reinsurance costs have also climbed, and weather events causing $1B+ in damage increased 5x from 2018–2022.

USAA disputes the figures, arguing the analysis didn't account for losses below deductibles or claims not pursued by customers. The Atlantic hurricane season officially begins today. In a Pew survey, 42% of homeowners say premiums have increased "a lot" in recent years — and since homeowners insurance is required for a mortgage, the ripple effects touch consumer spending and the housing market broadly.

Ex-DeepMind Group Raises $50M to Build Self-Improving Science AI

Inherent Labs came out of stealth applying recursive self-improvement not just to models — but to the entire research organization, including which problems are worth solving at all.

Co-founders Tantum Collins, Edward Hughes, and Louis Kirsch came from DeepMind; Kaloyan Aleksiev previously worked at Reka AI and Microsoft. Their Faraday platform pairs researchers with self-improving AI agents designed to spot higher-value scientific questions — not just answer prompts.

Inherent is exploring what "AI taste" looks like in science: letting AI make decisions about which problems are worth pursuing, including resource allocation across the research org. The lab says it will test recursive self-improvement across everything from agent training to grant decisions. Self-improving AI is a quest many top labs are pursuing; Inherent applies the recursive logic to the entire institution, not just the model.
