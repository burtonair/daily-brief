Trump Turns the Strait of Hormuz Into a Toll Road

President Trump announced the US will charge shipowners a 20% fee on cargo value for naval escorts through the Strait of Hormuz — and reimpose a full blockade on Iranian oil — as US and Iranian forces exchanged strikes over the weekend, pushing crude above $80 a barrel for the first time since last month’s ceasefire.

Trump posted on Truth Social that the US will be the waterway’s “guardian,” using its navy to keep it open while charging shipowners 20% of cargo value for the protection. Bloomberg estimated that amounts to roughly $30 million for a supertanker filled with oil — several times the ~$2 million tolls Iran had been charging. Analysts told CNN that normal escort fees run 2–3% of cargo value. The UN’s International Maritime Organization said it opposes any shipping tolls in the strait under international law.

The announcement followed a weekend of escalating strikes. The US struck additional targets in Iran overnight Monday and reimposed its blockade on Iranian ships effective Monday at 4pm ET. Iran retaliated by hitting two UAE tankers. Before the war, one-fifth of the world’s oil passed through the strait. Oil broke $80/barrel Monday for the first time since the ceasefire in June.

Meanwhile, three Saronic Corsair autonomous boats — 24-foot unmanned surface vessels built by a four-year-old Austin startup — sank a docked Iranian Ghadir-class midget submarine at Bandar Abbas naval base on Sunday. US Central Command called it the first combat use of American sea drones. The Corsairs cost a fraction of conventional naval assets, and the asymmetric logic is striking: cheap, autonomous, and able to reach targets conventional assets cannot.

The legal framing of the US fee remains murky. Some experts say charging for a specific, voluntary service could be legal. But insurers may refuse coverage for vessels in the strait entirely — regardless of who’s offering protection — making the escort fee academic for many operators.

Columnist Daily Dad

You Will Remember This

You remember the long hours you worked. You remember the sacrifices. You know the risks you took and the effort it required. You wouldn’t have been successful without it. We all have a story we tell ourselves about how we got here. And as inspiring as these narratives are, do they really illustrate the values we hold dearest? Do they celebrate the right sacrifices?

Mark Twain adored his daughter Susy. And yet, as he looked back after her sudden and devastating death, he was horrified by his own priorities. “If I could call up a single instance where I laid aside my own projects & desires & put myself to real inconvenience to procure a pleasure for her,” he wrote, “I would forget all things else to remember that.”

It’s not that he’d been a bad father — he’d just been busy. He hadn’t neglected her; he’d just put a lot of other things first, as we all sometimes do. He wished he had sacrificed more of his career for her, instead of sacrificing so much of her for his career. He wished he’d chosen her more.

Look, we all struggle with balancing family life and professional life — the way we want to spend our time and the reality of how we actually do, who or what we’re sacrificing our limited hours for. One day, we may wish the same as Twain. The work will still be there.

The Kill Heard ‘Round the Defense-Tech World

Three Saronic Corsair autonomous boats — 24-foot craft built by a four-year-old Austin startup — sank an Iranian midget submarine at dock on Sunday. CENTCOM called it the first combat use of American sea drones. The strategic implications go deeper than the footage.

The clip US Central Command posted runs about twenty seconds: the low approach, the submarine hoisted on a yellow gantry at Bandar Abbas naval base, then the feed washing white. The Corsair was built to solve the problem of American shipyards being slow and expensive — and three of them just sank a submarine that would have cost hundreds of millions of dollars to replace.

The historical frame offered by 1% Better’s Vector newsletter is pointed: at the Congress of Paris in 1856, great powers abolished privateering on the reasoning that a sea without privateers belonged to whoever owned the largest fleet. The US refused to sign, keeping the equalizer. The Corsair is that argument, resurfaced 170 years later — cheap, autonomous, and able to reach targets conventional assets cannot.

Saronic, founded 2021 in Austin, had been largely operating below the public radar. Sunday changed that.

Claude’s Personality Shifts by Language — Anthropic Doesn’t Know Why

Anthropic published research analyzing 309K user conversations across three models and 20 languages, finding that both the Claude version and the user’s language significantly change the AI’s personality on four measurable axes.

Sonnet 4.6 runs warmer and briefer. Opus 4.6 gets straight to the point. Opus 4.7 is candid and cautious. Switch to Dutch and Claude admits more errors; use Hindi and it adds warmth; write in English and it digs deeper into details. Anthropic says it doesn’t “yet understand why they vary, or whether that’s desired” — with uneven training data between languages as one suspect.

The implication: if this is true of Claude, it’s likely true of every major AI model, leaving personality blind spots the size of entire user bases shaped by native language.
