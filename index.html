Real Estate

America’s homes haven’t been this old since before Blockbuster existed Brew

The median US home is now a record 44 years old — and with new construction still lagging demand, aging housing stock is forcing millions of owners into expensive, unglamorous repairs they didn’t choose.

According to the Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies, the typical American house was built before the first Blockbuster video store opened. In cities like Buffalo, New York, the median home dates to 1955 — 71 years old. The consequences are compounding: nearly 50% of all home improvement spending now goes to necessary maintenance, like replacing furnaces and roofs, rather than discretionary upgrades.

The price of getting older isn’t cheap. Homeowners spent an average of $9,030 on replacement projects in 2023 — up 59% from 2009, inflation-adjusted. Between 2022 and 2024, the cost of structural repairs jumped another 14%, driven partly by tariff-related material price increases.

If you’re forced into a project, Zillow data shows the best return on investment goes to garage door replacements (349% ROI), entry door swaps (216%), and manufactured stone veneers (208%). Swimming pools and marble countertops, by contrast, may actually drive buyers away — though if you want a koi pond, no judgment.

Real Estate Brew

The calculus behind house flipping has fundamentally changed

The HGTV fantasy of buy-fix-sell profits is running into the hardest market in 17 years — though the first green shoots of recovery appeared in early 2026.

Attom data shows 297,045 single-family homes and condos were flipped in 2025 — the fewest since 2020, down 3.9% from 2024. The 25.5% gross return sounds decent until you factor in carrying costs, renovation inflation, and financing at elevated rates. The average profit was closer to 35% as recently as early 2024.

The tariff effect is real: rising material costs, some directly attributable to trade policy, are eating into margins on top of an already-compressed environment. Investors who bought at 2022 peaks with cheap debt are most exposed.

The Q1 2026 uptick — margins rising from 24.7% to 25.4% year-over-year — is technically the first quarterly improvement in two years. Forecasters are cautiously optimistic, though “cautiously” is doing heavy lifting in that sentence.

Global / Lifestyle Brew

Japan’s empty-home crisis is becoming everyone else’s bargain hunting opportunity

An aging population, inheritance tax quirks, and a collapsing yen have created a glut of abandoned Japanese homes that foreigners are snapping up at prices that would barely cover a US down payment.

Japan’s akiya phenomenon stems from demographic math: the country is getting older and more urban simultaneously, leaving rural and suburban homes to sit empty after inheritances. A tax policy that penalizes tearing down existing structures keeps inventory growing.

For foreign buyers, the equation is striking. Some properties sell for tens of thousands of dollars. The yen near multi-decade lows amplifies the discount further. AkiyaMart — a platform connecting international buyers with sellers — grew from 8,000 to 60,000 users in a single year.

The catch: many akiya are remote, need significant work (termites, collapsed roofs, overgrown land), and navigating Japanese property law as a foreign buyer has its own complexity. But for someone willing to treat it as a multi-year renovation project — with a view of a mountain — the numbers are hard to argue with.

Culture / Weekend Brew

Home renovation horror stories from real homeowners

Morning Brew readers submitted their renovation nightmares — and delivered a remarkable collection of Roman cemeteries, drain snakes, doll parts in walls, and propane tank fireballs.

The Roman cemetery: A reader renovating a family home in Europe reinforced the foundation and discovered 30+ intact Roman-era bodies, an ancient city wall, and a moat. Six years of archaeological teams and government oversight later — they still plan to open it as a B&B.

The snake in the bathtub: A contractor had to retrieve a live snake from a downstairs bathroom drain during a final walkthrough. The reader reports still having “issues” showering in that bathroom.

The doll parts: A DIY wall demolition turned up not one but dozens of dismembered antique dolls sealed inside the plaster. No explanation provided or possible.

The propane fireball: A pool installation ended when a backhoe fell through a 100-year-old septic system, then knocked the top off a 500-gallon propane tank. The resulting 100-foot flame required three days of fire department monitoring to burn out.
