Eli Lilly Obliterates Expectations as GLP-1 Empire Keeps Growing

The drugmaker's Q1 revenue of $19.8 billion crushed Wall Street's $17.62 billion forecast, up 56% year-over-year. Lilly raised its full-year sales outlook by $2 billion, now expecting up to $85 billion.

Eli Lilly's weight loss drug juggernaut shows no signs of slowing. Mounjaro revenue more than doubled, jumping 125% to $8.66 billion in worldwide Q1 sales, while Zepbound brought in $4.16 billion in US sales alone, up 80%. Lilly now commands 60.1% of the US obesity and diabetes drug market, compared with Novo Nordisk's 39.4%.

The big test ahead: whether the public adopts Lilly's new weight loss pill, Foundayo, approved just last month. CEO Dave Ricks said 20,000+ people have already started the drug, with about 1,000 new patients daily. Meanwhile, the FDA proposed excluding key GLP-1 ingredients from bulk compounder lists, which would kneecap copycat makers like Hims & Hers.

The GLP-1 ripple effects keep spreading: Magnum Ice Cream credited portion-control formats for offsetting slower treat sales, while Hershey CEO Kirk Tanner reported sharp rises in gum and mint sales, chalked up to the unofficial "Ozempic breath" side effect. Morning Brew

Columnist Daily Dad

We Can Choose to Get Back on Track

Maybe lately you haven't been as good a parent as you would like to be. You've been on your phone too much. You let your short temper get the best of you. You prioritized work over family. You got too wrapped up in your expectations, you were too harsh, you refused to see things their way.

Well? That's all in the past. It happened. It shouldn't have, but it did. And there's nothing you can do now to undo it. The question is, how do you move forward? We have the power, at any moment, to get back on track. We can choose, always, to return to the standard we want to live up to as parents.

It's like going to the gym. Sometimes you miss a day, then you miss another, and the next thing you know, it's been weeks since you've gone. Okay. It happened. But tomorrow hasn't yet. You have a choice. Will you do better? Will you do what you know is right? Will you, as Marcus Aurelius says, get back to the rhythm?

No parent has time to indulge in pity parties. Nor can any parent justify a continued slide. We screw up. We fall short. We're not what we want to be — what we promised ourselves we'd be, what we owe to our kids. And? We can choose right now to get back on track. We can choose right now to do better.

Venture & Finance 1% Better

The Smart Money Won't Sell: Insiders Are Holding at the Top of the Market

OpenAI's October tender drew $10.3 billion in buyer demand, but employees only sold $6.6 billion. Anthropic's tender fell short by similar logic. The venture secondary market is running in reverse.

For thirty years, the secondary market ran on a clean information trade: insiders sell, outside capital pays a discount. At the top of today's market, the pattern has flipped. Anthropic priced its last round at $380 billion in February; by late April the company was reportedly weighing offers above $900 billion. Engineers who tendered in February sold at less than half of what those shares now appear to be worth.

The discount that historically defined the secondary asset class has compressed — on the names that matter, it has flipped entirely. Buyers are now paying premiums for stakes the most-informed parties have actively declined to sell. The retail buyer and the frontier-AI engineer are on opposite sides of the same trade.
