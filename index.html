Anthropic Heads to DC to Fight Ban on Its Most Powerful Model

The Trump administration abruptly banned Anthropic's new Fable 5 model from foreign use Friday, citing jailbreak risks. Anthropic cut off all access, then dispatched senior staff to Washington to negotiate a reversal — while 100+ cybersecurity experts signed an open letter calling the ban counterproductive.

Anthropic dispatched senior staff to DC this week after the Trump administration restricted Fable 5 to Americans only. The model — debuted last week as a safer version of the unreleased Mythos — was taken offline within hours of the order. "Both parties are working quickly to get this resolved," an Anthropic spokesperson told the WSJ. The company is also separately suing the Defense Department seeking reversal of its designation as a supply chain risk.

The ban followed Amazon reportedly telling officials it found ways to jailbreak Fable into a potential hacker asset. Anthropic countered it was given a 90-minute deadline with "no details on the actual threat," and that the same vulnerability exists in rival models including GPT-5.5 and several Chinese models. Critics say the opaque procedure could have a chilling effect on all AI labs.

Over 100 cybersecurity leaders — tied to Adobe, Zoom, Sophos, Nvidia, Vercel, and Stanford HAI — signed an open letter urging the US to lift the ban, arguing it handcuffs defenders without slowing attackers who can pull the same capabilities from rival models. Former Facebook security head Alex Stamos noted the flagged jailbreak only produced a defensive "proof of concept" — the kind security teams use to patch systems, not compromise them.

Columnist | Daily Dad

You've Got Yourself a Wild Animal

Plato said, with total seriousness, that "of all the wild things, a boy is the most difficult to handle." He meant it as a compliment — not that boys are aggressive, but that they are extraordinarily smart and haven't yet been taught how to channel that intelligence.

That gap between raw intelligence and guidance is where things go wrong at scale. Young men are fourteen times more likely to be incarcerated than their female peers. The suicide rate among men has risen by a third since 2010. As social scientist Richard Reeves put it: "Particularly for young men, this question about their role, this question about how to be in the world, is relatively new. The script used to be pretty clear… We've torn that up and replaced it with nothing."

Reeves also points to a school-age gender gap in grades — not standardized tests — driven largely by behavioral factors. What's needed is an honest, empathetic, data-based conversation about the real problems of boys and men — one not pushed by algorithm outrage or fringe voices in either direction.

As parents, we have the power to begin addressing this. To direct the wildness. To model good behavior. To be patient and present. Plato believed it would take parents and teachers "thoroughly devoted to this work" — directing native dispositions into right ways, always toward the good. That job hasn't changed in 2,400 years.

Vet Any Business Opportunity with NotebookLM

Turn a rough idea into a source-backed research brief using a repeatable AI workflow. Works for vendor selection, partnerships, new markets, or any decision that needs real comparison — not hallucinated answers.

  1. Ask ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini to write a one-page decision memo: the opportunity, options, your constraints, and questions you need answered.
  2. Upload that memo into NotebookLM as your first source. Ask it to surface the decision, options, evaluation criteria, and what sources are needed — without making a recommendation yet.
  3. Use NotebookLM's source discovery to research each option. Generate a structured brief per option: best fit, proof points, pricing evidence, implementation effort, risks, and what still needs confirming.
  4. Ask for a comparison table and final recommendation — require a winner, runner-up, an avoid-for-now option, fragile assumptions, sales-call questions, and a 30-day validation plan.
  5. Save each prompt (memo, source-coverage, vendor brief, recommendation) as a reusable system for the next opportunity.

Community workflow from Tyler K. in Chicago: "Cold prospecting used to mean 30 minutes of manual work per account. I built a Slackbot skill that eliminates all of it. I paste raw LinkedIn Sales Navigator contacts, the skill cross-references my CRM, infers email patterns, confidence-scores each contact, and creates high-confidence ones in Salesforce automatically — all without leaving Slack. I ran it across six accounts and created 50+ contacts in one session. I didn't need to know how to code. I just described what I wanted in plain English and iterated."

Starbucks Korea Closes Stores for Sensitivity Training After Massacre-Adjacent Promo

The coffee chain's South Korean locations will close early next Monday for mandatory history and sensitivity training after a campaign accidentally commemorated the anniversary of a deadly 1980 military crackdown.

To promote its new steel "Tank" tumblers, Starbucks Korea declared "Tank Day" on May 18 — the anniversary of the Gwangju massacre that killed hundreds of pro-democracy protesters. Worse, the campaign slogan "Thwack it on the table!" mirrored language police used after killing a pro-democracy student years later. Marketers reportedly chose the slogan after prompting AI for suggestions.

Starbucks Korea canceled the campaign and fired its CEO within hours. Card payment volumes dropped 26% in one week. Police booked the CEO and the parent company's billionaire chairman as criminal suspects. Starbucks Corporation — which licenses its brand in South Korea (its third-largest market) but holds no equity — called it "an unacceptable marketing incident."

Tech / AI Rundown AI

Nadella: Your AI Edge Is the Learning Loop, Not the Best Model

Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella's new memo argues a company's real AI advantage comes from proprietary workflows and judgment baked into AI systems — not from renting the top frontier model.

Nadella frames company value in two parts: "human capital" (what employees supply) and "token capital" (AI a company owns rather than rents). His test: remove one model, drop in a different one — if your company's expertise walks out the door with the swap, you never owned it. The "learning loop" — AI that continuously improves from your own operations and judgment — is the actual moat.

He also warned against a world where "every company across every sector is ceding value to a few models that eat everything they see." With open, cheaper models now competitive on many tasks, this argument is gaining real traction against the frontier labs' narrative that the steamroller is inevitable.

FTC Cracked Down on Hidden Dealer Fees — They're Still Everywhere

Months after the FTC warned 200+ dealerships to stop hiding fees, the practice is still widespread: 59% of 500 used-car sales reviewed by CoPilot included undisclosed discretionary charges through mid-2026.

A second firm flagged price gaps at nearly 40% of more than 10,000 dealers. The most common culprit is the documentation fee, but charges for warranties and reconditioning can add thousands. The FTC recently settled with one dealer for up to $75 million and is preparing broader enforcement. Consumer advocates say there are only four charges buyers should ever pay — documentation, sales tax, title/registration, and a legitimate prep fee — and knowing them before negotiating is your best defense.

The Floor Test Most Desk Workers Fail

The sit-and-rise test: can you get from the floor to standing without using hands, knees, or a wall? Studies tie a low score to significantly higher mortality risk. Score yourself out of 10, subtract a point per assist. The fix is almost too simple: sit on the floor while watching TV, working up to 30 minutes daily. Kelly Starrett's hip-opening routine actually moves the number.
