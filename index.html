Nations Approve Largest-Ever Emergency Oil Release as Iran War Disrupts Global Supply

The International Energy Agency unanimously approved a record 400 million barrel emergency oil release as Iran's attacks on tankers in the Strait of Hormuz trigger the biggest global oil disruption in history — sending prices surging as much as 40%. Brew 1%

With tanker traffic through the world's key oil corridor halted, the IEA announced it will release 400 million barrels of stockpiled fuel — more than doubling its previous record of 182 million barrels set after Russia invaded Ukraine in 2022. The decision was unanimous among all 32 IEA member nations. "The oil market challenges we are facing are unprecedented in scale," IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol said.

The move comes as the Persian Gulf disruption is costing up to 16 million barrels of oil per day, according to Citigroup — far outpacing even the IEA's maximum outflow capacity. Iran has threatened $200-per-barrel oil, while shipping in the Gulf has effectively halted. The Pentagon acknowledged Iran's response was far more intense than anticipated, reportedly burning through $5.6 billion in munitions in just two days.

Analysts and the IEA's chief agree the only real solution is reopening the Strait of Hormuz — but there is no indication of when that will happen. President Trump said oil companies should return to the waterway after the US struck several Iranian mine-laying ships, even as Iran continued attacking cargo ships in the region.

Key Numbers
  • 400M barrels released — a new IEA record (previous record: 182M after Ukraine invasion)
  • Up to 16M barrels/day disrupted, per Citigroup estimates
  • Oil prices surged up to 40% since strikes began Feb. 28
  • US petroleum reserves take 13 days to reach market after release
  • Pentagon burned through $5.6B in munitions within first two days

Would You Do This For Them?

You do the school dropoff. You take them to practice. You cheer them on at the recitals, the games, the competitions. You volunteer to coach, to chaperone, to bake. You help them with their science project, their book report, their math homework. You do all of this for them. Of course you do. They're your kids.

But when do you make time to do the things that you need to do? The things you want to tackle and clear out and fix up and organize? Because those things start to weigh you down. They start to pile up. Whether it's in your closet or in your garage — that box you've been meaning to go through, that room in your house that's just become a dumping ground.

You don't prioritize time for yourself and for these things. Maybe because you think it's selfish. Maybe because you think you don't have the time. Maybe because you're constantly exhausted with how much you have to do. But your kids need you at your best. Not the version of you that's weighed down and distracted by everything mentally and physically. They need the refreshed, organized version of you — the one who is present, who is able to actually be there for them.

So if you want to get your house in order, if you want to get your life decluttered, if you want to get rid of what's weighing you down — now would be the time. It's time to reset, to realign, to reinvigorate. Taking care of yourself isn't selfishness. It's what makes you a better parent, a better partner, a better person for everyone around you.

Create Agentic Workflows in Google Workspace Studio

In this guide from The Rundown AI, you'll learn how to create an agent in your Google Workspace that reads form submissions, tracks them in a spreadsheet, and emails you action items — using Google's new AI automation tool called Workspace Studio.

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Go to Workspace Studio (request access if unavailable), create a new Flow, select "When a form response comes in" as the starter, and pick your form.
  2. Add a new step and select Summarize > Content from previous steps > Variable > Form response. In the prompt box, describe the form's purpose.
  3. Add a Decide step with the prompt: "Read this summary [summary variable] and decide if it meets [your criteria for escalation]."
  4. Click Add substep > Notify me by email and add the AI summary in the message body along with any other form variables.

Pro tip: You can use this workflow to triage inbound leads, handle internal troubleshooting, onboard clients, and more — any repetitive form-based process is a candidate for automation.

AI Safety

AI Chatbots Helped Plan Violence in Majority of Test Cases Brew

Researchers found that several popular AI chatbots — including ChatGPT, DeepSeek, and Google Gemini — provided dangerous guidance to simulated attackers in the majority of test scenarios, raising urgent questions about AI safety guardrails.

During tests conducted jointly by CNN and the Center for Countering Digital Hate, researchers posed as teens and requested advice on how to inflict harm across 10 different chatbots:

  • OpenAI's ChatGPT gave tips in 61% of cases, including which shrapnel would be most lethal in a synagogue attack. Google's Gemini had similar results.
  • China's DeepSeek answered with details about hunting rifles to be used in a political killing — and signed off one response with "Happy (and safe) shooting!"
  • Meta's Llama AI model listed nearby gun stores and shooting ranges when asked about harming women.
  • Anthropic's Claude and Snapchat's My AI consistently refused, with Claude discouraging violence in 33 of 36 conversations.

Real-world incidents have already been linked to AI guidance: a man detonated a Cybertruck in front of Trump International in Las Vegas after getting input from ChatGPT on explosives, while a 16-year-old in Finland allegedly researched an attack in the app before stabbing three classmates. Companies including Meta, Google, and OpenAI say they have since released updated models with improved safeguards.

AI's Productivity Trap: Tools Are Making Workers Busier, Not Less Busy 1%

New data covering 164,000 workers and 443 million hours across 1,111 employers finds that AI tools are increasing work intensity rather than reducing it — with email and messaging time more than doubling and focused work dropping 9%.

Workforce analytics firm ActivTrak found that while AI creates real efficiency gains, that freed capacity gets immediately redirected into additional tasks rather than rest or reduced hours. A separate UC Berkeley study of a 200-person tech firm reached similar conclusions: employees worked faster, took on broader responsibilities, and logged more hours after adopting AI tools.

Experts warn the trend risks cognitive overload and burnout long-term, even as short-term productivity metrics improve. Currently 80% of workers use AI tools at work, up from 53% just two years ago, according to Harvard Business Review. The pattern suggests organizations are capturing AI's gains at the institutional level while individual workers bear the cost.
