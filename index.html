The Business of Pretending to Be Other People

Celebrity impersonation is a booming industry — from Elvis look-alikes earning six figures to tribute bands commanding $50K per show to Cameo personalities charging by the message. Morning Brew

According to Glassdoor, celebrity impersonators can earn as much as $129,000 a year. "A-listers" who look like Tom Cruise or other major stars can pull in between $500 and $10,000 for a single corporate event or private party.

Tribute bands like Brit Floyd list their appearance fee between $35,000 and $50,000. But beware the dark side: as deepfake technology improves, scammers are pretending to be Brad Pitt to bilk victims for thousands, and one creator posted eerily convincing Tom Cruise videos.

Celebrities fight back, too: Elvis impersonators at Las Vegas chapels received a cease-and-desist in 2022, and the Johnny Cash estate sued Coca-Cola under Tennessee's Elvis Act for using a tribute singer without permission.

Cinderella Is in Your House: The Princess Party Boom

Since the pandemic, parents have flocked to hiring costumed princesses for kids' parties — creating a cottage industry navigating Disney's strict copyright laws.

Ashley Jantz runs Once Upon a Princess in Indiana. When she started, there were just a couple of similar businesses in her area. Now there are as many as 15 statewide. Birthday packages start at $250 for an hour and go up to $300 for 90 minutes for one character, with additional characters available for a fee.

Despite a post-pandemic surge in requests, Jantz has seen a slowdown in bookings over the last year, which she attributes to "everybody tightening their belts a little." Her company still receives 30+ requests per month, with July and the holiday season being the busiest.

Companies work around Disney's IP by renaming characters — Rapunzel becomes "Tower Princess," Chewbacca becomes "Big Hairy Guy." They got a big legal win in 2018 when a New York federal judge ruled against Disney in its attempt to sue a company for allegedly confusing consumers with knockoffs.

America's 250th Birthday Is Showtime for Historical Impersonators

Museums, historical societies, and event organizers are tapping powdered-wig-donning history buffs to enliven celebrations of the semiquincentennial.

The federal government, states, and localities are spending hundreds of millions to celebrate. A George Washington impersonator appeared at the US Mint's recent unveiling of a commemorative coin. Massachusetts250 is staging events near Boston with costumed impersonators of John Adams, Martha Washington, and Rachel Revere.

Historic Philadelphia hired 10 new performers this winter, each receiving street performance training at the "Benstitute". Colonial Williamsburg expects 2.1 million visitors in 2026, up from 1.8 million in 2024.

Brian Patrick Mulligan, a full-time Ben Franklin impersonator with 35 years of experience, told Morning Brew he's getting 5–10 private event inquiries per day — roughly as many as he got weekly last year. Historic Philadelphia says requests to hire its impersonators are up about 40%. Mulligan charges $1,776 for an hourlong keynote address.

From the Vegas Strip to Times Square: The Costumed Characters Who Work America's Tourist Zones

Ordinary people don capes every day to make sure we all have celebrity lookalikes to take a funny photo with. They do it out of duty and purpose — but mostly for tips.

Hollywood Boulevard's Walk of Fame is some of the most hallowed ground for these costumed copycats. But their presence isn't always welcome: in 2010, the LAPD cracked down following complaints that some impersonators were too forceful with their tip-seeking. Actors playing Batman, Spider-Man, and The Incredible Hulk were among those rounded up — proving that even a faux hero can live long enough to become the villain.
