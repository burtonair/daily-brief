Prediction markets: staggering growth, brutal math for everyday bettors

Kalshi controls over 90% of the US prediction market with annualized revenue exceeding $1.5 billion. Its valuation went from $11B to $22B in six months. Most users still lose money.

Unlike sportsbooks where you bet against the house, prediction markets are peer-to-peer: your loss is another person’s gain, and platforms collect fees and commissions. The combined global trading volume of Kalshi and Polymarket went from $4.5 billion in September 2025 to $24 billion in April 2026. The user base skews young and male (71%), with sports, crypto, and politics as the hottest categories.

Law Brew

Insider trading has followed prediction markets into every corner of public life

From classified military intelligence to corporate search data to reality TV scripts, the incentive to trade on non-public information is becoming prediction markets’ defining vulnerability.

A US Army Special Forces soldier will stand trial for allegedly making $400,000 on Polymarket using classified knowledge of plans to capture Venezuelan President Maduro. A Google employee was charged with fraud for betting on the company’s own most-searched terms. Kalshi fined a MrBeast editor $20,000 for wagers tied to unreleased videos from his employer.

Bloomberg reported that several well-timed Iran war wagers from multiple newly opened Polymarket accounts generated $1 million in profits — with no resulting investigation. Both platforms have fined and banned users for insider trading. Kalshi now plans to require users to disclose their employer before making sensitive trades. Critics say that’s not nearly enough.

Democratic senators have called on the CFTC to combat insider trading by restricting bet types. The agency is also being sued by three states seeking to assert regulatory authority — a battle that could reach the Supreme Court in 2027.

Entertainment Brew

Survivor, The Masked Singer, Next Level Chef: reality TV’s spoiler crisis

Survivor 50 winner Aubry Bracco had a 61% chance of winning before the season even premiered. By finale night, $32.7 million had been bet on her — at 97% odds. Kalshi sent a push notification naming her before the episode aired.

Host Jeff Probst told Variety that prediction markets are “incentivizing people to lie, cheat, and steal.” Kalshi says it investigated and found no insider trading. Other pretaped shows have faced the same problem: The Masked Singer and Next Level Chef had their winners correctly predicted before their finales. Polymarket partnered with the Golden Globes this year and picked 26 out of 28 winners correctly.

The CFTC announced last week it was seeking public comment to define whether reality game shows fall under its jurisdiction — specifically whether they involve enough “skill or athletic activity” to be considered games rather than contests subject to prediction market rules. The agency is trying to decide whether the platforms can legally offer these contracts at all.

How It Works

Sportsbooks vs. prediction markets: what’s the real difference? Brew

Sportsbooks (FanDuel, DraftKings) bet against the house and are regulated by state gambling law. Prediction markets (Kalshi, Polymarket) are peer-to-peer, regulated like oil futures by the CFTC, and available in states where sports betting is banned.

After a nearly four-year US ban following a $1.4M CFTC fine in 2022, Polymarket relaunched for American users following a federal court ruling clearing election contracts.
