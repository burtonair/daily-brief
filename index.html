Cover Story Morning Brew

The Big Business of Bunks and Bugs: America's $4.6 Billion Summer Camp Industry

As 20,000+ camps scatter across the US, summer camp has evolved from military-style drill grounds into a billion-dollar industry — one that serves gourmet farm-to-table meals, offers clay studios and podcasting classes, and can charge $17,000 for a single summer.

The US youth camp industry was worth an estimated $4.6 billion last year, according to IBISWorld. After a brutal $16 billion pandemic blow in 2020, camps have roared back — and so have prices. Day camps now cost an average of $87 per day; overnight camps clock in around $173 per day, per the American Camp Association. In cities like NYC, popular camps can fill up 10 months before start dates and easily run past $10,000 for the summer.

The spending doesn't stop at tuition. Wealthy parents have invented an entire ancillary economy: specialized laundry services to get bug spray out of everything, $125/hour packing helpers, and personal shoppers offering "new camper appointments" at children's boutiques. At the start of 2025, 25% of parents said they expected to spend over $2,000 on summer camps, per a NerdWallet survey.

Meanwhile, the market is shifting. Long-term residential camps are increasingly hard to find as hectic modern schedules drive parents toward shorter, specialized programs — from robotics to stand-up comedy to, yes, dynamite training.

History / Education Morning Brew

The Full History of Summer Camps: From Toughening Up "Weakly Boys" to Gourmet Meals

Summer camp's evolution from spartan character-building to billion-dollar childhood experience tracks neatly with American prosperity, anxiety, and the ever-shifting definition of what kids actually need.

The first independent summer camp appeared in 1866 — the North Mountain School of Physical Culture, which charged $200 for four months in the woods of Northeastern Pennsylvania to toughen up what it called "weakly boys." By the end of World War I, camps were well established across the US, serving both poor children gaining access to nature and wealthy children being whipped into shape outside their summer hotels.

After World War II, the philosophy inverted. Parents increasingly felt their children had already been toughened by rationing, air raid drills, and family members dying in service. Camp became a place to chill out instead — hence the handicrafts-and-s'mores era most people associate with it today.

The last decades of the 20th century brought specialized camps centered on single interests: Space Camp launched at the US Space & Rocket Center in Huntsville, AL in 1982, and the concept proliferated through computer, basketball, dance, and theater camps throughout the '90s. Today, long-term residential camps are giving way to shorter specialized programs as hectic modern schedules reshape what families want from the summer.

Business / Niche Morning Brew

There's a Niche Summer Camp for Literally Everything

Pretty much any pretend game kids used to play in the backyard has now been professionally organized and monetized by the summer camp industry.

Fighting zombies with foam swords: Kids aged 8-17 can LARP their hearts out at Wizards & Warriors Camp in Charlton, Massachusetts.

Jumping off roofs: Southern California's Hollywood Stunt Camp and Secret Agent Camp offer $1,695-per-week alternatives to letting kids fling themselves around living room furniture until you hear a crack.

Blowing stuff up: Missouri University of Science and Technology teaches 16- to 18-year-olds how to use dynamite, set off fireworks, and turn pyrotechnic proclivities into a career.

Honorable mentions: Kids 'N Comedy for young stand-ups, Camp BizSmart for 11- to 15-year-old aspiring entrepreneurs, and SparkForce for manufacturing-inclined middle and high schoolers who want to operate heavy machinery.
