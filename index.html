Why All AI Company Logos Look Like… That

Something distinctly anatomical is perennially happening with AI logos. Nearly every frontier AI lab has converged on the same circular, abstract, sphincter-adjacent aesthetic — and there’s a real design reason why.

You can blame OpenAI. When the company released ChatGPT in 2022, its hexagonal, donut-like emblem quietly set the standard for what “serious AI” is supposed to look like. “Circular. Abstract. Untouchable,” Lisa Smith, global chief design officer of Uncommon Creative Studio, told Fast Company. Other tech companies followed suit en masse. Morning Brew

By 2023, hexagons were 4.6 times more likely to appear in AI logos than in logos overall. The most rectally reminiscent of them all is Anthropic’s asterisk-like logo for Claude, which bears a striking resemblance to Kurt Vonnegut’s infamous illustration of a sphincter in Breakfast of Champions.

From a design perspective, circular imagery evokes completeness, infinity, and approachability — all qualities AI companies want to project to consumers who may be wary of the technology. AI executives may also be too nervous to break the mold now that concentric logos are as synonymous with AI as red and yellow are with fast food. The result: a global monoculture of swirling, vaguely cosmic brand marks.

Five Brand Redesigns That Didn’t Blow Up

Most rebrand announcements are greeted with online fury. These five companies found a way to update their visual identity without triggering mass unsubscribes, boycotts, or designer shaming.

English Premier League zoomed in on its lion mascot’s head for the 2016–2017 season, stripping the full-body logo down to a streamlined symbol that stayed instantly recognizable. Designers cheered the change.

Dunkin’ dropped “Donuts” in 2018, reflecting that coffee had quietly taken over from sugary pastries as its core product. A YouGov survey found positive buzz grew in the days after the rebrand.

Rolls-Royce swapped its double-R logo for a sleeker version of the Spirit of Ecstasy hood ornament in 2020 — praised for successfully carrying “the company’s sense of luxury into the digital age.”

Reddit hired Pentagram before its 2024 IPO to unify years of scattered DIY aesthetics. The result: a sleeker 3D Snoo alien and a custom font — and redditors didn’t downvote it.

eBay stripped sidebar text and added white space in 2024, earning Fast Company praise for following best layout practices “without appearing too cold to middle America.”

Choose Your Font Carefully — These People Didn’t

Fonts aren’t interchangeable. Calligraphy that works on a fine-dining menu is catastrophic on a road sign — and some people had to learn that the hard way.

Chanel’s 2016 “I Love Coco” campaign ran into trouble when the typeface made it read as “I Love Cow.” Online retailer Belle Chic printed “My favorite color is glitter” on a tote in 2017, but the font choice produced a deeply offensive misreading.

Most consequentially: Calibri helped bring down Pakistan’s prime minister in 2017. Investigators alleged documents in a corruption case were forged — the tell being that they were typed in Calibri, which wasn’t publicly available in the year they were supposedly written. PM Nawaz Sharif later stepped down.

The Avatar franchise quietly changed its typeface after an SNL sketch starring Ryan Gosling savaged the film’s use of Papyrus. And in 2024, the Philadelphia 76ers social media team provided a perfect reminder of why font vigilance matters.

Less Accurate Subway Maps Are More Useful — Here’s Why

A century-old design choice explains why a glance at a subway map tells you it’ll take three trains to reach a bar two miles away. The trick is deliberate, principled inaccuracy.

Harry Beck was a drafter for the London Underground in the 1930s who noticed something obvious: existing tube maps showed too much of London’s geography. The tangle of streets and curves made actual rail connections hard to read. His revolutionary solution was to ditch geographic accuracy entirely.

Beck’s redesigned map used only vertical, horizontal, and 45-degree lines in bold contrasting colors. Street names vanished. The central city was zoomed in relative to the outer boroughs. The result looked like an electrical circuit diagram — and it was far easier to navigate than any geographically accurate version. It was initially rejected by the Underground as “too revolutionary” before a trial run proved enormously popular.

Every major city metro map today draws from his design philosophy. New York’s recently revamped subway map follows the same playbook: boldly colored, angular train lines on a simplified depiction of the five boroughs, emphasizing connections over cartographic truth.

Rolls-Royce Takes the Spirit of Ecstasy Digital

Rolls-Royce ditched its classic double-R monogram in 2020 for a sleeker version of its iconic hood ornament. The gamble: modernizing a 120-year-old luxury brand for digital screens without alienating its ultra-wealthy clientele. Critics called it a rare success for a brand of that age and prestige.

Beck’s Tube Map Turns 90 — and Still Runs Every Subway on Earth

Harry Beck’s 1933 London Underground diagram was initially rejected as “too revolutionary.” A small trial proved so popular it became standard. Today virtually every mass transit system on earth uses some variant of his diagrammatic approach.

Font Hall of Fame & Shame

Papyrus: A Font So Bad It Changed a Franchise

James Cameron’s Avatar — the highest-grossing film ever — used Papyrus for its title card. The choice was mercilessly lampooned in an SNL sketch starring Ryan Gosling. The franchise quietly changed its typeface before subsequent releases.

Calibri: An Unlikely Tool of Political Justice

The humble Microsoft default font became a forensic weapon in 2017 when investigators noticed that documents allegedly from 2006 were typed in Calibri — which wasn’t publicly released until 2007. The discrepancy helped accelerate the fall of Pakistan’s prime minister.

The Algorithm-ification of the Third Place

Social media didn’t just homogenize coffee shop aesthetics — it created a global feedback loop where every owner can see what’s going viral in Seoul, Melbourne, or Brooklyn and replicate it before the trend cools. Platforms like Yelp and Google Maps then reward the similarity by surfacing familiar-looking spots to visitors.

Vice Signaling: Conspicuous Consumption Comes Back

Post-pandemic quiet luxury was a reaction to excess. Now the pendulum may be swinging: fall/winter 2026 trends show bold expression, visible logos, and “opulence-maxxing.” Even Gen Z — supposedly cash-strapped — is increasingly drawn to luxury labels.
