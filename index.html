Columnist Daily Dad

They Are Not Entitled — They’re Just Scared

It hits you the wrong way — the way they don’t ask, they just expect. The way they expect to get what they want, the way they want things you never got when you were a kid. The speed with which they got comfortable with comforts and privileges that are just not normal.

But Dr. Becky Kennedy has the explanation: “Entitlement,” she says, “is the intolerance of uncomfortable feelings.” They don’t want to go without. They don’t want to wait. They don’t want to miss out. They don’t want to have to do things the hard way. The discomfort scares them. And you know what? You were afraid of that when you were a kid, and you’re afraid of it now, too.

It’s not our job as parents to give them everything they want. It is our job to help them with these very reasonable feelings. Help them see that the frustration is manageable and so is the discomfort. Help them understand that not everything is fair or easy or quick, but that they — and we — can handle disappointment. That’s what builds resilience.

Health Morning Brew

Cyclospora Parasite Outbreak Hits Midwest — Taco Bell Investigated

An explosive diarrhea-causing outbreak of the cyclospora parasite has spread through 34 states, with Michigan reporting at least 3,309 cases. Health officials are investigating Taco Bell as a possible source, though no link has been confirmed.

The parasite spreads through infected human feces contaminating irrigation or wash water, and ends up on produce with high water content — leafy greens, berries, snow peas. Michigan Taco Bell locations have voluntarily pulled lettuce and cilantro as a precaution. The parasite isn’t deadly but can last months if untreated, causing severe dehydration.

Tracking the source is unusually hard: symptoms can be delayed for weeks, and the CDC scaled back one of its cyclospora tracking programs last year. What you can do: wash and scrub produce, avoid bagged lettuce, cook your vegetables, and wash your hands.

The Software Wrapper Around AI Is Now More Valuable Than the Model Itself

Run Claude Opus 4.5 against SWE-bench Pro with one harness: it scores 45.9%. Run it unchanged with a different harness: 55.4%. Not a single model parameter changed. The software scaffold — the “harness” — now moves the score more than the model does. This is the most important shift happening in AI right now that nobody is talking about.

A harness is everything that isn’t the model: the system prompt, the tool catalog, the memory rules, retry logic, execution loops. Claude Code is Anthropic’s harness. Codex is OpenAI’s. A Meta/Harvard scaffold took the cheaper Claude Sonnet 4.5 and edged out Claude Opus 4.5 on Anthropic’s own test — the weaker model, better harnessed, came out ahead.

Labs have noticed and are spending accordingly. Anthropic’s Code with Claude event released no new model — only harness features. Within seven days of each other, Anthropic and OpenAI both shipped managed-agent infrastructure. The switching costs are now in accumulated workflows and memory, not the raw model weights. The pattern is familiar: when the microprocessor commoditized, value moved to the OS; when smartphone silicon commoditized, it moved to the app store. Model capability is becoming abundant, and returns are moving to the layer that decides how to use it.
