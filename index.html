Anthropic Blacklisted by Pentagon — OpenAI Steps In to Lock Up the Defense AI Contract

The US Department of Defense labeled Anthropic a "supply chain risk" — a designation typically reserved for adversarial nations — and within hours reached a deal with OpenAI to deploy its models on classified military networks, with the same ethical guardrails Anthropic had demanded.

Even before the Pentagon's 5:01 PM ET deadline expired for Anthropic to lose its $200 million defense contract — unless it agreed to let Claude be used for mass domestic surveillance or fully autonomous weapons — President Trump posted on Truth Social that the government would "IMMEDIATELY CEASE" using Anthropic's technology and "not do business with them again!"

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth then confirmed the DOD would label Anthropic a "supply chain risk" — a designation typically applied to companies from adversarial nations that bars businesses with government contracts from working with them. The move represented the most aggressive government stance yet against a major AI safety firm over its ethical limits on Claude's use.

Around 10 PM ET, Sam Altman posted on X that OpenAI had "reached an agreement with the Department of War to deploy our models in their classified network." Altman's post explicitly listed "prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance" and "human responsibility for the use of force" as bedrock principles — the same red lines Anthropic had held. And the DOD agreed to all of them.

AI & National Security

The AI Governance Fight: Who Gets to Set the Rules? Morning Brew

Behind the Anthropic-Pentagon standoff lies a deeper structural tension: private companies built AI, giving them control over how it's used. The government, entirely dependent on public-private partnerships, is now pushing back — with mixed results.

The US military has developed advanced technology like GPS, which gave it control over how that tech was disseminated. But it didn't lead AI development — private companies were better positioned to raise billions and attract specialized talent, leaving the government reliant on public-private partnerships that come with complications.

To turn a profit, tech companies must focus on commercial applications. Government contracts are important too — but if a company gets a bad reputation for letting its tech be used in ethically questionable ways, it risks losing commercial customers. Anthropic's refusal to bend on Claude's guardrails reflects that reality: its commercial viability depends on the trust of enterprise clients and civil society who have no interest in mass surveillance tools.

OpenAI, by accepting the same red lines Anthropic held while still winning the contract, may have threaded the needle — serving both the government and the commercial market simultaneously. The Defense Department has said it wants to be an "AI-first" fighting force, and all major AI companies are competing for lucrative contracts as the technology evolves faster than any regulation on its use.

AI & Business

OpenAI's $110 Billion Round: Amazon, Nvidia, and SoftBank Go All-In Morning Brew

OpenAI announced one of the largest private investment rounds in history — $50 billion from Amazon and $30 billion each from Nvidia and SoftBank — valuing the ChatGPT maker at $730 billion pre-funding, more than double its valuation from a year ago.

This is the most cash Amazon has ever invested in another company. The full $110 billion is contingent on OpenAI either going public — which the Wall Street Journal reports it plans to do in the fourth quarter — or announcing it has achieved artificial general intelligence, per an unnamed source who told Bloomberg.

The timing is striking: the funding announcement came the same day OpenAI locked up the Pentagon contract Anthropic had just lost — handing the company military credibility and historic commercial backing in a single 24-hour period. Stocks broadly fell sharply yesterday as investors worried about ripple effects from risky private credit and AI — but OpenAI's trajectory appears entirely separate from Wall Street's anxieties.
