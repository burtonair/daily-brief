Alan Greenspan, the Fed’s most celebrated chairman, dead at 100 Brew

The legendary economist who led the Federal Reserve under four presidents from 1987 to 2006 died Monday from Parkinson’s disease complications — celebrated for engineering boom-era soft landings, blamed by some for planting the seeds of the 2008 financial crisis.

Greenspan entered government during the Ford administration after co-founding a successful economic forecasting firm, and became known for basing decisions on meticulous data analysis rather than textbook economics. He gained guru status for managing monetary policy through some of the economy’s trickiest moments: engineering a swift recovery from the 1987 market crash by slashing rates, presiding over mid-’90s rate increases without causing a recession, and correctly foreseeing that productivity gains from personal computers would tame inflation during the dot-com boom.

He was infamous for hard-to-decipher “Fedspeak,” but also pioneered interest rate change announcements as a way to guide markets. His tenure from 1987 to 2006 coincided with one of the longest economic booms in US history, from 1991 to 2001.

On the other hand: an acolyte of libertarian writer Ayn Rand, Greenspan championed light-touch financial regulation during the Clinton years. His refusal to raise rates and rein in subprime mortgage lenders in the 2000s led the bipartisan Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission to say he was partially responsible for the Great Recession. “Chairman Greenspan’s legacy endures at the Federal Reserve — in those he mentored directly, in the economists and public servants he inspired,” the Fed said yesterday.

Columnist — Daily Dad

It Feels Good For a Moment … And Then Horrible

Has there ever been a parent who didn’t yell, who didn’t lose their cool? Probably not. Yet we know this is not a good strategy — for them or for us.

Behavioral analyst Samantha Mann wrote about her effort to go a whole month without yelling at her young son. She said: “For a minute, the yelling feels good. The moment I open my mouth it’s an emotional release disguised as a lecture … Here I am, demanding respect, gentleness, and emotional control while not exuding any of it. That’s when my post-rage hangover hits: the guilt that I have felt more anger toward this tiny person I love than toward those who have actually harmed me. The people who deserved my anger got understanding, grace, and even empathy.”

Release or not, you’re going to feel horrible every time you yell at your kids. It’s not necessary, it doesn’t help, and it doesn’t fix anything — in fact, it only makes things worse.

Instead, take that moment to think. To breathe and calm down. To prevent yourself from saying or doing something you’ll regret — something that will affect them long after the moment has passed. Isn’t that exactly what you want them to be able to do, too? Show them what it looks like.

US eases Iran oil sanctions as Switzerland nuclear talks grind forward

The Treasury Department suspended sanctions on Iranian oil for 60 days — letting Iran sell in US dollars for the first time in decades — as negotiators work toward a final nuclear deal.

The change should make it easier for Iran to profit from its oil without relying on a shadow network to sell mostly to China. VP JD Vance, who returned from Burgenstock, Switzerland while technical negotiators stayed for further talks, said the weekend sessions “laid a very good foundation for a successful final deal.” Vance said Iran had agreed to allow nuclear inspectors back into the country, though Iranian officials did not confirm this and said “no new commitments” had been made.

Talks are expected to continue through the week and remain among the top market movers to watch. The sanctions suspension is a major reversal of longstanding US policy implemented across multiple administrations.

Business Brew

Polymarket paid creators to fake $900K in winnings using a lookalike website: WSJ

A Wall Street Journal investigation found Polymarket used fake wagers, a near-identical dummy site, and online creators to run predatory ads targeting Americans — despite being banned from US crypto trading since 2022.

College-aged creators posted videos of themselves making winning bets — but on a fake Polymarket site where the lowercase ‘l’ was replaced with a capital ‘i.’ Polymarket then paid a marketing firm to deploy “sockpuppet” accounts that hid their affiliation, reposting videos to amplify reach. Clippers were only paid if 60% or more of their audience was in the US, which Polymarket can’t legally serve.

The WSJ found creators claimed ~$900,000 in winnings that would have been ~$166,000 in losses on the real site. Polymarket told CBS it is launching an internal investigation. Federal law requires truthful advertising and disclosure of business ties; the CFTC would not comment on potential investigations.
