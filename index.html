Disney's Baby Yoda Bet May Become the Franchise's Biggest Box-Office Flop

The Mandalorian and Grogu hit theaters yesterday, but the Force may not be with it — the film is projected to open at just $80–$100M domestically, which would make it the worst-opening Star Wars movie ever.

That would place it below Solo: A Star Wars Story — currently the only Star Wars film to lose money — which also opened over Memorial Day weekend in 2018. Preview revenue is already trailing Solo's pace, a grim sign heading into the holiday.

The challenge is structural: the film continues a Disney+ series viewers may assume requires homework to understand. Casual walk-ins might skip it; die-hard fans who soured on Season 3 in 2023 may not bother either. The Memorial Day timing was meant to capture family audiences — Baby Yoda and Pedro Pascal's Mando represent the franchise's most accessible corner of the galaxy — but that strategy is under pressure.

Disney has wider reasons to be nervous. DC's Superman outgrossed every Marvel release last year, breaking a 17-year MCU winning streak. The most recent Star Wars theatrical release — The Rise of Skywalker in 2019 — earned half as much as The Force Awakens. Analysts note that even a box-office disappointment could spur a Grogu merchandise resurgence, a reliable Disney fallback when the movies stumble.

The Time You Actually Have With Your Kids

"Do you think anyone has ever really regretted choosing family first? Maybe they missed out on some stuff here and there, but looking back on their life, do you think anyone even remembers?"

This week's Daily Dad passage hits at a rhythm every parent knows but struggles to hold onto. The early years feel endless — and then suddenly you get them for forty-five minutes in the morning. Fifteen minutes in the car. An hour or two after dinner before bed. What seemed like an interminable stretch reveals itself to be finite and fleeting.

On the Daily Dad YouTube channel this week, Ryan Holiday shared his top parenting books — the argument being that instead of learning by trial and error alone, you learn from others' experiences. There's no Rosetta Stone for parenting. The feedback loop is slow and the stakes are high. Reading around it is one of the few shortcuts that actually works.

Ryan is currently reading Small Things Like These by Claire Keegan: "It seemed both proper and at the same time deeply unfair that so much of life was left to chance." A quiet reminder that the moments we get aren't guaranteed — and that presence in them is the whole point.

Tech Morning Brew

Waymo Halts Service in Four Cities After Robotaxis Drive Into Floodwaters

After multiple incidents of its self-driving cars plowing into flooded streets, Waymo has temporarily suspended service in Atlanta, Austin, Dallas, and Houston. A software update issued last week to prevent exactly this scenario failed when Atlanta flooding began before emergency alerts were sent.

During Wednesday's heavy Atlanta rain, an empty Waymo car drove directly into a flooded road and got stuck. A separate vehicle carrying a journalist also repeatedly attempted what amounted to self-boating. Waymo had already halted service in San Antonio last month over a similar incident and issued a recall on thousands of vehicles for a software fix — the same fix that didn't work in Atlanta.

The company maintains its vehicles are statistically safer than human drivers for injury-causing collisions. But it's also under federal investigation for failing to stop for school buses and for striking a child in California. Waymo says it's developing additional software changes and will monitor conditions before lifting the suspensions.

Business Morning Brew

Estee Lauder's $40 Billion European Merger Dies Over One Founder's Stake

Estee Lauder and Spanish beauty group Puig called off merger talks that would have united Clinique, MAC, and La Mer with Charlotte Tilbury under one empire. The deal collapsed when Charlotte Tilbury moved to trigger a sale of her 21.5% stake — a move that could have cost Puig roughly 850 million euros.

Markets rewarded the collapse differently: Estee Lauder jumped more than 10% Friday while Puig fell over 13%. Estee Lauder says it will go it alone. For Tilbury — the British mogul behind the wildly popular Pillow Talk lipstick — the move underscored just how much leverage a single founder can wield inside a complex corporate deal.

Immigration Morning Brew

Trump Admin Will Require Green Card Applicants to Leave the US First

The administration will require most people pursuing permanent US residency — including work visa holders and spouses of US citizens — to return to their home countries while their applications are processed, a dramatic break from current practice.

Under the existing system, many applicants can adjust their status without leaving the country. The new requirement creates significant logistical burden for H-1B visa holders, family members of US citizens, and others who may have lived in the US for years and have little connection to their country of origin. Legal challenge is widely expected.

Finance Morning Brew

House Committee to Probe Insider Trading on Prediction Markets Polymarket and Kalshi

The House Oversight Committee announced it will investigate potential insider trading on prediction markets — platforms that let bettors wager real money on real-world political and economic outcomes.

Prediction markets exploded in popularity during the 2024 election when Polymarket (based offshore) outperformed mainstream polls. Kalshi, which operates legally in the US under CFTC oversight, has expanded to elections, Fed decisions, and other high-stakes events. The investigation centers on whether traders with material non-public information — political operatives, insiders, government contacts — have been gaming these markets for profit.
