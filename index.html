Who Do They Behave For?

Yeah, they're maniacs. Yeah, they curse like sailors. Yeah, they talk back — to you. But have you heard this complaint from their teacher? Has another parent come to you and said, "Hey look, this is a problem?"

Because if they haven't, then maybe you don't have a problem. Your kids are well behaved — where it counts. They understand that out in the world, out in public, certain norms have to be observed. They understand there are certain things that aren't appropriate at school or when they're a guest.

At home though? With the people they trust, who love them unconditionally? They understand that this is a safe place, that you're a safe person. They're exhausted behaving at school, on the street, in front of other people. In front of you, they understand they can let their guard down, they can stop pretending, stop observing some silly niceties.

Instead of being upset about that, you should be proud. You should take it as a compliment. The fact that your child saves their authentic, unfiltered self for home means they feel safe enough with you to truly be themselves. That is the whole point.

Science — AI & Medicine

Dog Dad Uses AI to Design Custom mRNA Cancer Vaccine for His Rescue Dog Morning Brew Rundown AI

Sydney tech entrepreneur Paul Conyngham chained ChatGPT, Grok, DeepMind's AlphaFold, and genomic sequencing to create a custom mRNA cancer vaccine for his rescue dog Rosie — and one of her tumors has shrunk by half.

Rosie was diagnosed with mast cell cancer in 2024 and given months to live despite chemotherapy and surgery. Conyngham, a machine-learning expert, used ChatGPT to map the research, paid $3,000 for genomic sequencing, then fed the data through AlphaFold to model Rosie's mutations.

  • The UNSW RNA Institute helped turn the formula into a physical vaccine.
  • Conyngham revealed the "final vaccine construct was designed by Grok."
  • One tumor shrank by half after her December injection; Conyngham is now working on a second vaccine for non-responding tumors.

Critics, including science communicator Hank Green, caution that this one case study doesn't generalize well and that Conyngham's extraordinary personal investment of time and money was the critical factor.

Colossal Biosciences Plans to Resurrect the Dodo Using Ancient DNA 1% Better

Colossal Biosciences, a Dallas-based company valued at $10.2 billion, announced plans to resurrect the dodo, woolly mammoth, and other extinct species using ancient DNA and CRISPR gene-editing technology.

The firm gained attention last year after producing three wolf pups it called resurrected dire wolves. Scientists remain divided: critics argue Colossal's animals are genetically modified versions of existing species, not true resurrections — the company edited just 14 of 19,000 gray wolf genes to produce the dire wolf pups.

Bird species like the dodo present additional technical challenges, as birds cannot be cloned like mammals. Colossal says its work addresses what it describes as a moral obligation amid Earth's ongoing extinction crisis.
