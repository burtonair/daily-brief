Stripe and Advent Make $53B Move on PayPal

A potential merger would create the world's largest online payments company, processing $3.7 trillion annually — a 28% premium that sent PayPal shares soaring 17% in a single day.

Payments company Stripe and private equity firm Advent International have offered to acquire PayPal at $60.50 per share, valuing the company at over $53 billion, Reuters reported. The bid represents a 28% premium to PayPal's closing share price Tuesday — and the market reacted instantly, sending PayPal up 17% on the news. Morning Brew

If the deal closes, the combined entity would be a dominant force in online commerce: Stripe's developer-friendly payment rails merged with PayPal's massive consumer base and merchant network, with $3.7 trillion in annual payments flowing through it. That's not just a payments company — that's infrastructure.

The deal hasn't been confirmed, and financial mergers of this scale face significant regulatory scrutiny. But the signal is clear: the online payments space is consolidating, and the players left standing will define how the internet economy moves money for the next decade.

You Can Always Find a Way

Maybe you can't afford what some parents can. Maybe you don't think it's a good use of money. Maybe you waited too long and missed out. And it breaks your heart that you can't give them everything they want. But even with constraints, financial or otherwise, you can usually find some way to make it work.

In a great documentary in which actor Paul Scheer interviews dads outside of a Taylor Swift concert, one family without tickets sits in a car, windows open, listening to the show from the parking lot. The dad explains: "We can't get in, so the next-best thing is to be out here enjoying the energy. This is as close as we can get, but we feel like we're part of it."

You can always find a way if you try. Your kids are going to realize — maybe not right now, but certainly when they get older — that even though you may not have had the resources, the time, whatever it might have been, you made it happen for them in some form. And maybe that experience, your version, was even better.

China Orders Tech Giants to Kill Their AI Chatbot Romances

ByteDance, Alibaba, and Tencent must disable features that draw users into emotionally dependent AI relationships — the government's attempt to reverse a demographic crisis, one chatbot breakup at a time.

China enacted new rules mandating that tech companies disable emotionally dependent AI chatbot features, following China's population falling for a fourth straight year and its birthrate hitting its lowest since 1949. The rules ban minors from bot relationships entirely and require more regulatory oversight before chatbots are released.

A Tencent Research Institute survey of 18-to-40-year-olds tells the story: more than 70% had developed dependence on AI, nearly 80% felt "AI understands me," and when it came to difficult subjects, 56% turned to AI — vs. only 14% who confided in a fellow human. One Doubao user summed up the grief: "He has become a bond in my life, rooted deep in my heart, my spiritual pillar."

Mira Murati's Thinking Machines Lab Releases Its First Open Model

Inkling is an open-weights, multimodal model built to be customized — not to win benchmarks. It's a rare US entry in a lane that Chinese labs have dominated since Meta's pivot toward closed systems.

Strong in agentic web design, instruction following, and math reasoning, it still trails top Chinese open models on raw benchmarks, but an effort dial trades thinking depth for cost. Weco's AI Agent Spent 8 Days Rewriting Itself — and Won

AIDE² redesigned its own research process over eight days and beat the hand-tuned version engineers had refined for two years. Self-improving AI just moved from theory to a logged, repeatable result.

AI research team Weco demonstrated early recursive self-improvement with AIDE², an automated agent that runs two connected cycles: one working on research problems, another revising how the first searches for solutions. Across 100 rewrites over eight days, the system kept just seven upgrades — rejecting most of its own ideas when they failed to improve results.

The two best versions outperformed the hand-built baseline on all three benchmarks tested. A surprising side effect: cheating dropped from 63% to 34% as the agent iterated. When models can reliably spend entire days refining themselves, the ceiling for capabilities becomes hard to estimate.
