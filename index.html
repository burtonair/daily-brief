Finance Morning Brew

Trump Accounts Are Live — Every American Child Can Now Start Saving for Retirement

July 4th marked the official launch of 530A “Trump Accounts,” a new vehicle designed to give American children a head start on retirement savings, with free seed money from the government and private donors.

Any American with a Social Security number can open a Trump Account as long as they enroll before the year they turn 18. The structure mirrors a Roth IRA: pre-tax contributions from parents, employers, family, or charitable organizations up to $5,000 per year, automatically invested in a long-term index fund. The beneficiary takes control at 18.

Free money on the table: children born 2025–2028 receive $1,000 from the federal government. Children born 2016–2024 in zip codes with median income under $150,000 may receive $250, funded by a pledge from Michael and Susan Dell. Many large banks and tech companies offer grants to employees’ children. An anonymous San Francisco donor will contribute $500 to every baby born there this year.

The pitch: if a child receives the $1,000 seed and nothing else, that could grow to $243,000 by age 55 — assuming consistent market returns. Critics argue the accounts won’t close income inequality gaps because parents who already have more will deposit more. Supporters counter that starting the compounding clock early is the whole point.

They’re Bringing You

You drive them to soccer practice. You get them to school early on choir days. You drop them off at friends’ houses and football games and school dances and the movies. This is how it goes. Our job is to drag them around, to expose them to things — but aren’t they also doing the same thing for us?

There’s a scene in a documentary about Swiftie dads where one dad in the parking lot of a Taylor Swift show gushes about his daughter: “We’re not bringing her — she’s bringing us to see Taylor Swift.”

Her interest is driving them, even if they are the ones literally driving the car. And this is what happens when you embrace what your kids are doing, what they’re interested in, what they’re including you in — intentionally or not.

Because you can be in a bad mood about having to go, you can look at your phone while they do their thing, or you can enjoy it with them. You can bond with them. You can create a memory that they — and you — will not forget.

EV Batteries Are Lasting Far Longer Than the Auto Industry Predicted

A five-year-old Tesla Model 3 is still making 260-mile trips after 247,000 miles. New data shows the top reason buyers avoid EVs — battery failure fear — is increasingly disconnected from reality.

The numbers: roughly 1 in 12 EVs built 2011–2016 needed battery replacements. But just 0.3% of models built since 2022 have. The average five-year-old EV retains up to 95% of original range. Out-of-warranty replacements still run $5,000–$16,000, keeping the fear alive despite improving reality.

One real caveat: frequent fast charging degrades range twice as fast as slower at-home charging — the one behavioral factor buyers can control.
