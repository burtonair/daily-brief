Cover Story The Rundown AI 1% Better

AI Is Already Building AI — And No One Knows How to Stop It

Anthropic published a landmark report this week revealing that Claude wrote more than 80% of the company's merged code in May — and that engineers are now shipping 8x as much code as they did in 2024. The lab is openly warning that recursive self-improvement may be arriving faster than anyone expected.

The report, authored by Marina Favaro and Jack Clark — Anthropic's co-founder and head of policy — is notable for both its candor and its numbers. An internal benchmark that had models rewrite training code to run faster went from roughly tripling speed last spring to a 52-fold gain by April. An unreleased model called Claude Mythos Preview reportedly found more than 10,000 serious software vulnerabilities in its first weeks, and has since been handed to the NSA.

"Each new version of Claude could be built by the version before it, without human involvement," co-author Jack Clark wrote of where the trend leads. OpenAI flagged the same loop this week in its own Democratic Governance of Frontier AI blueprint.

Anthropic's offer: it would slow or pause frontier AI development if peer labs did the same. The catch, which Anthropic itself acknowledges, is that verification is the part nobody knows how to build. Training runs hide more easily than missile silos, and the reward for cheating quietly is the lead in the most valuable technology of the age.

One data point that stands out: in 129 research moments chosen because humans had fumbled, the best model picked the better next step 64% of the time — up from 51% last November. A coin that lands a little better than a coin. The company is honest that this is no fair fight, then asks you to extrapolate from it anyway.

Screwworms Breach the US Border for First Time Since 1966

The USDA confirmed a flesh-eating screwworm larvae case in a Texas calf — the first in the state since 1966. The parasites lay hundreds of eggs in animal wounds; they had been pushed south to Panama's Darién Gap via biological barriers since the 1950s. With the US cattle herd at 75-year lows, even a small infestation could be catastrophic.

Columnist | Daily Dad

Suddenly You Don't See Them

It was overwhelming. It was exhausting. There was never a break. You had them for every second.

Your kids had every second and every inch of you. They were in your space. They were in your business. They needed something. They wanted something. Crawling over you, reaching for you, until you felt completely touched out. They wouldn't even give you a break at night — at 3:40am, they needed and wanted you.

And as exhausting and overwhelming as this was, you got used to it. So used to it, in fact, that one day it just hits you: You don't see them for hours a day now. Like, you drop them off in the morning at school and don't see them until night time. What is this? When did it happen?

But it did happen. It does happen. Even if you are a stay-at-home parent, even if you retire, even if you homeschool. Because their own life is gathering up speed, their own interests and obligations are on a collision course with yours. And this development is continuing and continuing forever in one heartbreaking direction. Soon enough, they'll move out. Soon enough, they'll move away. Soon enough, they'll have their own family and the same cycle will happen again.

So while you do see and have them — while they have you — enjoy it.

Stress Test Any Business Idea with One Perplexity Prompt

Use Perplexity Deep Research to pressure-test any business idea in 5–6 minutes. Save the prompt once and rerun it on every idea you have.

ChatGPT Now "Dreams" — Memory Overhaul Doubles Factual Recall

OpenAI launched a new memory system called "dreaming" that builds a running, category-sorted profile of each user from past conversations — replacing the old one-off fact lists.

ChatGPT now maintains summaries grouped into areas like travel, hobbies, and work, with users able to review, correct, or restrict topics. OpenAI says factual recall jumped from 41.5% to 82.8%, and preference-following climbed from 31.4% to 71.3% in internal evals.

Dreaming rolls out now to Plus and Pro users in the US, with Free tier and international expansion over the coming weeks. Sam Altman has frequently pointed to hyper-personalized AI as a core competitive moat — this is the first major step toward it.

Sports / Finance Morning Brew

A Bar Used Kalshi to Hedge Its "Free Drinks If the Knicks Win" Promotion

For NBA Finals Game 1, a Manhattan bar offered to cover tabs up to $100/person if the Knicks won — and used Kalshi to hedge the risk. The Knicks won.

Owner Andrew Freedman told Semafor he placed $5,000 on the Knicks at odds that would return $8,000 — covering roughly half the estimated bar tabs if they won. Kalshi is reportedly in conversations with other businesses to expand this kind of promotion-hedging play, as it faces legislative scrutiny over its similarity to sports betting in certain states. If every bar starts doing this, the competitive advantage disappears.
