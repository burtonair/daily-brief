Gen Z Is Reshaping the $150B Pet Industry — With Cats, Reptiles, and Radical Devotion

Gen Z households owning pets jumped 43.5% in a single year. They’re more likely to celebrate pet birthdays, consider pets equal to children, and — in one survey — sell an organ to save one. Today’s Morning Brew is a special “Pet Brew” issue.

According to a 2025 survey by the American Pet Products Association, 18.8 million US Gen Z households owned a pet in 2024 — a 43.5% jump from 2023. Nearly half of 500 Gen Z respondents said they see no difference between a pet and a human child, and 18% said they would sell an organ to save their pet’s life.

The generational shift is clearest in what they’re choosing: dogs are stabilizing, cats are surging. Following the pandemic, cat adoptions rose 15% among people under 30. Rover CEO Brent Turner says it’s partly economic — cats don’t need walks, don’t cause noise complaints, and cost less to care for. This year, Rover acquired Meowtel, a US-based cat-sitting service, signaling the company sees a feline future.

Gen Z also makes up one-third of all reptile owners — a 27% increase in their share since 2023. The generation that made plant parenthood aspirational is now applying the same devotion to cold-blooded creatures.

Debate Morning Brew

Should Offices Be Dog-Friendly? The Brew Argues Both Sides

Dogs have worked alongside humans for 15,000 years — on farms, battlefields, in coal mines, and polar expeditions. Whether they belong in a cubicle farm is a harder sell.

The case for: Research suggests office dogs improve employee engagement and workplace rapport. Dogs are the only beings genuinely excited to spend eight hours under fluorescent lighting. Their positive energy is contagious — and one could argue opposing dogs in the office is bad for the economy.

The case against: Even the best-behaved office dog is a tripping hazard, an allergen, and a guaranteed distraction. Studies show dog owners who bring pets in are less productive, and their dog-free colleagues at least feel less productive with a pup around. Also: someone always leaves peanut butter M&Ms on their desk, and those are toxic to dogs.

Consumer Trends Morning Brew

Jumping Spiders, Isopods, and Roaches: Inside the Unlikely Bug Pet Boom

One jumping spider seller went from three or four a week to thousands. Rare isopods fetch hundreds of dollars. The demand is now real enough to drive poaching in protected areas.

Hard sales data is difficult to come by — much of the trade happens in online auctions, via DMs, and on gray markets — but hobbyists and sellers say business is booming. Jumping spiders are the personality hire of the bug world: charismatic, expressive, and built for social media. One spider-centric account has 1.6 million followers.

Isopods — roly-polies, pill bugs, wood lice — have become collector’s items, with rare colorful or spiky varieties commanding serious prices. The demand for exotic specimens has even led to poaching in protected natural areas. Beetles, mantises, roaches, and caterpillars round out a market that didn’t meaningfully exist five years ago.

Entertainment Morning Brew

Westminster Dog Show: 147 Years, Seven Groups, One Best in Show — Now Coming to Netflix

The Westminster Kennel Club Dog Show has run almost uninterrupted since 1877. Starting in 2027, it streams on Netflix after a decade on Fox Sports. Over 2,500 dogs compete across seven breed groups in late January or February at Madison Square Garden.

Dogs first compete at the breed level, then advance to one of seven groups: Hound, Toy, Non-Sporting, Herding, Sporting, Working, and Terrier. The Terrier group has won Best in Show 47 times — the most of any group by a wide margin. Netflix’s acquisition reflects its broader live-events strategy alongside NFL games and boxing.
