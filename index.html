Iran Strikes Dubai Airport as UAE's Safety Bubble Bursts

An Iranian drone struck Dubai International Airport, causing a fire and halting flights for hours at the world's busiest international hub — shaking the city-state's reputation as a conflict-proof haven for the global wealthy. Morning Brew

Since the war in Iran began, Dubai has tried to project an image of business as usual — influencers sipping gold-flaked cocktails even as drone attack videos circulated online. That facade cracked significantly when an Iranian drone struck Dubai International Airport on Monday, sparking a fire. No injuries were reported, but flights were paused for hours at what is, in peacetime, the world's busiest airport for international travel, connecting 291 cities and serving 95 million passengers last year.

Iranian attacks have now killed four people in the UAE as Iran retaliates against the US-Israel campaign of strikes throughout the region. Shrapnel has damaged several luxury landmarks in Dubai. Emirates, the UAE's flagship carrier famous for in-flight extravagance, has already cut US-bound Airbus A380 flights by 51% in March. Passengers have posted videos of eerily empty planes and photos of Dubai's usually bustling beaches and markets looking like ghost towns.

The deeper worry is capital flight. Lawyers and asset managers are telling the Wall Street Journal that wealthy clients have begun inquiring about moving money out of Dubai. Urbanist Richard Florida argued in the New York Times that Dubai is particularly vulnerable because nearly nine in ten residents lack citizenship status — meaning little sense of rootedness in a crisis. The city has over 80,000 millionaires who can pack their bags at a moment's notice.

UAE authorities have banned posting photos of damage from Iranian attacks, and the country's president visited a luxury mall in the conflict's early days in an attempt to project normalcy.

How To Use Grok for Free Automated Research

If you have a free X.com account, you get 2 automated tasks each day via Grok. Use them to build automated daily research briefings pulled from live X data — no paid subscription required.

Step-by-Step

  1. Go to grok.com and sign in with your X account. Click on your profile picture in the bottom left, then click Tasks in the pop-up.
  2. Click New Task, give it a name, set your schedule (daily, weekly, or specific days), and enter your prompt: "Search X for the top trends in [your niche] from the last 24 hours. Summarize the top 3 and flag anything gaining traction."
  3. Once you save the task, Grok will run the search on schedule and send you the results via email and as a push notification in the mobile app.
  4. To view run results on desktop, navigate back to grok.com/tasks and click on the task.

Pro tip: In addition to your two daily tasks, you can stretch your free Grok account further by scheduling up to 10 research tasks per week or per month — giving you a powerful, zero-cost intelligence pipeline.

Your Pokémon Go Data Is Now Training Food Delivery Robots Morning Brew

The 2020 Pokémon Go "Field Research" feature incentivized players to scan real-world landmarks for in-game rewards. Niantic only announced its plan to build a navigation model from this data in 2024 — meaning millions of players contributed without knowing it would power commercial robotics.
