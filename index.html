Pentagon Seeks $200 Billion for Iran War as Trump Weighs Boots on the Ground

The Department of Defense asked the White House for $200 billion to fund the war, while the administration is actively considering deploying thousands of additional troops to the Middle East. Morning Brew 1% Better

The U.S. military campaign against Iran is now in its fourth week, with over 7,800 strikes conducted since operations began February 28, destroying more than 120 Iranian vessels. Thirteen American troops have been killed and roughly 200 wounded. At a press conference, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said, “It takes money to kill bad guys” and “that number could move.”

Options on the table include securing the Strait of Hormuz and deploying forces to Kharg Island, which handles 90% of Iran’s oil exports. A 2,000-Marine expeditionary unit arrives next week, though the USS Gerald R. Ford’s removal for maintenance reduces current U.S. naval capacity in the region. No ground deployment decision has been finalized.

The request needs Congressional approval, where its fate is unclear given bipartisan unease with the scope of the operation. The war had already cost about $12 billion as of Sunday — roughly $1 billion per day. Meanwhile, stocks slid for a second day before rallying late when Israeli PM Netanyahu suggested the war could end faster than people think.

How Will You Choose To Look At This?

There’s so much we don’t control as parents. We don’t control what’s happening in the world. We don’t control what our kids are going through. We don’t even really control what they do — not as much as we’d like to think anyway. But the one thing we do control, the Stoics would say, is how we choose to look at things. We choose what handle we grab in each situation, the story we tell ourselves about it.

You walk into a room you just cleaned up and find it littered, once again, with toys. This can drive you nuts, or you can choose to see it as what it also is — well played in, a room performing its function, a function you will one day miss. You can fight like crazy with your teenager over every little thing. Or you can step back and realize this is an important part of their development, where they figure life out, where they rebel against authority.

You can look at the events of the world as maddening and unnecessary and dangerous — which of course they are. You can also understand that within this adversity comes an opportunity for luctor et emergo — that by struggling against this, both you and your children can emerge stronger and better for it.

We choose what we see. That’s what we control. But it’s worth pointing out that while we can’t force our kids or our spouses to follow suit, if we live this way long enough, if we model it long enough, maybe they’ll pick it up too.

How To Use Replit’s Tasks Feature to Improve Your Site Faster

Learn how to use Replit’s Tasks feature to improve an existing site without breaking your already working app, keeping isolated fixes from piling on top of each other.

Step-by-Step

  1. Go to Replit, drop in your app idea, and hit Plan. If you already have an app, open that instead. You’ll need at least a Replit Core plan.
  2. Click the plus button on the left, create a task for a fix like mobile optimization, and prompt: “Make dashboard and all components mobile responsive. Use different components for mobile if not possible.”
  3. While it’s planning, queue a second task in parallel — try improving the landing page design, cleaning up the nav bar, or fixing a bug.
  4. Once task planning is done, start them. Preview each task, and when they finish, click Apply Changes to Main Version to update the production app.

Pro tip: Toggle your main agent into plan mode and tell it the improvements you want. It will generate a PRD. Then divvy up that PRD into new parallel tasks.

Scrolling Makes English-Speaking Countries Sadder, World Happiness Report Finds Morning Brew

Finland, Denmark, and Iceland topped the World Happiness Report ranking of 140 countries, while English-speaking nations continued to fall — a decline researchers attribute in part to heavy social media use.

The US (No. 23), UK (No. 29), Canada (No. 25), Australia (No. 15), and New Zealand (No. 11) each failed to make the Top 10 for the second year in a row. Young people in English-speaking countries and Western Europe saw the biggest drops in happiness.

Studies cited in the report suggest platforms featuring updates from people you know — like WhatsApp and Facebook — can improve well-being, while algorithmic apps like Instagram, TikTok, and X are detrimental to mental health. Some point to Finland’s national obsession with the simple pleasures of the outdoors as the real secret.

Tech & AI

Cursor Ships In-House Coding Model That Rivals Frontier at 1/10th the Cost Rundown AI

Anysphere, the company behind Cursor, shipped Composer 2, a third-generation in-house model competitive with frontier coding models from OpenAI and Anthropic at a fraction of the cost.

Composer 2 topped Opus 4.6 on the independent Terminal-Bench 2.0 (61.7% vs 58%) and sits within 5 points of GPT-5.4 on Cursor’s own CursorBench. At $7.50/M output tokens on its fast tier, it costs roughly 1/10th of GPT-5.4 and 1/20th of Opus 4.6 at comparable speeds.

Scores on CursorBench have climbed from 38% to 61.3% across three model generations since October. Nearing the frontier as an application-layer company is an impressive feat that could change the math for developers paying full price for coding with top models.
