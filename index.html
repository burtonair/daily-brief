LIRR Strike Snarls 270,000 NYC Commuters as Talks Stall

Morning Brew

The Long Island Railroad’s first strike since 1994 is now into its third day, with no new negotiations scheduled and quarter of a million commuters scrambling for alternatives.

The dispute pits five LIRR unions against the Metropolitan Transportation Authority over wages frozen since 2022. Workers are seeking a 9.5% retroactive raise covering 2023–2025 — which the MTA agreed to — but the sticking point is a 5% raise for 2026. The MTA countered with 3% plus a lump-sum payment, which was rejected. Average cash compensation for affected workers in 2025 was reportedly $136,000.

"The raises weren't really raises, when you factor in inflation," said Steve Ammirati of the Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers and Trainmen. The MTA also sought to abolish work rules that result in higher pay — for example, workers switching from a diesel to an electric train in the same day currently receive two days’ pay.

New York Gov. Kathy Hochul warned striking workers that three days on the picket line would erase any gains from a new contract. Full coverage from the NY Times.

The AI Chip Boom Has Two Clocks — and They’re Not Synchronized

1% Better

Cerebras Systems went public at $185/share — $66B valuation, 20x oversubscribed, largest US tech IPO since Uber. The IPO market says the AI infrastructure window is wide open. The chip suppliers say something different.

SK Hynix posted a 72% operating margin in Q1 and has reportedly sold its entire 2026 HBM output already, signing 3–5 year supply agreements with Microsoft, Google, and Amazon requiring advance payment. Yet capex grew only ~17% year-over-year — vs. the 50%+ increase during the 2017–2018 boom that preceded the 2019 memory bust. Micron says it can fill only 55–60% of core customer demand.

TSMC guided 2026 capex to a record $52–$56B. SK Hynix’s new mega-fab doesn’t start production until early 2027. Samsung’s P5 is 2028. Intel CEO Lip-Bu Tan: “no relief until 2028.”

The capital cycle is pricing multi-year compute demand at any valuation the market will bear. The manufacturing clock is monetizing discipline, letting new wafers arrive in 2028 — after the demand signal has been pressure-tested. “Strategically managed scarcity,” as one analyst put it. Watch for which clock breaks first.

Artist Tricks X Users Into Trashing a Real Monet as “AI Slop”

The Rundown AI

Conceptual artist SHL0MS posted a genuine Monet from the Water Lilies collection (c. 1915) and told followers he’d generated it with AI, asking them to explain why it was inferior. Thousands obliged.

Critics called the image “emotionless,” “slop,” and picked apart its depth, reflections, and composition — all of which are, of course, authentic Monet. The original post drew thousands of confident critiques before the reveal.

The saga aligns with 2024 Norwegian research finding that people actually prefer AI art but show a clear negative bias against it once they know it’s AI-generated. For a growing crowd, the word “AI” alone triggers backlash regardless of context.

ChatGPT Connects to Your Money via Plaid

The Rundown AI

OpenAI launched a personal finance experience inside ChatGPT, giving the chatbot real-time access to your spending, investments, and bills across 12,000+ institutions.

Users connect via Plaid across Chase, Schwab, Robinhood, and more, with a dashboard tracking spending, portfolio, and upcoming bills.
