Tech & Business Morning Brew

Dell Surges 32% as AI Servers Officially Eclipse Its PC Business

Dell’s fiscal first-quarter results obliterated Wall Street expectations, with AI server revenue hitting $16.1 billion — outpacing PC sales for the first time. A same-day $9.7 billion Pentagon software contract sent shares to a single-day record.

The company reported an 88% year-over-year increase in fiscal Q1 sales, rising to $43.8 billion against analyst expectations of $35.5 billion. Dell’s share price has now climbed over 225% this year, and the company raised its fiscal 2027 full-year revenue estimate to $167 billion — up from $140 billion — with $60 billion projected from AI server sales alone.

The AI server boom is being driven by cloud infrastructure companies like CoreWeave and Nscale that rent computing power to run AI programs and have been buying Dell’s hardware at a rapid pace. Meanwhile, the S&P 500 closed out its ninth straight winning week — its best streak since 2023 — while software stocks logged their best month since 2001 as fears that AI would destroy the sector failed to materialize.

The Pentagon deal and CEO Michael Dell’s proximity to the Trump administration drew scrutiny from government watchdogs who questioned whether Trump’s personal Dell stock purchases — made before publicly saying “go out and buy a Dell” — represent a conflict of interest on a federal contract.

Let It Go — And Cast a Wide Net

This week’s passage: “You’ll be kicking yourself for being such a hard-ass, for making something out of nothing. You’ll wish you’d let it go.”

Ryan Holiday’s weekly read, “Is This Really How You Want Tonight to Go?” is a gut-check that lands every time: before you dig in on something with your kid, ask yourself how you’re going to feel about it in an hour. Most of the time, you already know the answer.

The YouTube takeaway this week was a reminder worth posting on the fridge: “There’s always a reason they are behaving this way.” Not an excuse — a reason. Kids aren’t acting out for sport. Something is going on. The work is figuring out what.

On the podcast this week: helping kids find their thing. The insight worth holding onto: it won’t happen where you expect. Which is why we cast a wide net — try things, go places, say yes more than feels comfortable. You never know where it lands.

This week’s reading pick: Kafka’s Letter to the Father. The line Holiday flagged: “I know it is my father’s first time on this Earth, too. And I know he had it worse when he was little. But I was little, too.”

Politics & Law Morning Brew

Judge Orders Trump’s Name Off the Kennedy Center — Only Congress Can Rename It

A federal judge ruled President Trump lacks the authority to rename the Kennedy Center and temporarily blocked the administration’s plan to close the theater for two years of renovations.

The ruling is the latest judicial check on executive overreach, finding that only Congress can change the venue’s name. The closure plan — which would have shuttered one of Washington’s premier performance venues through renovations — is also on hold pending further proceedings.

Auto & Trade Morning Brew

Mercedes-Benz Could Be Banned From the US Under a Bill Targeting Chinese Cars

The bipartisan Motor Vehicle Modernization Act of 2026 would bar vehicles with any indirect equity interest held by a foreign adversary government — and Mercedes’s largest shareholder is China’s state-owned BAIC.

The legislation’s language is sweeping enough to snare European brands operating with Chinese capital ties, even though the law’s stated target is Chinese EVs. If it passes in its current form, Americans may need to rethink their German luxury choices.

Media & Music Morning Brew

Universal Music Group Rejects Bill Ackman’s $64 Billion Buyout Bid

UMG’s board turned away the hedge fund manager and power-tweeter’s proposal, saying it undervalued the world’s largest music catalog.

Ackman’s strategy had been to build a portfolio of IP-driven media assets, and UMG was the crown jewel. The board’s rejection leaves him without a path to the music business’s most durable revenue stream — the kind of catalog that generates royalties regardless of which streaming platform is winning.

Markets & Crime Morning Brew

Google Engineer Charged With Insider Trading on Polymarket — Made $1.2M

Michele Spagnuolo, a Google info-security engineer known as “AlphaRaccoon,” allegedly used confidential internal search data to bet that singer D4vd would be the most-searched person of 2025 — and won big.

Prosecutors say Spagnuolo made $1.2 million on Polymarket wagers using data he had no authorization to trade on. The case joins a growing string of prediction-market insider trading allegations, raising questions about whether platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket have created regulatory blind spots that securities law wasn’t built to cover.
