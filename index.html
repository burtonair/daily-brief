Apple Sues OpenAI for Systematic Trade Secret Theft

Apple filed a federal lawsuit against OpenAI, its chief hardware officer Tang Tan, and device startup io Products, alleging a coordinated campaign to steal proprietary hardware secrets through the hiring of 400+ former Apple employees — a legal attack that lands just as OpenAI marches toward one of the largest IPOs in history.

The lawsuit filed in federal court Friday alleged that “every level” of OpenAI was involved. At the center of the case: Tang Tan, a former Apple VP who spent 24 years at the company before becoming OpenAI’s chief hardware officer. Apple alleges Tan ran job interviews where candidates were told to come with “actual parts” — hardware components they were not authorized to remove.

Former iPhone engineer Chang Liu is named as a co-defendant. Apple says Liu exploited a bug to access confidential systems after joining OpenAI, texting a colleague: “LOL, I found out I can access the [network storage], so funny” — then downloading presentations and manufacturing details. Apple also alleges OpenAI coached departing employees to delay their last day so they could continue accessing internal systems as a final harvest of secrets.

Apple is seeking damages and wants OpenAI to destroy all proprietary materials, calling its hardware business “rotten to its core.” OpenAI denied all charges, saying it has “no interest in other companies’ trade secrets.” Elon Musk celebrated by calling Sam Altman “Scam Altman” on X; Altman responded by calling Musk “homeboy.”

The timing is brutal: OpenAI filed its draft S-1 with the SEC on June 8th. Simultaneously, the New York Times and co-plaintiffs asked a court to sanction OpenAI for allegedly destroying evidence in their separate copyright case. Two lawsuits, one accusation — that OpenAI’s foundations belong to someone else. Every new filing gives institutional investors another reason to wait out the IPO.

Columnist Daily Dad

Each Time Is Closer to the Last Time

Each night, you have to fight them to get to bed. Each afternoon, you have to fight them about homework. Each winter, it’s three or four trips to the doctor. Each summer, it’s complaints about being bored. Each morning, you’re dragging them out of bed.

It’s arguments. It’s reminders. It’s picking up messes. It’s getting out your wallet. It’s raising your voice. It’s throwing up your hands. But each time, we cannot forget, is closer to the last time.

And this matters not because it means they won’t behave this way forever, but because it means it won’t be this way forever. They are growing up. They are moving on. Children are a ticking clock. They are on their way to not being children.

So as you get frustrated or annoyed or tired, remind yourself that you are inching closer to a thing that’s going to make you terribly sad one day. You will, in fact, miss this. You will kick yourself for seeing these obligations as obligations and not as a fleeting, wonderful period of time that does not last. Each step is closer to the last time.

Go from Idea to Website with ChatGPT Work + Codex

OpenAI’s new ChatGPT Work and Codex apps (powered by GPT-5.6 models) let you turn a website idea into a working prototype using sub-agents for both research and code-writing. Here’s the step-by-step:

  1. Update the ChatGPT desktop app. When ChatGPT Work appears top-left, toggle between Work and Codex modes.
  2. Create a project folder and start a task there. Give ChatGPT your directory idea, ask it to plan the Astro.js build, then save a one-page PRD in the folder.
  3. When the PRD is ready, ask Work to task sub-agents with researching directory items. Open a new thread, switch to Codex, point it at the PRD, and ask it to build with sub-agents.
  4. Open the preview and ask Codex to fix the biggest visible issue. Works for any directory concept.

Apple’s Lawsuit Is a Precision Strike on OpenAI’s IPO

The suit won’t stop OpenAI’s Jony Ive-designed hardware device — trade-secret injunctions against unshipped products are hard to win — but it imposes a carefully calculated cost on the company’s path to public markets.

A named defendant in the C-suite. OpenAI’s chief hardware officer Tang Tan is personally named. Underwriters and institutional buyers doing pre-IPO diligence now have to price litigation reaching the company’s leadership.

A discovery window. Apple gets subpoena power over OpenAI’s secretive hardware program — the one part of the company it has worked hardest to keep dark. Discovery runs on the court’s calendar, not the roadshow’s.

A disclosure line. Material litigation goes in the S-1’s risk factors. Apple’s complaint writes the worst version of it: that OpenAI’s hardware business is “rotten to its core.” Meanwhile, the NYT is separately alleging OpenAI destroyed or hid evidence in their copyright case — both surfacing during the same bookbuild. OpenAI’s own finance chief has reportedly told people a 2027 listing is more realistic than 2026. Every new filing adds another reason to agree.

Economy Morning Brew

Funflation: Now Staying Home Costs More Too

After years of concert and event prices soaring, the cost of entertaining yourself at home is climbing — gaming, streaming, home fitness, and electricity are all getting more expensive simultaneously.

Gaming. Consoles used to get cheaper after launch. Tariffs and AI-driven demand for memory chips have kept hardware prices elevated, bucking decades of precedent.

Streaming. Netflix, Prime Video, Spotify, Crunchyroll, Paramount+, and YouTube Premium have all raised prices recently. The bundle that was supposed to save you money now costs more than cable.

Home fitness. Peloton, Jazzercise On Demand, and Pvolve raised digital subscription prices in the past year.

Electricity. US prices expected to break a record high this summer. The one silver lining: reading books is making a comeback, and it’s still cheap.
