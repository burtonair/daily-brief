Geopolitics

US strikes Iran after ceasefire violation in Strait of Hormuz Brew

The United States struck Iranian missile and drone storage locations and coastal radar sites after President Trump accused Iran of violating their ceasefire by attacking commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz.

US Central Command confirmed the strikes Friday, saying forces hit "Iranian missile and drone storage locations and coastal radar sites." The action came after Trump posted on Truth Social that Iran had attacked ships in the Strait of Hormuz with drones, one of which struck a commercial cargo ship. "Obviously, this is a foolish violation of our Ceasefire Agreement," Trump wrote.

The Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of the world's oil passes — is one of the most contested issues in ongoing US-Iran peace negotiations. The status of the strait and broader shipping access are among the many unresolved points in talks toward a final peace deal. The strike signals Washington's willingness to use force even as diplomacy continues.

Separately, Israel and Lebanon have reached a framework for a peace deal, officials said Friday — though Iran-backed Hezbollah was not party to the agreement, a significant caveat that clouds its durability. The back-to-back developments in the region underscore how fragile the broader Middle East security architecture remains.

Politics & Law Brew

John Bolton pleads guilty to mishandling classified information

Trump’s former National Security Advisor — and one of his sharpest critics — entered a guilty plea Friday on charges of mishandling classified material.

Bolton served as Trump’s NSA from 2018 to 2019 before becoming a prominent critic, writing a memoir in 2020 that the administration attempted to block on national security grounds. The guilty plea brings a long legal saga to a close, though it also marks one of the more notable prosecutions of a former senior national security official in recent memory.

World Brew

Venezuela earthquake death toll reaches 920 — more than 51,000 still missing

Rescue teams continued to search for survivors Friday as the scale of devastation from two powerful earthquakes became clearer across Venezuela.

The confirmed death toll rose to at least 920 with more than 51,000 people still unaccounted for — a number that points to the catastrophic displacement caused by the back-to-back quakes. Earlier USGS predictive modeling warned the true toll could far exceed official figures given Venezuela’s vulnerable building stock. The US pledged $150M in emergency aid.

Education & Politics Brew

Texas mandates Bible passages in public school reading lists

The Texas State Board of Education approved a required reading list for public schools that includes passages from the Bible, drawing immediate criticism from civil liberties advocates.

Critics say the list favors Christian interpretations despite the religious diversity of Texas’s 5.4 million public school students. The move reflects a broader national pattern of states pushing religious content into public school curricula, and comes in the context of Supreme Court rulings that have expanded the scope for religion in public spaces.

Culture Brew

Hacky sack is back — and there’s already a supply shortage

The humble footbag has staged a full-blown viral comeback, driven by teens in the Northeast posting videos and rankings on social media and causing genuine supply chain disruptions for manufacturers.

TikTok searches for hacky sack jumped 7,000% year-to-date, while Google searches spiked more than 5,000% in a single month. Footbag manufacturer Bomb Footbags tripled the price of one model from $13 to $34 — and it still sold out. Some stores are limiting purchases per customer. Parents and teachers are broadly thrilled: it gets kids off screens and socializing outside. For the record: “Hacky Sack” is a brand name; the correct term is “footbag,” which is the most uncool correction in sports history.
