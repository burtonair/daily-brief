Economy — Breaking

A New Era for the Fed: Warsh Replaces Powell Today

Morning Brew

By the slimmest margin in history, the Senate confirmed Kevin Warsh as the 17th chair of the Federal Reserve, ending Jerome Powell's eight-year run — a tenure that spanned Covid stimulus, peak inflation, and relentless executive pressure.

Powell leaves with inflation well above his long-stated 2% goal — currently at 3.8% — but his tenure was like no other: he raised rates four times in his first year, then deployed every tool available to avoid an extended Covid recession. By end of 2021, unemployment fell back under 4% after peaking near 14.8% in 2020. Critics say the Fed was slow to react to post-pandemic spending, kicking off the worst inflation in decades. Powell deemed it "transitory" — a call that did not age well.

Warsh has said he favors rate cuts, but getting a majority of the Fed's 12 voters to agree is a tall order — FedWatch has not forecast a likelihood of cuts higher than 2.8% for the rest of the year. Like Alan Greenspan before him, Warsh believes the AI productivity explosion could be the foundation for future rate cuts without triggering inflation.

Trump originally wanted Warsh when he first took office and later called tapping Powell a "really big mistake." Warsh is officially the new chair as of today.

Columnist Daily Dad Daily Dad

What's the Secret to Your Hardest Job?

It doesn't matter if you're the president, a doctor, or a professional athlete — they all agree that the hardest job in the world is being a parent. Because it's not just a job. It's the job you have to do on top of your other jobs. The one you can't quit, get promoted in, or retire from. The responsibilities are never clearly defined and always seem to be changing. There's no training. No paycheck either.

So how do we do it well? Epictetus was once approached by a student struggling with this exact problem. Tell me what to do, the student said. Epictetus refused. That wasn't even the right request. It would be far better, he said, to ask a teacher to "make me adaptable to any circumstance." The reason: "There may be events that call for you to act differently… So hold on to general principles and you won't need specific advice."

What are your general principles? What's your framework? Your kid gets in trouble. You lose your job and have to relocate the family. She has her heart broken and doesn't want to talk about it. Your spouse picks a pointless fight. What do you do in these situations? How do you respond?

This is what parenting is. Like rafting a river, it is navigating a seemingly unending, ever-changing flow of complicated circumstances — dilemma after dilemma, new situation after new situation, trying not to let the boat flip. You must train to be able to respond. You must be ready for it all.

Automate Marketing Assets with ChatGPT Images 2.0

Use Codex Desktop to build a ChatGPT Images 2.0 app that turns a marketing campaign and design brief into prompts, test images, and a review gallery — end to end.

  1. Create a project folder and initialize git: mkdir gpt-image-review-app && cd gpt-image-review-app && git init. Open the folder in Codex Desktop.
  2. Ask Codex to build a local browser app for GPT Image 2.0: enter a marketing brief, generate editable prompts, create test images, choose image dimensions, and review outputs with Keep / Reject / Needs Revision controls.
  3. Add your OpenAI API key to the .env file, run the app locally, and describe your campaign, design brief, and desired formats.
  4. Click "Generate Prompts," then "Generate Images to Test." Label outputs Keep, Reject, or Needs Revision — save the strongest results.

Pro tip: Once the app works, use Codex Desktop Annotation Mode to click or drag over fixes for labels, spacing, states, controls, or layout.

Business — Autos

Honda's EV Bet Backfires: First Annual Loss in 70 Years

Morning Brew 1% Better

Japan's second-largest carmaker posted a $2.7 billion annual loss — its first since listing on the Tokyo Stock Exchange in 1957 — after a $10 billion hit from EV investments that failed to deliver. CEO Toshihiro Mibe admitted the 2040 all-EV target is dead.

Honda canceled three U.S. electric models, shelved its Sony EV joint venture, and froze a Canadian factory project. Chinese sales collapsed more than 50% in five years as domestic rivals took the market. Trump's auto tariffs and axed tax incentives further squeezed margins.

Pivot to Hybrids
  • Ditched goal of 20% EV sales by 2030
  • 2040 combustion-engine phase-out target scrapped
  • Focusing on 15 new hybrid models by 2030
  • Motorcycle business (~1/3 of global market) accounted for ~half of profit, with 11% sales growth

Honda forecasts a return to $1.6 billion profit next year. The recovery plan looks a lot like Toyota's: lean into hybrids for U.S. buyers while rivals absorb EV transition risk. Motorcyclists, it turns out, are far more open to electrification than car drivers.

Martha Stewart Launches AI Home Management Platform "Hint"

Morning Brew

Stewart announced Hint, an always-on AI-native platform that helps homeowners manage the logistics of home ownership — joining a growing wave The Cut called "The Girlbossification of AI."

Hint asks for your address and pulls relevant public data: flood risk, soil composition, property history. Upload bills and documents and it'll remind you to update insurance, or advise when to replace the roof. The platform monetizes via premium features and affiliate fees for service recommendations, but claims to be "blind to commercial deals."

Stewart and co-founders (former Red Ventures exec Yih-Han Ma and AI engineer Kyle Rush) raised $10 million in seed funding led by Slow Ventures. The app goes live this summer. VCs are eyeing the $500 billion residential renovation and repair market.

Anthropic's New Agent Credit Split Triggers Dev Backlash

The Rundown AI

Anthropic announced a policy splitting agent usage into a separate monthly credit pool — restoring third-party agentic tool support but drawing immediate fire from power users who called the new limits insulting.

Starting June 15, the Agent SDK and claude -p will draw solely from new agentic credit pools — separate from subscription limits for Claude Code, Cowork, and chat. Pro users get $20/month; Max 5x gets $100; Max 20x gets $200. Credits reset monthly and do not roll over.

The move reverses Anthropic's April ban on third-party agents like OpenClaw, but removes the implicit compute subsidy. T3 founder Theo Browne and hundreds of others publicly canceled their subscriptions — all while OpenAI keeps raising Codex limits to attract switchers.
