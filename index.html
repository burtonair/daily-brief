Columnist Daily Dad

It’s Supposed To Be Difficult

We understand that sports are supposed to tire them out. We understand that math is supposed to be hard. We understand that learning a new language takes time and focus. But then, for some reason, we shelter our kids when it comes to the world of ideas—when it comes to history, literature, even movies.

We pick what’s easy, what we think they’ll understand. We go with what they like. We keep them away from things that might be inappropriate or offensive or morbid. Author George Saunders explained how a teacher was teaching Ambrose Bierce’s dark, beautifully written Civil War nonfiction to fifth-graders—and some parents objected it was too hard. They got rid of it. Nobody was saying that calculus was too difficult.

Ideas and discussions are not always going to be comfortable. Just like an equation, we have to work at them. Books are supposed to challenge our thinking, art is supposed to challenge our perspective. We are not helping them when we decide that real and true things are too hard for our kids to handle.

As the Daily Dad puts it: Don’t baby your kids when it comes to books and ideas. Teach them how to be mature enough to understand them, to learn from them, to grow from them.

The Rundown AI How To — AI Training

Build a YouTube Research Bot in 15 Minutes

Learn how to build a Gumloop agent that tracks YouTube channels or search topics, reads transcripts, and turns the useful videos into a ranked research brief.

  1. Go to Gumloop agent builder, create an agent named YouTube Scout, and enable YouTube and Google Sheets under “Apps.”
  2. Prompt: “Build me a YouTube scout for (niche). Check (channels/queries), find videos from the last (hours/days), read the transcript, and return a brief with title, link, 3–5 takeaways, why it matters, follow-up ideas, usefulness score, and a ‘what changed’ summary. Track topics and videos in a Google Sheet.”
  3. Start small: one niche, a few trusted channels, one or two searches, and a 24–48 hour lookback window. The tighter the scout’s beat, the better the brief.
  4. Run the agent, then review the Sheet it creates. Make sure each result has a source link, concrete takeaways, and a usefulness score.

Pro tip: Dial in the signal score early. If the agent calls a mediocre video an 8, tell it why that should be a 5. You can also add a User Signal Score column for future runs.

Real Estate Morning Brew

Spring Housing Market Stalls Despite Lower Mortgage Rates

Sales of existing homes rose just 0.2% in April—far below the 3% economists expected—as buyers remain sidelined by high prices and economic uncertainty.

The national median existing-home price hit an all-time April record of $417,700, according to the NAR. There were 1.47 million unsold homes on the market, the highest for April since 2019, but still far below the pre-pandemic average of 2 million.

The war in Iran pushed mortgage rates back above 6% and triggered inflation concerns. Agents told the WSJ that buyers are also put off by a frozen job market and steep prices. NAR chief economist Lawrence Yun says a 30% increase in inventory would help balance the market.

Looking ahead: If the current 6.37% mortgage rate falls back below 6%, sales will rise. Above 6.5% could scare away even more buyers.

Law Morning Brew

The Trial That Could Upend Short Selling

Legendary short seller Andrew Left faces fraud charges that could set a major precedent for activist short sellers who publish damaging research and profit from stock drops.

Federal prosecutors claim Left made at least $16 million by weaponizing his massive media reach to manipulate markets with dishonest commentary about stocks like Nvidia and Tesla. He’s accused of cashing in on short-term swings caused by viral posts while misleading investors about his exposure.

Left’s defense will likely argue his stock opinions are protected by the First Amendment. A guilty verdict could send him to prison for up to 25 years and undermine the bread and butter of activist short sellers.

Google Confirms First AI-Written Zero-Day Exploit

Google’s Threat Intelligence Group confirmed the first known case of hackers using AI to discover and write a zero-day security flaw.

The hack targeted two-factor authentication on a widely-used web management tool. Google caught them before they could break past login protections. Clues included unusually polished attack code, long explainer notes, and a made-up severity score.

GTIG’s John Hultquist called the find “the tip of the iceberg,” with Anthropic’s Rob Bair warning cybersecurity defenders’ lead is “months, not years.”

Scientists Built a Hantavirus Cure—Then Funding Ran Out

A hantavirus antibody that protected hamsters from the exact strain killing cruise-ship passengers was never tested in humans because federal funding dried up in 2024.

Three people are dead and several sick. The treatment, developed by researchers at Albert Einstein College of Medicine and the U.S. Army, works by blocking the virus from entering cells. Even if greenlit today, manufacturing enough to help the current outbreak would not be feasible.

Moderna is also running early-stage vaccine research through partnerships with the U.S. Army and Korea University, but neither program has reached human trials.
