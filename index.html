National Parks Morning Brew

The Hidden Price Tag of America’s National Parks

The National Park Service manages more than 85 million acres of public land on a budget of $2.88 billion — down sharply from 2023 — while facing $23 billion in deferred maintenance and a decade of staffing cuts that have left parks overwhelmed and visitors running wild.

In January, Congress approved $2.88 billion to operate the NPS through October — down from $3.48 billion in 2023. Last May, the Trump administration proposed a more than $1 billion cut, but public pushback forced a retreat. The damage from reduced staffing, exacerbated by 25% cuts last year, is already visible: in Yosemite, too few rangers mean visitors are flying drones, climbing without permits, and throwing trash wherever they want.

The deferred maintenance backlog reached $23 billion at end of 2024 — covering broken restrooms, deteriorating trails, and crumbling infrastructure across more than 400 sites. Last summer, some parks without enough staff to collect fees resorted to QR codes and an honor system at the gates.

The economic case for funding parks is massive: in 2024 they helped generate $56.3 billion for the US economy and supported 340,100 jobs, with a huge chunk flowing to small gateway towns that depend almost entirely on park visitors. The National Park Foundation donated $83.8 million last year, but the gap between what parks produce and what they receive keeps widening.

Safety Morning Brew

Overcrowded Trails Are Turning Deadly

At Angel’s Landing in Zion, at least 17 people have died from falls on the cable-assisted summit section. Several more have died on Yosemite’s Half Dome cables. When crowds pack the trail, holding the cables becomes dangerous: “When there’s a lot of people, it can be so dangerous,” mountaineer Sherin Elmeleegy told KSNV Las Vegas. Traffic also prolongs heat exposure and traps hikers in sudden storms.

Dark Skies Morning Brew

18 Parks Are Now Official Dark Sky Sanctuaries

DarkSky International has certified 18 national parks as Dark Sky Places, including the Grand Canyon, Death Valley, Joshua Tree, and Zion. The Gold tier — best Milky Way views — includes Death Valley, Big Bend, Canyonlands, and Great Basin. Only 20% of North Americans can see the Milky Way from where they live.

Travel Morning Brew

Five Hidden Parks That Feel Like the Old America

As Yosemite and Zion buckle under visitor pressure, a handful of less-known parks offer genuine wilderness — often more spectacular — with a fraction of the crowds.

Gates of the Arctic, Alaska — The northernmost and second-largest US national park drew fewer than 15,000 visitors last year. Vast Arctic tundra, backcountry backpacking, river rafting, and caribou. For serious outdoorspeople only.

North Cascades, Washington — Less than three hours from Seattle, visited by only 47,000 people last year. “America’s Alps” offers alpine climbing for all levels and the glacier-blue Diablo Lake. Wildfires are a risk.

Isle Royale, Michigan — An island in Lake Superior, accessible only by boat or seaplane. The Midwest’s best-kept secret: forested seclusion and near-zero light pollution.

Dry Tortugas, Florida — An archipelago near Key West anchored by Fort Jefferson, a Civil War-era prison. Snorkel and dive over a massive coral reef once the history lesson is done.

Great Sand Dunes, Colorado — The highest-altitude desert environment in North America, with superb birdwatching and dunes that genuinely surprise first-timers.

History Morning Brew

150 Years of National Parks: The Key Dates

From Lincoln’s Yosemite grant to Fat Bear Week, the story of America’s national parks is longer and stranger than you probably remember.

1864 — President Lincoln signs the Yosemite Grant, protecting the valley for “public use, resort, and recreation.” The concept of government-protected public land is born.

1872 — The Yellowstone National Park Act creates the first national park, signed by President Grant. It goes on to inspire, eventually, multiple Taylor Sheridan series.

1916 — President Wilson establishes the National Park Service as a dedicated federal agency.

1933 — FDR consolidates 56 national monuments and military sites from the Forest Service and War Department into the NPS, massively expanding its scope.

1944 — Smokey Bear (no “the”) is created by the Forest Service. He has been warning us about forest fires ever since.

1972 — Annual park attendance reaches 165 million for the first time.

2014 — Fat Bear Tuesday debuts at Katmai National Park in Alaska, setting the stage for Fat Bear Week the following year.

2020 — New River Gorge in West Virginia becomes the 63rd — and most recently designated — US national park.

Park Economics

Morning Brew
$56.3B Economic Output — On a $2.88B Budget

National parks generated $56.3 billion for the US economy in 2024 and supported 340,100 jobs. A large share flowed to small gateway towns entirely dependent on park visitors — towns that would be gutted by closures or further staffing cuts.

Morning Brew
Private Philanthropy Filling the Gap

The National Park Foundation — a nongovernmental nonprofit — donated over $83.8 million last year. Parks also take a cut of concessions revenue from private lodging and services on park land. Neither source gets close to the $23B maintenance backlog.

Morning Brew
Congress Pulled Back a $1B+ Cut — Barely

The Trump administration proposed slashing the NPS budget by more than $1 billion last May. Public backlash forced a retreat, but the final 2026 budget of $2.88B is still $600M below 2023 levels — with more visitors than ever and a decade of accumulated staffing losses to overcome.

The Crowds Problem

Morning Brew
Honor System Entrance Fees: Parks Running on Trust

Several parks without enough staff to collect entrance fees installed QR codes at gates last summer and asked visitors to pay voluntarily. The experiment speaks to a broader staffing collapse that leaves land unprotected, trails unmaintained, and rules unenforced at scale.

Morning Brew
Drones, Permit Dodgers, Littering: Yosemite Off the Rails

A skeleton ranger force has effectively lost control over visitor behavior at Yosemite. Visitors are flying drones (prohibited), climbing without required permits, and littering on trails. Post-pandemic visitor numbers surged; staffing did not follow.

Morning Brew
Trail Traffic Also Creates Heat and Storm Risk

Crowded trails turn 20-minute hikes into hours-long ordeals, prolonging exposure to high temperatures and making it harder to exit before afternoon storms hit. The danger compounds at high-altitude and cable-dependent trails where there is no safe way to pass a slow or panicking hiker.

Dark Skies & Conservation

Morning Brew
Gold-Tier Dark Sky Parks: Best Milky Way Views in the US

The clearest Milky Way and Zodiacal Light views in the country are found at Death Valley (CA), Big Bend (TX), Canyonlands (UT), and Great Basin (NV) — all designated DarkSky Gold tier. Only 20% of North Americans can see the Milky Way from their home location.

Morning Brew
Channel Islands: A Conservation Redemption Story

Sacred to indigenous Americans, then devastated as a working ranch by invasive sheep and pigs, the Channel Islands were eventually reclaimed as a national park. A New Zealand hunting firm with helicopters eradicated thousands of wild pigs, and the land came back. Reader Carol from Monument, CO calls it “a genuine success story.”

Morning Brew
How Parks Protect the Night Sky

The NPS uses glare-free, downward-pointing fixtures across park facilities and encourages red-light flashlights to preserve visitors’ night vision. Many parks host astronomy events with telescopes and ranger talks about what you’re seeing overhead.
