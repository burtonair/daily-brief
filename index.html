SpaceX Files for the Biggest IPO in History at $1.75 Trillion

Elon Musk's aerospace and AI mega-company filed to go public as soon as June, seeking to raise $75 billion at a $1.75 trillion valuation — more than double the largest IPO ever. Morning Brew Rundown AI

The current IPO record holder is Saudi Aramco, which generated $29 billion from its 2019 debut. Last year, the entire US IPO market raised $44 billion combined from 202 listings — barely more than half of SpaceX's target.

Up to 30% of IPO shares could reportedly be reserved for individual investors — triple the norm — signaling SpaceX wants hefty retail participation. A special two-tier voting structure lets Musk keep full control after going public.

SpaceX absorbed xAI last month, making this the first U.S. AI-era mega-listing — beating OpenAI and Anthropic to public markets. Proceeds would fund data centers in space, a moon base, and an "insane flight rate" for Starship. After its IPO, SpaceX can join Nasdaq's main index after just 15 days thanks to a rule change effective May 1.

Columnist Daily Dad

They Don't Know What's Wrong

Why are they acting this way? Why are they hitting their sister? Why are they whining? Why are their grades dropping? Why are they being so difficult? The answer is that they don't know. They have no idea why. They are kids. This is all new to them.

As Aeschylus has the chorus say in The Libation Bearers: "A child in the cradle can't explain what troubles it — whether it wants to eat, to drink, or to make water. A baby's insides take no orders; it's too young."

It's not just babies, but toddlers and teenagers, too. They are dealing with new feelings, new issues, new powers, near unknowns. They can't articulate yet what this means or where it comes from — because they are still learning, as you yourself were learning at that age what it means to be hangry or depressed, to be pumped full of hormones or coming down with something.

You couldn't explain it then — but now you can. So help them. Help them see what's happening. Help them solve the problem. Help them understand themselves.

Build a Productivity Tool with Replit

Learn how to build a lightweight work tracker that counts what you actually did each day and turns it into a clean weekly report:

  1. Open Claude or ChatGPT and ask it to interview you about your role. Find the 5–8 task types you repeat most often — things like sales calls, deliverables sent, client replies, internal meetings, or content published.
  2. Take that task list into Replit and tell it: Build me a simple tracker app with daily number inputs, an optional note field, a calendar heatmap, and a report generator for any date range.
  3. Log a few fake days first so you can see the app working right away.
  4. Click Generate Report and the app outputs totals, daily averages, and a short summary — already formatted for a manager update, client recap, or team check-in.
  5. Deploy the Replit and bookmark the URL. Use it every day at the end of your workday.

Pro tip: Add a client or project tag to each entry for filtered reports by account instead of one big pile of activity.

Nike's China Slump Hammers Stock by 15% Morning Brew

Sales in China fell for the seventh consecutive quarter, and Nike expects them to remain weak for the rest of the year — even as earnings and revenue beat expectations.

CEO Elliott Hill was brought on in 2024 to refocus the brand on sports. North America revenue grew for a second straight quarter, but overseas problems persist. In China, consumers are pulling back as they look for bargains during tough economic times — but other foreign brands aren't being hit as hard.

Hill vented in an all-hands: "I'm so tired, and I know you are too, of talking about fixing this business." Nike also cited the Middle East conflict and US tariffs as headwinds.

Education & Money

Is Grad School Worth It? New Study Says It Depends — A Lot Morning Brew

Graduate degrees in medicine and law have high ROI, but degrees in social work and psychology can yield negative returns, according to a study of 800,000 students.

Researchers at American University examined income before and after grad school for students at Texas public universities (1992–2019), factoring in program costs and foregone earnings. Pharmacy doctorates saw 68% adjusted returns; clinical psychology saw -5%. Doctors earned 173% returns, lawyers 41%, and MBAs 13%.

Foundayo becomes the second GLP-1 pill on the market after Novo Nordisk's Wegovy pill, with sales starting as soon as Monday.

Analysts predict Foundayo will generate ~$21 billion in global sales by 2030, compared to $4 billion for Wegovy's pill. Lilly has almost $1.5 billion worth of pills ready to ship.
