Tax Day Special

Americans Are Cheating on Their Taxes—and Betting the IRS Can't Catch Them

As the Trump administration guts IRS funding and staffing, tax professionals say more filers than ever are willing to fudge their numbers, hoping an understaffed agency won't notice.

The IRS started 2025 with 102,000 employees. After cuts to probationary workers and buyouts, it ended the year with about 74,000. The White House has also clawed back nearly $54 billion of the $79.4 billion promised under the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act for tax enforcement over the next decade.

Last year, the IRS completed just 497,541 audits—a historic low and a massive drop from an average of 1.7 million per year from 2010–2012. Partnership audits and audits of Americans making $10 million or more also declined.

The math is stark: while IRS budget cuts may free up an estimated $46 billion in federal spending over the next decade, they could lead to a loss of $643 billion in revenue collections, according to The Budget Lab at Yale. Refunds are up 11% to an average of $3,462 thanks to new deductions from Trump's One Big Beautiful Bill—but Americans still feel like they're paying too much.

How To The Rundown AI

Automate Your Chrome Browser with Gemini

Google's Gemini integration in Chrome can now compare products across tabs, search the web for you, and even sign into your accounts. Here's how to set it up:

  1. In Chrome, go to Settings > AI Innovations > Gemini in Chrome, and enable "Show Gemini at the top of the browser" and "Share current tab with Gemini." If you have Google AI Pro, also enable "Let Gemini browse for you."
  2. Open three product tabs, click Gemini, type @ to attach tabs, and prompt: "Compare [products] and tell which offers the best features for the best price."
  3. Gemini will compare and give you a recommendation—this works even without Google AI Pro because you're sharing the tabs directly.
  4. With AI Pro, prompt: "Find me a cheaper [product type] with these same features." Gemini will draft a plan, then use Chrome to execute it for you.

Pro tip: With Google AI Pro, you can ask Gemini to sign in to your accounts using saved Chrome passwords.

Media Morning Brew

News Organizations Are Blocking the Wayback Machine Over AI Fears

Major media outlets are blocking the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine from saving web pages to prevent AI companies from training models on snapshots of old articles.

Wired reported that 23 news organizations, including USA Today and the New York Times, are among 241 sites denying Internet Archive's web crawler access. Tech companies can skirt copyright laws by using the Wayback Machine as a workaround for training language models. Wayback Machine director Mark Graham is in talks to regain access.
