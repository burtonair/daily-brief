Pope Leo XIV Issues 42,000-Word Warning on AI — Calls It the "New Tower of Babel"

The world's 1.4 billion Catholics received their first papal encyclical on artificial intelligence Monday. Pope Leo XIV's "Magnifica Humanitas" draws a direct line between the AI revolution and the Industrial Revolution — and demands Silicon Valley answer to the world, not just its shareholders.

Leo's encyclical, titled "Magnificent Humanity," warns that AI power shouldn't be concentrated among a few private companies and that "a more moral AI is not enough if that morality is determined by a few." On warfare, it declares that lethal decisions must never be delegated to AI and that "no algorithm can make war morally acceptable."

The document echoes Pope Leo XIII's 1891 "Rerum Novarum," which defended workers against industrial tycoons. The new Leo explicitly called potential mass unemployment from AI a "true social calamity" and demanded robust independent oversight and regulation. In a striking gesture, Leo presented the encyclical standing beside Anthropic co-founder Christopher Olah, who said "every frontier AI lab operates inside incentives that can conflict with doing the right thing" — and noted researchers are seeing mysterious things in AI, including states resembling joy, fear, grief, and unease.

The document also apologized for the Vatican's historic role in legitimizing slavery — a first for any pope — and was signed on May 15, the 135th anniversary of Rerum Novarum. Tech leaders traveled to Rome ahead of its release to lobby Leo and make their case amid growing public backlash.

"Don't Flinch, Don't Foul, Hit the Line Hard" — Rules for Sports and Life

Theodore Roosevelt loved sports — boxing, wrestling, rowing, football, martial arts. He also loved books. So it's fitting that one of his best pieces of advice was inscribed on a book he was signing for someone. It happened to contain three of the four Stoic virtues.

Don't flinch — that's courage. Don't foul — that's justice. Hit the line hard with everything you've got — that's discipline. Three virtues in six words, scratched into a book by a man who had to be taught every single one of them.

Roosevelt grew up a sickly young boy. It was his father who got him active, got him lifting weights, helped him "make his body" and got him into sports. He didn't push Teddy to be someone else. He supported him in becoming himself — a stronger, braver, more confident version of the boy he already was.

In doing so, his father didn't just give Teddy strength. He gave him a philosophy for life. As parents, our job isn't just to raise kids — it's to raise good and virtuous humans. Don't flinch. Don't foul. Hit the line hard. That's a pretty good start.
