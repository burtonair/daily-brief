Sanders' AI Sovereign Wealth Fund: 50% in stock, and a seat at the boardroom 1% Better

Bernie Sanders is preparing the American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act — a one-time 50% tax, payable in stock, on every U.S. company with more than $200m in annual AI sales. A seven-person presidential commission would hold the shares and vote them, with board seats at every major AI firm.

The headline figure is 50%. The clause that matters sits further down: a commission, nominated by the president and Senate-confirmed, would hold the confiscated shares and vote them — with equal representation on each company's board — to block decisions that "hurt the American people." The stock seeds the fund; the votes are the point.

There's a precedent attached. Last August the federal government became Intel's largest single shareholder via a 9.9% stake funded with unspent CHIPS Act grants. Intel's filing recorded that Washington had agreed to vote its shares as the board recommended. That was ownership without control, by design. Sanders proposes to invert every term — half instead of a tenth, board overrule instead of board deference, teeth instead of teeth pulled.

The sweep is wide. Defined by AI revenue rather than the marquee labs Sanders names, the levy catches Nvidia, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Oracle — the whole index. Anthropic and OpenAI were last valued at $965bn and $852bn; half their combined equity is about $900bn, a long way from Sanders' $7trn estimate. The arithmetic only closes if the tax captures the index, and the more of the index it captures, the more the levy reprices the rest.

Strip the trillions the taking would vaporize, and what survives is what doesn't depend on the stock holding value: the seats, the votes, the veto. A commission that cannot make Americans rich can still vote on whether OpenAI ships its next model. That, in the end, is the asset the bill actually delivers — and it's the one Sanders has been clearest about wanting.

This Is How We Get Stronger

Most dads try to figure things out as they go. The work changes when you stop figuring it out alone.

You read the emails. You listen to the podcast. You care about being a better dad. But what kind of parent could you be if you took that intention just a little bit further?

One dad started a new practice of checking in with his kids — asking how their days went, always ending with "I love you." Another tried to pull back on yelling, only to notice his wife sliding into a "bad cop" role. That observation became a conversation, and the conversation changed how they parented together.

Another dad ran an audit on himself, counting how many times he hugged his daughter in a day. After realizing it wasn't enough, he committed to at least three a day. The relationship got better. The listening got easier. The work, day after day, is what compounds.

Learning to be more patient and less reactive. More present and less distracted. Breaking cycles you don't want to pass down. Showing up every day with more intention than the day before. That's the work.

Build and host a custom CRM with Google Antigravity

Turn a plain-English app spec into a hosted full-stack CRM. The key move: make the agent plan first, then use Firebase Auth, Firestore, and Firebase Hosting so the app ends up with login, saved data, search, and a real URL.

  1. Download Google Antigravity. Install and open the desktop app.
  2. Create a new folder, then open that folder in Antigravity. A clean folder keeps the agent focused.
  3. Make sure you're signed in with Google — this is what Antigravity and Firebase will use.
  4. Give Antigravity a two-line prompt: "Build Northstar CRM with login, contacts, companies, deals, notes, search, and dashboard cards. Use React, Vite, TypeScript, Firebase Auth, Firestore, and Firebase Hosting; plan first."
  5. Review the plan, then let Antigravity build the app. If it adds billing, teams, or extra SaaS features, cut the scope back.
  6. Ask Antigravity how to install Firebase and follow those instructions. Then have it deploy to Firebase Hosting and test the live URL.

Going further: Ask Antigravity to add Google login, automatically set up Google Analytics, or connect a custom domain after the Firebase deploy works.

Tech / AI Rundown

Pew: Americans using AI more, but trusting it less

Pew's 2026 data on more than 5,000 U.S. adults shows adoption and optimism running in opposite directions — usage climbing while sentiment slides.

Chatbots just crossed a milestone: about half of U.S. adults now use one, and a quarter do so daily — a leap from a third of the public in 2024. But pessimism is the dominant note. Nearly 40% expect AI to make society worse over the next 20 years; only 16% think it will change things for the better.

The under-30 crowd leans on AI the hardest and trusts it the least, with only 14% seeing a positive payoff for society. ChatGPT dominates at 44% of adults — double its 2023 reach — with Gemini at 24% and Claude at just 6%. Anthropic remains the constant talk of the industry while barely registering with the average American.

Business Brew

Carvana's latest strategy: a "new-car playground"

Carvana unveiled its first salespersonless pseudo-dealership in Dallas — themed outdoor areas for each brand, QR codes for info, and staff on hourly wages with no commission.

Jeeps perch on off-road displays, Dodges have fake racetracks, and Chrysler minivans guard a soccer net. Customers can scan QR codes to request test drives. All purchases still happen online. Cox Automotive data shows most customers prefer a blend of online and in-person interactions, and payroll at playground locations could run well below typical dealerships.

The company has purchased seven Stellantis-franchised dealerships since February 2025 — one of which became Stellantis's top-selling U.S. location. More new-vehicle sales will also help Carvana's loan business, which still drives most of its profits.

Inside the 14-point US–Iran memorandum

The agreement establishes a $300bn rebuilding fund (the U.S. won't contribute), waives all sanctions and unfreezes Iranian funds if nuclear terms are met, and — in what's viewed as the biggest win for Iran — allows immediate oil sales.

Negotiations on a final settlement begin in Switzerland tomorrow, with 60 days to finalize. Iran has pledged to reopen the Strait of Hormuz and not pursue nuclear weapons. Trump said he was motivated to avoid being compared to Herbert Hoover, and floated handing the deal off to VP JD Vance: "If it works out, I'm going to take the credit. If it doesn't work out, I'm blaming JD."
