Anthropic Files Confidentially for IPO at $965 Billion Valuation

The maker of Claude has taken the first formal step toward going public, filing a confidential S-1 with the SEC — setting up a landmark AI IPO race with OpenAI, which has not yet filed.

Anthropic filed a confidential prospectus with the SEC on Monday, officially entering the IPO queue after its latest fundraising round valued the company at $965 billion — nudging it just above rival OpenAI. The company said "the number of shares to be offered and the price have not yet been set," signaling the process is still in early stages.

The filing comes amid surging demand for Anthropic's Claude models, which are now being used directly inside Nvidia's new Vera CPU chip — described by Jensen Huang at COMPUTEX 2026 as "the CPU for agents." Anthropic, OpenAI, and the NYSE are all listed as early Vera adopters, underscoring how deeply the company has embedded itself in the emerging agentic AI infrastructure stack.

On Capitol Hill, Anthropic's impending IPO is already drawing political attention. Sen. Bernie Sanders previewed the American A.I. Sovereign Wealth Fund Act, which would route half the stock of the largest AI companies — including Anthropic, OpenAI, and xAI — into a public fund that pays dividends to ordinary Americans. Sanders wrote in a New York Times op-ed that "A.I. is being built on a public resource far more valuable than oil: the accumulated knowledge, creativity, and labor of mankind."

OpenAI, which is also planning an IPO but has not yet filed, now faces pressure to accelerate its timeline. The race to the public markets between the two dominant AI labs will be one of the defining financial stories of 2026.

Columnist | Daily Dad

You Keep What You Can Of Them

We don't get them for long. We don't get them as much as we'd like. Because they grow up so fast. Because of the divorce. Because we travel for work. Because they have to go to school.

And as crazy as they drive us when we have them, we miss them so much when we don't. Our hearts ache for them.

It's worth remembering that's how it goes the next time they are driving you crazy — just how much you will be missing them soon enough. You'll be jealous of this person you are now soon enough.

We must, then, not just be present — but take in what this is. Take in traces of them, of this, to sustain us later. There's that great line in The National song that goes — speaking of love and family: "I keep what I can of you / Split-second glimpses and snapshots and sounds."

Because that's what we'll have. That's what we'll carry. Pay attention to it. Hold onto it. Keep what you can.

Economy / Labor Morning Brew

Study Says WFH — Not AI — Is the Bigger Culprit Behind Gen Z's Hiring Woes

A New York Fed study found remote work is responsible for 64% of the rise in unemployment among college graduates under 29 since the pre-pandemic era.

The unemployment rate for young college graduates hit 5.6% in March 2026, up from 3.6% in March 2019. The NY Fed found that the jump in "remotable" jobs drove nearly 1% more unemployment among workers under 29 — while the same shift actually reduced unemployment among those 29 and older. For non-remote jobs, there's little difference between the age groups.

Using proprietary data from a Fortune 500 company, the Fed found employers reluctant to hire inexperienced workers for remote roles because skill development is harder in isolation. A Wharton management professor told Fortune that without older millennials in the office to model professional behavior, Gen Z's long-term value to employers is declining — pushing companies toward candidates who already developed skills in the pre-remote world.

As for AI: the NY Fed said it "may play a more primary role" in the future. A Goldman Sachs report found ~21,900 layoffs in April were explicitly attributed to AI — the highest monthly total since GS began tracking in 2023.

Hackers Accessed High-Profile Instagram Accounts by Simply Asking Meta AI

Meta fixed a flaw where its AI help tool could be prompted to hand over password reset codes for any account — including a dormant Barack Obama profile, Sephora, and Space Force leadership.

Starting in March, Meta gave its AI the ability to handle password resets on Facebook and Instagram. Hackers discovered they could switch a VPN to a target's region and ask AI support for a password reset and email change — and the AI would send a verification code to the new address. Accounts were then resold within minutes.

Meta told 404 Media the exploit "has been resolved and we are securing impacted accounts." The vulnerability was reportedly active for months before being patched. The incident raises broader questions about what happens when frontier AI companies hand their entire support stack to a model so easily prompted into dangerous behavior.

Consumer Morning Brew

Gym Rats Beware: A Protein Powder Shortage Has Arrived

Whey powder wholesale prices hit record highs in April as America's protein obsession outpaces the dairy industry's capacity to supply it.

Demand for whey protein has surged as food companies race to add it to everything from Doritos to Starbucks cold foam. Some suppliers are already sold out for the rest of the year, according to the USDA. A tub that cost ~$40 on Amazon six months ago hit $54 last month; one supplement company paused sales after its costs jumped more than 300% since 2023.

Food companies targeting the gains-obsessed and GLP-1 crowd are now eyeing soy powder and milk protein concentrate as alternatives — which could noticeably change flavors and textures. Relief may be coming: the North American dairy industry is undergoing a $12 billion expansion projected to close the supply-demand gap "in the near future," since whey is a byproduct of cheese-making.
