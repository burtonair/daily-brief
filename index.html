Live Events Are Becoming Traveling Boom Towns

From BTS selling out 34 cities worldwide to sports tourism surpassing $1 trillion, event travel is reshaping local economies around the globe. Morning Brew

Taylor Swift's and Beyoncé's GDP-shifting world tours showed how restaurants and hotels can reap massive benefits when big arena shows come to town. Now BTS has taken the baton — flights and hotel rooms in 34 cities sold out after the K-pop group announced its reunion tour, with Booking.com searches surging 6,700% for one Taiwanese host city.

The World Economic Forum estimates music tourism could surpass $9 billion by 2030 — a 50% increase from 2023 — driven by millennial and Gen Z spending power. Sports tourism is even bigger, on track to comfortably surpass $1 trillion in the next few years.

California is ground zero: after hosting the Super Bowl and NBA All-Star Game, the Golden State will host FIFA World Cup games this summer, the Super Bowl again next year, and the 2028 Olympics — generating billions in local revenue.

Sports & Business Morning Brew

Formula 1 Is Not Slowing Down

The open-wheel racing competition hit $3.9 billion in revenue last year — a 14% jump from 2024 — as it continues to turn racetracks into economic engines.

2025 was the most-attended F1 season ever, drawing 6.7 million spectators over 24 races. Nineteen events sold out, eleven set new attendance records, and the British Grand Prix drew 500,000 fans. Tickets can run hundreds of dollars for multiday passes that include live entertainment — Post Malone is set to perform at the US Grand Prix in Austin this October.

Cities pay F1 millions in promoter fees for hosting rights, accounting for about $824 million of F1 earnings in 2025. The Belgian Grand Prix alone contributes around $250 million to the country's economy each year.

The downside: the war in Iran forced the cancellation of two Middle Eastern races in Bahrain and Saudi Arabia, potentially costing F1 about $200 million in revenue.

Culture & Travel Morning Brew

The Biggest Mass Gatherings Involve the Divine

Religious pilgrimages are among the oldest forms of travel — and cheap airfare has made them some of the planet's largest mass-migration events, generating nearly $290 billion in economic activity.

The Hajj: Last year drew over 1.6 million pilgrims to Mecca, who paid from $5,000 to over $25,000 for tours. Some countries, including Nigeria and Indonesia, subsidize the cost for their citizens.

Rosh Hashanah in Uman: Up to 60,000 Hasidic Jews trek to a small Ukrainian city 150 miles south of Kyiv every September — a pilgrimage that continued even after Russia's 2022 invasion.

Maha Kumbh Mela: Known as the world's largest human gathering, last year's Hindu festival drew over 660 million people to sacred river sites for purifying bathing rituals.

Feast of the Black Nazarene: Almost 10 million devotees walked barefoot through Manila streets alongside a 30-hour procession carrying a replica of a sacred 17th-century wooden statue.

Alibaba's Accio Work: From AI Assistants to Autonomous Operators

An exclusive Q&A with alibaba.com president Kuo Zhang reveals how agentic AI is shifting from helping with tasks to running entire business operations.

The organizational shift: Zhang says success with AI operators isn't about speed — it's about "high-value decisions it can navigate and the actual profit it can bring." Teams need to move from doing grunt work to designing the logic, becoming "architects and referees" who set standards and provide final human sign-off.

How it works: Accio Work assembles Qwen-powered agent teams based on business goals — no code required. A real example: agents can analyze market trends, select products, set up e-commerce stores on Shopify or Amazon, negotiate with suppliers across multiple rounds, then handle logistics and compliance — with humans only stepping in for final payment approval.

The bold prediction: Asked how far we are from the first one-person billion-dollar company, Zhang replied: "Probably just months." He sees a future where "most business tasks flow between autonomous agents, with minimal human initiation" — what he calls the A2A (agent-to-agent) era of B2B.
