SUNDAY DEEP DIVE — INVESTING

How Gen Z Got So Savvy at Investing

Young Americans are investing earlier than ever — 40% of 26-year-olds now move money into investment accounts, up from just 8% a decade ago. App-based platforms, housing unaffordability, and distrust in traditional safety nets are rewriting the rules.

The number of 26-year-olds who moved money into investment accounts (not including 401(k)s) after turning 22 jumped from 8% in 2015 to 40% as of May 2025, according to data from the JPMorgan Chase Institute.

In a 2024 World Economic Forum survey, 30% of Gen Z respondents said they began investing in early adulthood before entering the workforce, compared to just 6% of baby boomers. Six percent of Gen Zers said they started investing "in adolescence."

Studies show that young people don't have much faith in traditional financial safety nets or a stable job market, pushing younger investors toward nontraditional, riskier investments like crypto and prediction markets. But perhaps the biggest factor is that most young people with extra cash are still priced out of buying a house. The share of American homebuyers aged 18 to 39 dropped from 51% in 1999 to 44% last year, according to a Redfin analysis of census data. Morning Brew

SOCIAL MEDIA & FINANCE

The Rise of the Finfluencer — And Why Gen Z Trusts Them Anyway Morning Brew

"Suddenly, you have someone who doesn't look like your dad's financial advisor. You have somebody who looks like I could be anybody's college best friend," says Vivian Tu, aka Your Rich BFF.

More than any other generation, Gen Zers invest by sowing what they scroll. Social media is the main reason 55% of Gen Zers say they started investing, compared to 44% of millennials, the think tank Oliver Wyman Forum recently found.

But here's the paradox: in a Charles Schwab study, Gen Zers ranked social media as the least trustworthy source for financial tips — even as they relied on it most. Last year, more than half of respondents in a Certified Financial Planner Board survey said they made regrettable financial moves based on misleading online information.

The latest finfluencing entrant: Jimmy Donaldson, aka MrBeast, whose company recently bought a teen-focused banking app that has toyed with launching a crypto feature, prompting questions from Sen. Elizabeth Warren.

SPECULATION

Financial Nihilism: Why Americans Are Betting Big on Risky Assets Morning Brew

In 2025, 17% of American investors said they owned crypto, up from 2% in 2018. About a third of Gen Zers plan to invest in sports betting or prediction markets this year.

Much of this trading is happening on digital platforms like Robinhood, Coinbase, and Kalshi that have made it possible to go long and short on speculative assets from a smartphone. Stock options — once the domain of professional traders — have seen retail investors' share hover between 40% and 50% in recent years, compared to about 35% pre-pandemic.

Meanwhile, many Americans are tying their financial futures to volatile physical assets like gold, luxury watches, and trading cards. Data cited by Bloomberg shows that crypto investments were more prevalent among renters with a net worth under $300,000 than among homeowners — suggesting Americans may be placing risky bets instead of pursuing homeownership.

PERSONAL FINANCE

A Day in the Life of a Day Trader Morning Brew

The cliché of a twitching, unshaven man pounding energy drinks is just a stereotype. The reality is lonelier and less profitable than the TikTok highlights suggest.

Many day traders say too much caffeine is actually a detriment to making money. Studies show most day traders are losing money. Nearly a quarter surveyed in 2022 reported severe stress levels, and a study on isolated careers found only crypto traders scored higher on the UCLA Loneliness Scale.

Investing Jargon, Explained

TERM
Buying the Dip

Buying a stock or asset after its price drops, hoping to get it at a discount before it recovers. Like when Timon and Pumbaa rescued Simba at his lowest point — they bought the dip.

TERM
Selling the Rip

Selling a stock or asset when its value is strong to lock in gains. Like selling your Forrest Gump stock after he became a shrimp millionaire — Forrest ripped a lot.
