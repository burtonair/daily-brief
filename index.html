Supreme Court

SCOTUS blocks Trump from firing Fed Governor Lisa Cook — for now Brew

The Supreme Court ruled 5–4 that Trump violated due process when he tried to dismiss Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook, preserving the central bank’s independence — while simultaneously expanding presidential power to fire officials at other regulatory agencies.

Chief Justice John Roberts wrote the majority opinion, stressing that both the fact and the appearance of independence are fundamental to the Fed’s design: “Not only the fact of independence but also the appearance of independence is key to the Federal Reserve’s design.” Cook can keep her job while she challenges the firing — but the door remains open for Trump to oust her if he can demonstrate “for cause” as the law requires.

Trump alleged Cook committed mortgage fraud — a charge she denies and critics call a pretext to install someone who will vote for his preferred interest rate cuts. That independence matters to investors: it underpins faith in the bond market and the strength of the US dollar.

A separate 6–3 ruling written by Roberts went the other way, upholding Trump’s firing of FTC Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter and granting the president power to dismiss officials at other independent agencies. The court closes its term today with additional rulings expected on birthright citizenship, transgender athletes in women’s sports, and political party spending limits.

Today’s SCOTUS docket also includes rulings on Trump’s bid to end birthright citizenship, state bans on transgender athletes, and whether spending restrictions on political parties coordinating with candidates violate the First Amendment.

These Are Not Unreasonable Feelings

They don’t want to wait. They don’t want to be the only one not doing something. They’d rather eat something that tastes good. They don’t want to be bored. You know what these are — the things you get upset at your kids for? They are reasonable preferences. They are perfectly understandable feelings.

And they are all the more reasonable when you’re little and impatient and insecure and still figuring out the world. Dr. Becky describes entitlement as “the intolerance of uncomfortable feelings.” Their feelings are reasonable — they’re just struggling a little bit at dealing with them, at understanding how to express them and how seriously to take them.

They don’t know, as you know, that they’ll survive this bit of discomfort or boredom. They don’t yet grasp that life isn’t always getting what you want. So help them learn that. Don’t smack them down or shame them. Talk to them. Help them see. Better yet, show them how you deal with it when you have those very same feelings.

Automate Any Manual Task with OpenAI’s Record & Replay

OpenAI’s “Record & Replay” tool in Codex (macOS) lets you teach AI to do any repetitive task by simply recording your screen once — then it runs the workflow itself.

  1. Install Codex, sign in, and pick a task (uploading a YouTube video, exporting reports, etc.). Open Plugins and install Record & Replay and Computer Use.
  2. Prompt: “Record me doing this task and turn it into a reusable skill. Open apps from the dock, paste URLs into fresh tabs, and show Codex how you reached each file or page.”
  3. Hit the red stop button when done. Codex drafts a named skill. Test it in the same thread by typing / plus the skill name.
  4. Watch Codex run the workflow itself and give feedback on how to improve. Once it works, schedule it monthly — like the last day of each month for reporting.

AI & Science Rundown

Meta turns brain scans into full typed sentences — without surgery

Meta’s Brain2Qwerty v2 can decode whole sentences from a non-invasive brain scan at 61% average word accuracy — leaping past the previous non-invasive best of 8% and closing the gap on surgical implants.

Nine volunteers spent 10 hours each inside a brain scanner, typing while the system read along, producing nearly 22,000 sentences of data. One model reads raw brain signals while a second adds meaning — the top-scoring volunteer hit 78% accuracy. Meta also open-sourced the code for v1 and v2.

Meta found accuracy climbs with more data, saying the gap with surgical implants “could be further narrowed through data scaling alone.” For people who have lost speech, this is the meaningful path forward: the difference between a rare operation and something far more could access.

Election Law Brew

SCOTUS rules 5–4 that states can count mail-in ballots received after Election Day

Federal law sets Election Day as the deadline to cast a vote, but does not set a deadline for ballot receipt — so states with grace periods for late-arriving mail ballots can keep counting, the court ruled.

Justice Amy Coney Barrett wrote for a majority that included Chief Justice Roberts and the court’s liberal wing. The specific case challenged Mississippi’s five-day grace period, but the ruling affects more than half the states that already count at least some late-arriving ballots.

That’s a setback for Trump, who used the ruling to push the SAVE Act, which would tighten voting restrictions. Three other SCOTUS decisions are expected today.

Workplace Brew

Study: CEOs who score high on narcissism are also the most anti-WFH

A Wharton study by organizational psychologist Adam Grant found that narcissistic executives — measured by pay-package size and headshot prominence in company reports — are disproportionately opposed to remote work.

The researchers tracked factors like the size of an exec’s photo on annual reports over six years to build a narcissism index. Those who scored higher on that index were also the most anti-WFH — likely because the admiration and respect they crave is harder to receive over Slack.

In other words: if your boss is plastering their face in every company-wide communication and demanding you come back to the office, a new study suggests those two things may be related.
