America Is Sobering Up — And the Booze Business Is Feeling It

Alcohol sales are slumping to historic lows as young people embrace sobriety, wellness culture, and cannabis, reshaping a trillion-dollar industry.

Only 54% of Americans reported consuming alcohol last year — the lowest since Gallup began polling in 1939. The teetotalism trend is fueled by rising health consciousness, mounting studies on the harms of even moderate drinking, weight-loss drugs reducing cravings, and tighter budgets. Trade war tensions have compounded the problem, with Canadian boycotts and European consumers pivoting to domestic options.

Iconic brands are reeling. Global wine consumption dropped 12% between 2020 and 2024. Diageo — which owns Guinness, Smirnoff, and Johnnie Walker — saw sales fall nearly 3%. Jim Beam paused distillation for a full year at its flagship Kentucky facility amid a global bourbon glut. Heineken is cutting 6,000 jobs after selling 1.2% fewer liters of beer in 2025. The market cap of the world's top publicly listed alcohol producers was down 46% last fall from its June 2021 peak.

Yet not all is lost for the industry. US sales of cheap tequila and premixed cocktails have grown, and while home drinking is down, spending at bars was up 4% in January year-over-year — suggesting consumers still crave the social experience, just not necessarily the hangover that comes with it.

AI Will Make Engineering More Human, Not Less: What Changes, What Breaks, and What Still Belongs to Humans

A week ago, Spotify's co-CEO claimed their best developers haven't written a "single line of code" this year. It echoes a wave of executives who describe AI coding agents as the future of software. But bringing AI into real engineering workflows is more nuanced than flipping a switch and going on autopilot. The Rundown AI sat down with Rajeev Rajan, CTO of Atlassian — the company behind Jira, Confluence, and Loom — to understand how human roles evolve in an AI-native world.

🤖 The Non-Negotiable Cost of Coding with AI

Research shows that 45% of AI-generated code still contains security flaws. Rajan's answer isn't to slow down — it's to redesign the workflow on the assumption that AI is in the loop by default.

"In code review, we are entrusting AI to catch bugs, enforce coding standards, and explain complex changes. Rovo Dev helped reduce PR cycle time by 45% and auto-resolved 51% of potential security vulnerabilities. The nature of review is changing: instead of humans reading every line of a peer's code, it's about a human owner reviewing an agent's work."

At deployment, Rajan says, safety systems must keep pace with speed: smaller batches, heavier continuous integration, stronger observability, and fast rollbacks. "You can't operate in a black-box scenario."

❓ When AI Writes the Code, What Will an Engineer Do?

"By 2028, I would not be surprised if most new code in large companies is AI-generated," Rajan says. "We're seeing a shift where every engineer is a tech lead, orchestrating systems and agents. Engineers now spend more time driving clarity and owning what happens 'left of code' and 'right of code' — from planning and design on one side to testing, rollout safety, and operations on the other."

On new graduates entering the field: "Focusing on the right fundamentals and adopting the AI-native way of working will give new grads a big advantage — potentially allowing them to leapfrog senior developers who haven't adopted AI ways of working yet. Your edge will come from judgment: knowing when to trust the AI and when to challenge it."

⚙️ Building Agentic AI for Developer Joy

Atlassian framed developer productivity as "Developer Joy" — and raised developer satisfaction scores from 49% to 83%. One key lesson from early Rovo Dev testing: engineers didn't trust opaque "magic." Atlassian scrapped a "one click, do it all" flow because their own teams wouldn't use it without transparency.

"They wanted a way to understand each step the agent took, how their instructions led to different outcomes, and the ability to steer the agent. That pushed us hard toward agent sessions you can inspect and experiences that keep developers in the loop."

🧠 What Happens When AI Makes a Mistake?

"When something goes wrong, 'the AI did it' can't be the answer — AI doesn't own customer trust, we do," Rajan says. "Every AI-assisted decision or action has to have a clear human owner. If we can't understand or observe how an AI is behaving, it doesn't belong in a critical path."

Atlassian logs and audits AI actions, puts guardrails and observability around every agent, and treats AI like any other powerful piece of infrastructure: understand failure modes, monitor it, and don't ship without ownership.

⚡ Is SaaS Really Dead? The "Saaspocalypse" Theory Debunked

"The idea that one person can vibe-code an in-house solution over a weekend and replace a mature SaaS product — in my view, is overrated," Rajan says. "When customers buy software-as-a-service, they're not just buying the code; they buy workflows, shared context, security, compliance, and reliability."

His take: AI makes great SaaS even more valuable. "Your projects, docs, tickets, and conversations live in those systems, and AI can now move across them, automate the boring parts, and orchestrate agents around trusted workflows and controls. I am more interested in SaaS that becomes AI-native than in hot takes that SaaS is dead."

⚡ Quick Hits with Rajeev Rajan

Most underrated AI trend? "Lots of AI products today are designed for a single-player system. We see much greater potential in how AI helps entire teams work better together — allowing important context to flow across a team of humans and agents."

Something most people in tech would disagree with? "I think AI will make engineering more human, not less. A lot of people worry we will lose the craft — I believe we will spend less energy on repetitive implementation and more time on strategic, creative work and collaboration."

Advice for teams struggling with developer burnout? "Start by fixing one concrete, high-friction problem that impacts your team. You'd be surprised by how quickly chipping away at issues like slow build times and noisy tooling can multiply and have a greater impact."

Gen Z's Relationship with Alcohol: A Multi-Ingredient Mocktail of Reasons Morning Brew

Only about half of US adults aged 18–34 reported drinking in 2025 — a nine-point drop from 2023 — putting young people below the drinking rate of Americans aged 35 and up for the first time.

There isn't one single cause driving younger Americans away from alcohol. Per Gallup, Gen Z is more likely than any other generation to believe that moderate drinking is harmful. Fewer in-person social gatherings also mean fewer occasions to drink, while legalized marijuana, an older legal drinking age, and alcohol's evolving cultural reputation are all contributing factors, according to experts who spoke to Time magazine.

Bars and nightclubs have had to adapt, expanding non-alcoholic drink menus and focusing more on atmosphere than alcohol volume. But the shift is creating financial friction — when customers close out after one or two drinks rather than running open tabs, bartenders lose income and establishments absorb more credit card fees per transaction.

Sober Bars: From Niche Recovery Spaces to a Booming Hospitality Trend Morning Brew

Non-alcoholic bars are proliferating across the US, catering to sober-curious consumers — and the data suggests that going booze-free can actually grow a bar's bottom line.

What began as spaces for people in recovery has evolved into a broader cultural movement. Sober bars have opened in Los Angeles, Columbus, Atlanta, Denver, and beyond since the pandemic. Pioneers like Sans Bar in Austin and Listen Bar in NYC have been operating for nearly a decade. Hekate in NYC even runs a boozy sister bar alongside its alcohol-free venue.

The financials are surprisingly compelling. In 2024, establishments generating at least $2 million annually reported revenue boosts of $95,000 after expanding their non-alcoholic drink menus. And 92% of consumers who purchased NA products last year also purchased traditional alcohol — meaning sober-curious drinkers aren't abandoning bars, they're supplementing their visits.

Alcohol retail sales dropped an average of 5% last year, yet customers spent 4% more at bars, suggesting that Gen Z and other sober-curious consumers still value the social ritual — they just want to wake up feeling good the next morning.

The Accountability Layer: Why "The AI Did It" Will Never Be an Excuse The Rundown AI

As AI agents take on increasingly autonomous roles in software development, Atlassian CTO Rajeev Rajan argues that accountability structures must evolve just as fast as the technology itself.

"When something goes wrong, 'the AI did it' can't be the answer — AI doesn't own customer trust, we do," Rajan told The Rundown AI. Every AI-assisted decision or action must have a clearly designated human owner. If a team can't observe or understand how an AI is behaving in a given context, it has no place in a critical production path.

Atlassian's approach includes comprehensive logging and auditing of AI actions, guardrails and observability layers around every agent, and a cultural norm of treating AI like any powerful tool: understand its failure modes, monitor it continuously, and never ship without clear ownership. The lesson, Rajan says, is that speed is only an advantage when the system behind it is airtight.

The Bar Is Out. The Sauna Is In. How Young Tech Workers Network Now. Morning Brew

Silicon Valley's infamous party culture has given way to cold plunges, social saunas, and 80-hour work weeks — as young founders and investors abandon alcohol-centric schmoozing for wellness-forward alternatives.

Young tech professionals are increasingly rejecting the martini-lunch era of professional networking. A 2025 Gallup survey found that for the first time on record, more than half of Americans believe moderate drinking is unhealthy. The Wall Street Journal reported that so-called "social saunas" — sweat boxes fitting dozens of people with nightclub-like lighting — have flourished in New York and San Francisco as investors grow tired of meeting over dinner and drinks.

One 23-year-old AI founder told the Wall Street Journal: "Why would I go drink at a bar if I can be building a company?" Prominent teetotalers now inspiring the next generation of entrepreneurs include Bryan Johnson, Joe Rogan (who quit drinking last year), Warren Buffett, Larry Ellison, and Marc Andreessen — who was partly inspired by podcasting neuroscientist Andrew Huberman.

Government influence is playing a role too. The former US Surgeon General opened 2025 by calling for cigarette-style warnings on alcohol. Neither President Trump nor Health Secretary RFK Jr. drink.
