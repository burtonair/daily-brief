The Death of Swiping: IRL Meetups Woo Dating App Defectors

As dating apps see declining use, a constellation of services is throwing in-person events that offer singles an opportunity to mingle in person — from dinner clubs and game nights to salsa dances and no-couples ski trips. Morning Brew

The Dinner Table Club organizes dinners with strangers at local restaurants in New York and Los Angeles, costing around $75–$100. TimeLeft lets users book dinner, drinks, or a run with strangers. Thursday puts together singles dinners, game nights, and salsa dances in dozens of cities worldwide.

Even the swiping apps have acknowledged the trend. Tinder recently introduced features allowing users to find or organize get-togethers, while Hinge expanded its $1 million initiative giving grants of up to $25,000 to nonprofits organizing IRL dating events.

Some gyms are even hosting dating-oriented workout sessions, Eventbrite saw singles nights grow in popularity, and Partiful now notifies users if someone who attended the same event has a crush on them. But investors are wary the model can match the $6.2 billion swiping industry’s returns.

Meetup’s Long Road: From Post-9/11 Community Tool to Bending Spoons Turnaround

The 24-year-old platform that once boasted 50 million members is betting higher fees and paywalled features can fuel a post-pandemic resurgence.

Meetup initially grew by inviting niche online blogs to organize Meetups for fake holidays like International Pug Lovers Meetup Day. It crossed 1 million users in 2004 after being publicized as a grassroots organizing tool for the presidential race, and hit profitability in 2010.

WeWork bought Meetup for $156 million in 2017 — about 100,000 people had already attended Meetups at WeWork spaces. But when WeWork crashed in 2019, Meetup laid off 25% of staff and then was sold for far less than the purchase price in March 2020.

Italian firm Bending Spoons — known for buying struggling tech companies like Evernote, Vimeo, AOL, and Eventbrite — acquired Meetup in 2024 and immediately raised prices. Despite user frustration, new app registrations increased by 20% last year.

Solo Travel (in a Group) Is Really Taking Off

A quarter of Americans find solo travel intimidating, but tour companies are calming jitters by tailoring group trips specifically to solo travelers.

Solo travel can be an opportunity to build confidence, broaden your horizons, and unplug from daily stresses. Per Grand View Research, the global solo travel market was worth almost $550 billion last year and is expected to nearly triple by 2033.

Women are driving the trend: according to luxury travel company Virtuoso, more than 70% of solo travelers are women, prompting many tour companies to specialize in women-only trips. Safety concerns, cost, and fear of being alone are the biggest barriers, per Talker Research.

Are Running Clubs for Dating or Running?

As run clubs boom post-pandemic, TikTokers and members are divided on whether the real endorphin boost is the jog or the romantic potential.

The case for dating: TikTokers have pointed out that running clubs offer a better endorphin boost than scampering across the pavement: finding a date. You already have something in common, you’ve seen each other at your sweatiest, and with some clubs reaching 300+ members, the odds of rejection aren’t as high.

The case for running: Most people are at run clubs to run, not to hook up. Unsolicited flirting at sunrise, especially through heavy panting, is not the romantic moment some think it is.
