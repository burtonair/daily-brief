Ferrari's First EV Gets a Bruising Reception — Stock Drops 5% at Launch

The $640,000 Luce, co-designed by Jony Ive, was supposed to be Ferrari's triumphant electric debut. Instead, the internet compared it to a Nissan Leaf, a former Ferrari boss warned of “destroying a legend,” and shares shed 5% on reveal day.

Ferrari launched the Luce Tuesday — its highly anticipated first EV, featuring a dramatic shell-like windscreen extending to the car's nose, a five-seat family-friendly interior branded as “the most comfortable Ferrari ever,” and 329 miles of range. It mimics Ferrari's signature engine rev by amplifying electric motor vibrations through the chassis. Orders open this week with a price tag of roughly $640,000.

The reaction was swift and harsh. Reviews ranged from “unlike that of any other Ferrari” via Car and Driver, to a “soulless robotaxi” per autoevolution, to downright ugly across social media. Former Ferrari chairman Luca Cordero di Montezemolo, who led the company from 1991–2014, said publicly: “We risk destroying a legend.” A Morningstar analyst warned the Luce could dilute Ferrari's supercar brand identity.

The Luce arrives as Lamborghini, Porsche, Mercedes-Benz, and Bentley have all delayed their EV timelines. Ferrari is betting it can sell to wealthy families, tech-enthused younger buyers, and Chinese customers where EVs are popular and petrol cars heavily taxed. Analysts say the real test is how long—or short—the order waitlist turns out to be.

Columnist | Daily Dad

Is This Really How You Want Tonight to Go?

Tomorrow you're heading out of town. Or today is the last day of your trip. Or next week is their week with their mom. You're at a point of transition—so why are you spending it like this?

Why are you choosing to pick this battle? Why are you on them about something so insignificant? When you're missing them tomorrow or next week, you're not going to be glad that by being really stern you sped up bedtime by two and a half minutes. You will not remember whether you had to ask them an extra three times to do something. You will not care about their attitude. You will not dwell on the mess they made.

What you will do is regret the argument, regret the conflict, regret that it went this way. You'll be kicking yourself for being such a hard-ass, for making something out of nothing. You'll wish you'd let it go. You'll wish you'd been more helpful, more understanding, more focused on what the real opportunity was—which was connection, which was a little more time together, which was laughing instead of fighting.

So give them—and yourself—the gift of choosing this over that right now.

Build a Weekly Marketing Report That Runs Itself

Using Claude Cowork, you can turn your weekly marketing review into a fully automated workflow—Claude preps the data, drafts the report, and packages everything for delivery.

  1. Create repo folders: marketing-metrics-review/, inputs/ for data, working/ for drafts and verification, skills/, and outputs/ for final reports.
  2. Prompt: “Create a skill that prepares weekly marketing review inputs. Summarize last week's Slack messages, check Gmail for sales updates, review Google Analytics revenue, pull meeting notes from Drive, and save summaries in inputs/.”
  3. Then prompt: “Create a skill that looks at the inputs and creates a first draft of the marketing report, leadership brief, Slack update, and action items.”
  4. Run the input skill, then the reporting skill. Approve the story before Claude expands it. Finally, create a publishing skill to automate delivery.

Pro tip: After each update, ask Claude what changed, what it got wrong, and what should become durable—then approve a small skill update so next week starts smarter.

Texas BBQ Joints Are Closing as Brisket Prices Surge 32% — Iconic Pits in “Survival Mode”

Storied barbecue restaurants across Texas are shuttering under record beef prices, squeezed by drought, tariffs, and a national cattle herd at its smallest in 75 years.

The retail price of brisket is up 32% from a year ago per the USDA. Kirby's BBQ, Brett's BBQ Shop, and Sabar BBQ have already closed. Award-winning Burnt Bean Co. says it's in “survival mode” despite lines out the door and is considering limiting brisket to once a week.

The causes compound: drought has shrunk the national cattle herd to its smallest since 1951, tariffs on imported beef have raised costs, and the US Justice Department is investigating four meatpacking giants—which control 85% of the US beef market—over anti-competitive pricing. A screwworm threat near the border could shrink herds further. The Texas Restaurant Association expects closures to worsen through summer.

Stanford: AI Hiring Tools Show “Clear Racial Disparities”—Shared Models Compound the Problem

An analysis of 4 million job applications found Black and Asian applicants disproportionately screened out—and that shared AI models mean one company's bias can cascade silently through many others.

Stanford analyzed Pymetrics data across 156 employers and found 10.62% of positions showed adverse impact against Black applicants, 5.32% against Asians. The key concern: 42 models are shared across employers, so rejection at one can trigger rejection at another using the same underlying model. Four percent of applicants who applied to 10 positions were rejected from all of them—higher than independent chance.

The study covers 2018–2022 and may not generalize to today's LLM-based tools, which work differently. But it illustrates how bias in shared infrastructure can quietly affect entire industries simultaneously without any individual employer knowing.
