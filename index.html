Columnist Daily Dad

They’ll Be Cool Soon (And That’s a Tragedy)

How many adults do you know who are openly affectionate? Or sing at the top of their lungs in the car? Or put on a cape and cowboy boots or dress wildly outside convention? Or get up and dance joyously when their favorite song comes on? Or compliment every stranger they pass in the grocery store?

Kids do these things without a second thought, without a hint of self-consciousness. But like so much of the rest of childhood behaviors, it is just a phase. It doesn’t last. Soon enough, they’ll be too cool. They’ll be too shy. They’ll be embarrassed.

Understand how special it is while you have it. Understand that it is a ticking clock. With each passing day, their inhibitions are growing, their social skills are developing. Just like it went for you and everyone else you know.

Tempus fugit. Time flies. “Long days, short years,” as the saying goes. Don’t let them slip away because you were distracted.

Let Claude Use Your Computer Remotely with Dispatch

Hand Claude a task from your phone and have it finish the work on your PC — a simple way to keep work moving when you step away.

  1. Update the Claude Desktop app on your computer by clicking Claude > Check for updates in the toolbar. Update the Claude mobile app, too.
  2. Open the Desktop app, click Dispatch > Get started, and turn on file access and keep-awake to ensure Claude works with files while keeping the PC active.
  3. When prompted, pair Dispatch with your phone app. Send Claude a simple task from your phone, like cleaning up emails or organizing files.
  4. Check the result on your desktop or mobile when Claude finishes. The same conversation stays synced, so you can keep going without restarting the thread.

Pro tip: Set up a local project folder before you start. Use one clean folder for the files Claude should edit, save, or organize so the output is easy to find later.

Science — Physics

CERN Successfully Transports Antimatter by Truck for the First Time Morning Brew

Researchers at CERN successfully transported 92 antiprotons in a specially designed tank that prevented the antimatter from touching matter — which would annihilate both.

The 30-minute drive around CERN’s campus is a first step toward an eight-hour trip to Heinrich Heine University in Düsseldorf, where more precise measurements can be made. Studying antimatter could explain why the universe is dominated by matter despite the Big Bang creating equal parts of both. HHU won’t be ready until 2029.
