UN Warns of "Super El Niño" With 80% Chance of Arriving by August

Weakening trade winds over the Pacific have sharply raised the odds of a powerful El Niño cycle — one that scientists warn could be supercharged by the warming climate and trigger cascading disruptions to supply chains, agriculture, and global GDP.

The UN's World Meteorological Organization says El Niño now has an 80% chance of developing by the end of August — and the odds of a "Super El Niño," defined by Pacific Ocean temperatures rising more than two degrees Celsius above normal, have jumped from 25% to 37%, according to the National Weather Service. A normal El Niño is classified by only a half-degree rise.

Every two to seven years, trade winds that normally blow east to west over the Pacific weaken or reverse, pushing warm water back toward the Americas. The effects are wide-ranging: Australia and Southeast Asia dry out and face elevated wildfire and drought risk; the southern US and Central America become more flood-prone; hurricanes and monsoons grow more extreme and unpredictable.

Some data suggests a deep wave of abnormally warm water could push Pacific temps higher than they've been in a decade, potentially setting a new global temperature record in 2027. Scientists warn this cycle could be amplified by long-running climate trends, further straining supply chains for fertilizer, fuel, and food commodities.

The economic stakes are significant. A Dartmouth study estimated a $5.7 trillion drop in global GDP in the five-year fallout from the 1997–98 El Niño — still the benchmark for worst-case planning. Preparation, not panic, is the message from the WMO.

Steph Curry Signs 10-Year Deal with Li-Ning — Betting on China's Rise Over Nike's Decline

The greatest shooter in NBA history is leaving his Under Armour past behind and aligning with a Chinese sportswear company rapidly closing the gap on Nike in its home market.

Curry announced a 10-year partnership with Li-Ning covering basketball gear, athleisure, and a full golf line. Terms were not disclosed. He parted ways with a faltering Under Armour seven months ago after a 12-year run that included a shoe notoriously roasted by the internet in 2016.

The numbers tell the story of why this deal makes strategic sense. Li-Ning generated $4.3 billion in revenue in 2025 — almost entirely from China — versus Nike's $6.5 billion in Greater China. But that gap has compressed sharply: in 2020, Nike ($6.7B) tripled Li-Ning ($2.2B). Li-Ning is now on track to outsell Nike in China by 2030.

Nike has seen Greater China revenue fall 20% over four full years as stiffer domestic competition bites. For the 38-year-old Curry — who has his sights on a post-basketball career in golf — Li-Ning offers both a platform in the world's second-largest basketball market and a potential foothold in the US for the Chinese brand.
