Labor & Economy

The Great Pivot: 80% of US Workers Are Ready to Quit

Morning Brew

Nearly 80% of US professionals say they’re ready for a new job — and roughly half want to switch fields entirely. AI anxiety, aging workforces, and surging demand for tradespeople are reshaping who America is at work.

According to a recent FlexJobs survey, about half of professionals actively looking want to change fields entirely, not just employers. The average career pivot age is around 39, with millennials leading the charge. Remote work options, higher salaries, and better work-life balance top the reasons — but mass tech and media layoffs have also forced many hands.

The workforce is also aging. Baby boomers working longer have pushed the average age of US workers from 40.5 in 2022 to over 42 in 2025, per Revelio Labs. Fields that saw the biggest age jump over the last decade: insurance, sales, real estate, and the service industry.

Meanwhile, construction, plumbing, and the trades face an opposite crisis: a mass boomer retirement with no one to replace them. As data center buildouts surge, recruiting firm Broadstaff reports a spike in demand for electricians and fiber-optic technicians — a potential second act for the hundreds of thousands of tech workers laid off since 2022.

Labor & Workforce

The Second-Act Economy: Who’s Pivoting and Why

Morning Brew

The data on career switching reveals a generational and structural reshuffling of the US workforce — driven by everything from remote work promises to AI-induced anxiety.

Millennials lead pivots at around age 39, but Gen X isn’t far behind — Americans aged 45–54 have held an average of 2.2 jobs in that decade alone. The workforce is aging overall: the average US worker age crossed 42 in 2025 as boomers delay retirement due to rising costs and life expectancy, leaving fewer entry rungs for younger workers in fields like insurance, real estate, and sales.

The exception is in the trades: construction, plumbing, and electrical work face a mass exodus of boomer retirees with no incoming generation to replace them. Meanwhile, the AI-driven data center buildout is creating urgent demand for highly specialized technicians — making it a potentially lucrative landing spot for laid-off knowledge workers.

Sports & Second Acts

Game Over: What Pro Athletes Actually Do After Sports

Morning Brew

The average pro sports career lasts 5–7 years and ends before age 30. Most athletes don’t become coaches or broadcasters — they go into business. The results are mixed.

LinkedIn data from 2022 shows more than a quarter of retired athletes go into sales, where competitiveness and drive translate naturally. Finance is also a common path — Goldman Sachs prioritizes recruiting former athletes; ex-Giants player Justin Tuck has been there since 2018.

The marquee success stories: George Foreman turned his name into a grill empire. Shaq has Papa Johns stakes, car washes, and Big Chicken. Magic Johnson built a multibillion-dollar investment firm and holds stakes in multiple pro sports franchises. Michael Jordan bought the Charlotte Hornets for $275M in 2010 and sold his stake for $3 billion in 2023.

The cautionary tales are just as instructive: Curt Schilling’s video game company collapsed spectacularly. Lenny Dykstra’s financial advice magazine failed. Mark Brunell’s real estate business went under. The athlete-to-entrepreneur pipeline is not a guarantee.

Education

Grad School’s Uneven Bet: The ROI Gap Is Getting Wider

Morning Brew

A new study from American University finds grad school returns vary wildly by field — and with loan rates at 9.08%, the math on some degrees has turned deeply negative.

Pharmacy, law, and medicine deliver strong financial returns on investment. Psychology and social work often yield negative returns once tuition and interest are factored in. Some MBA programs are now offering steep discounts to attract applicants in a softening market.

The clearest winner right now: registered nursing. The Labor Department puts average salary at $93,600 per year, with advanced-degree nurse employment projected to jump 35% from 2024 to 2034. Healthcare created more US jobs in 2025 than any other sector.

AI & Labor

AI Is Driving a White-Collar Exodus to the Trades

Morning Brew

Students are fleeing computer science and data analysis for construction and electrical work. When a robot can’t replace you, suddenly the trades look very attractive.

A Gallup survey found that 16% of college students have already switched majors due to AI’s impact on the job market, while 47% have considered it. About 25% of Gen Z is considering or actively pursuing a trade career instead of a white-collar path, per SupplyHouse. Many office workers are leaving employment entirely to train as electricians or firefighters.

The data center buildout is creating real urgency here: both BlackRock and Meta recently announced tens of millions in investments to train and recruit electricians and fiber-optic cable techs. The caveat: blue-collar employment still shrank last year overall, and average wages remain below those for degree-required positions. The shortage is real, but so is the trade-off.
