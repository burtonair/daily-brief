Omaha Without an Oracle: Can Berkshire Still Deliver the Sizzle?

Berkshire Hathaway holds its first annual shareholder meeting in 60 years without Warren Buffett as host. New CEO Greg Abel faces tough questions as shares trail the S&P 500 by more than 30 points since the transition.

It's not easy taking over hosting duties from a legend (just ask Ken Jennings), but that's exactly what Berkshire Hathaway's new CEO, Greg Abel, will be tasked with today at the conglomerate's annual shareholder meeting. Buffett, 95, remains board chair and will be in attendance, but the meeting — long known as "Woodstock for Capitalists" — is now Abel's show, with an estimated 25,000 to 30,000 guests expected.

Berkshire shares have trailed the S&P 500 by more than 30 percentage points since Buffett's announcement last May. Year to date, Berkshire is down more than 5% while the S&P 500 has risen 4%. Abel faces no shortage of questions: Will he unwind Buffett-era positions? What about the disappointing Kraft Heinz stake? And what will he do with Berkshire's massive $373 billion pile of cash?

Berkshire's Q1 earnings will also be released tomorrow, showcasing Abel's first quarter as the big boss — yet another reminder that the Buffett era is over. Morning Brew

Mad Scientists Want to Bring Back an Extinct Antelope

Colossal Biosciences, the $10 billion de-extinction company backed by Peter Thiel, Tom Brady, and Paris Hilton, announced steps to restore the bluebuck antelope, which vanished from Africa around 200 years ago.

The firm extracted DNA from a bluebuck specimen at the Swedish Museum of Natural History in 2024 and is now engineering new bluebucks in the lab. However, scientists note Colossal isn't technically bringing back extinct species — it's creating hybrids by editing the DNA into a modern relative's genome. CEO Ben Lamm said the technology could help preserve 29 currently endangered antelope species.
