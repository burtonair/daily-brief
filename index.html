250 Years of American Business: From Farms to $1 Trillion Fortunes

The US turned 250 yesterday. Morning Brew’s special edition traces how an agrarian economy that ran on tobacco and firewood became the birthplace of the iPhone, ChatGPT, and the world’s first trillionaire.

In 1776, 95% of American laborers worked on farms. The economy was so fragile that bad weather could trigger a recession. Firewood accounted for 18% of GDP. The most impressive tech on the continent was Ben Franklin’s iron stove.

Two and a half centuries later, the US is home to Nvidia — the world’s most valuable company by market cap — and Elon Musk, the world’s first trillionaire, worth roughly $1 trillion. That trajectory — from whale oil exports to AGI startups — is the subject of today’s issue.

The key inflection points: mass production (early 1800s), commercial electricity (Edison, 1882), air conditioning (1902), microchips (late 1950s), GPS (2000), the iPhone (2007), and ChatGPT (2022). Each one didn’t just create an industry — it restructured how Americans worked, spent, and spent too much time looking at screens.

The richest American in history, adjusted for inflation? Elon Musk at ~$1 trillion. Behind him: Rockefeller ($516B), Carnegie ($475B), Larry Page (~$300B), and Vanderbilt ($284B). Vanderbilt made one major philanthropic donation before dying in 1877: $1 million to build a university. Musk’s philanthropy math looks a little different.

Business History Morning Brew

The most iconic US brands of the last 250 years

Coca-Cola, McDonald’s, Ford, GE, and Big Tech — the companies that didn’t just dominate markets but shaped American culture, labor rights, and what it means to have an eight-hour workday.

Coca-Cola was incorporated in Atlanta in 1892 to sell a syrupy concoction invented by a morphine-addicted chemist and former Confederate soldier. It now controls ~40% of the nonalcoholic beverage market.

Ford and GM gave us the assembly line, the Mustang, and ground zero for the American labor movement. The 1936 Flint sit-down strike at GM saw 136,000 workers hold out for 44 days — and won. The UAW got recognition.

General Electric, tracing to Thomas Edison in 1892, ushered in home appliances. CEO Jack Welch later introduced something even more integral to American business: maximizing shareholder value as the primary corporate objective.

Big Tech: Microsoft hit a $500 billion market cap in 1999. Apple passed $1 trillion in 2018. Amazon and Meta kick-started the era of celebrity CEOs. None of them existed 50 years ago.

Innovation Timeline Morning Brew

250 years of American invention: the moments that restructured everything

From mass production in the early 1800s to ChatGPT in 2022 — each inflection point didn’t just create an industry. It changed what ordinary Americans could do with their time, money, and attention.

1882: Thomas Edison flips the switch on the world’s first commercial power station in Manhattan. Gas lamps begin their long goodbye.

1902: Air conditioning is invented to stop magazines from wrinkling at a Brooklyn publishing plant. It later enables the entire post-WWII Southern population boom.

1955: Dr. Jonas Salk introduces the polio vaccine and refuses to patent it to keep costs low. A decision that looks increasingly unusual in hindsight.

Late 1950s: The first microchips are created independently in Texas and California, igniting the semiconductor industry and beginning the long march from room-sized computers to pocket-sized ones.

2007: Steve Jobs unveils the iPhone — sounding the death knell for BlackBerry and, arguably, boredom.

2022: OpenAI releases ChatGPT, kicking off the race for business leaders to say “AI” as many times as possible on earnings calls.

Economic History Morning Brew

The US economy in 1776: prosperous, fragile, and entirely dependent on the weather

Before Silicon Valley, Wall Street, or air conditioning made the South livable — the 13 colonies ran on tobacco and whale oil, and were somehow the richest people on earth.

Agriculture was the economy in 1776. 95% of laborers — many enslaved — worked on farms. The major exports: tobacco, flour, rice, dried fish, whale oil, and indigo. Firewood was 18% of GDP in the late 1700s.

Yet American colonists were technically the most prosperous people on Earth at the moment of the Revolution. An average American earned ~14 pounds per year vs. 10–12 for a Brit. Colonial wealth was also more equally distributed: over 50% of white Americans were property holders, compared to a small sliver in England.

“Affluent citizens flaunted their wealth by displaying a single pineapple at dinner — which could cost around $8,000 in today’s dollars.”

The 13 colonies weren’t even Britain’s most valuable New World territory — that was Barbados and Jamaica, with their sugar and pineapple exports. Context for everything that followed.

Made in USA Morning Brew

FTC standard: “all or virtually all” domestic

Companies can list caveats like “Couch assembled in USA from Italian leather and Mexican frame.” Levi’s, Rawlings baseballs, and Harley-Davidson motorcycles are all made abroad.

Tariff scorecard: 108,000 manufacturing jobs lost

Trump’s April 2025 Liberation Day tariffs were designed to reinvigorate domestic production. The Congressional Joint Economic Committee found 108,000 manufacturing jobs lost in year one. Reshoring impact could take years to materialize.

11% of US export value comes from foreign inputs

Per the Bureau of Economic Analysis. The US has 14 free trade agreements with 20 countries. True end-to-end domestic supply chains are structurally expensive to rebuild.
