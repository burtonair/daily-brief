AI Pivot of the Year

Allbirds Ditches Sneakers, Reinvents Itself as an AI Compute Company

The sustainable-shoe brand—valued at $4 billion at its 2021 IPO peak—just closed a $50M deal to become “NewBird AI,” a GPU rental business. The stock soared more than 600% on the announcement.

Before the final pair of Forage Green Tree Runners could be loaded onto the moving truck, Allbirds announced it was pivoting from sustainable footwear to AI compute. If shareholders approve next month, the company will change its name to NewBirdAI, raise $50 million, and “acquire high-performance, low-latency AI compute hardware and provide access under long-term lease arrangements.” Translation: buy a bunch of GPUs and rent them out.

The pivot boosted Allbirds’s stock from under $3 Tuesday to over $24 at its peak—an 876% single-day jump. Just two weeks ago, Allbirds had sold its brand assets to American Exchange Group (owner of Ed Hardy and Aerosoles) for $39 million. Shareholders will also vote to strip the company’s “public benefit” status, formally ending its sustainability mission.

Skeptics note that $50 million is chump change in AI infrastructure—CoreWeave, a real player, is spending up to $35 billion this year. Allbirds has no GPU-rental experience. The Rundown AI called the move the same playbook blockchain rebrands used to resuscitate dying tickers—this time with a compute crunch as cover.

Allbirds joins an unusual lineage: former crypto miners have retooled power contracts to score huge AI contracts, and a former karaoke company decimated the trucking logistics industry after its own AI rebrand. Every CEO loves to predict all companies will eventually be AI companies. Nobody had “wool sneaker brand” on their bingo card.

Columnist Daily Dad Daily Dad

Instead of Worrying, Try This

It seems so dangerous. What if something happens? It’s just not a good idea. Do they have a backup plan? It’s just not realistic. Don’t they get how the world works?

Our kids are reckless. Our kids are kids—that means they don’t always think things through. Our kids are their own people, which means they have their own likes and dislikes, their own needs, their own interests. This puts us—the people who desperately want to keep them safe—on a collision course. It’s like in Morgan Wade’s song, where a child’s dreams can sound like a parent’s worst fears.

It’s all very reasonable, but as we’ve said, it’s not their problem. It’s our problem. So we have to find some other way to deal with those feelings. Something you might try: curiosity.

Instead of asking those rhetorical questions—Are you kidding me? You want to do WHAT!?—actually ask them real questions. Try to understand. Try to get them to explain what they’re thinking. Help them come up with a backup plan. Ask them how they think the world works…explain to them how you think the world works. Maybe you’ll learn something that alleviates some of those worries. Maybe they’ll come to their senses. But either way, the two of you will connect—and that always makes things better.

Audit Your Business with Notion’s Built-in Claude Agents

Notion’s prebuilt Claude-powered AI agents can audit your workspace for efficiency—and The Rundown found the output was thorough and well-formatted, closer to a real consultant’s report than a bullet-point brain dump. Here’s the full guide:

  1. In Notion, go to Notion AI in the sidebar, click the + button, browse templates, and install the Business Workspace Auditor.
  2. Give the agent access to relevant pages and workspaces, then hit save. Open it and @ mention the pages or databases you want reviewed.
  3. Prompt: “Audit this page.” It runs its preconfigured audit and produces an issue report with severity levels and a recommended fix for each item.
  4. Give the agent edit permissions and it will make the recommended fixes itself.

Pro tip: The same flow works for other Notion agent templates. Try Business Process Audit to map and diagnose a workflow, or Task Triager to route a brain-dump to your Tasks database.

Geopolitics · Energy

U.S. Crude Tankers Converge on Gulf Coast as Iran Blockade Reshapes Global Oil Flows 1% Better

A record 171 crude tankers are bound for the U.S. Gulf Coast—up from ~110 in a typical month—after the U.S. naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz cut off Iran’s two million daily barrels.

Exports are projected to hit 5.2 million barrels per day in April, up about a third from 3.9 million in March, as European and Asian buyers redirect demand toward American crude at record pace. U.S. crude futures settled near $99 per barrel this week.

The catch: higher global oil prices flow directly into domestic gasoline costs. Some vessels are rerouting around Africa’s Cape of Good Hope, adding up to 15 days of travel time. Meanwhile, the Wall Street Journal reports Iran’s shadow fleet has met its match in the U.S. blockade.

Markets are pricing in optimism for a ceasefire extension, but the underlying regime change in Tehran has made things worse, not better, per the WSJ’s reporting. Is there a diplomatic offramp?

Health · Regulation

Peptides May Get Easier to Access This Summer as FDA Reconsiders Restrictions Morning Brew

The FDA will convene a panel in July to discuss easing restrictions on seven injectable peptides purported to build muscle, reduce inflammation, and improve skin.

HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. is a vocal supporter—he says he uses peptides for injury recovery—and has pledged to loosen regulations. But there’s been little research into many of the claims. Most peptides, including BPC-157 (one of the seven under review), haven’t been evaluated by the FDA for safety.

In 2023, the FDA removed 19 peptides from the list of drugs compounding pharmacies were allowed to produce. That ban fueled a gray market of peptides labeled “research use only” to avoid oversight. Users began treating themselves—with inconsistent dosage and contamination leading to side effects as serious as muscle paralysis and sepsis. Kennedy could fill the FDA’s vacant pharmacy panel seats with peptide-friendly faces before the meeting.

Defense · Policy

Private Equity Takes Over the Pentagon: Feinberg Pushes for $1.5 Trillion Military Budget 1% Better

The Pentagon’s No. 2 official, former private-equity billionaire Steve Feinberg, is pressing Congress to approve a $1.5 trillion military budget—a $441 billion jump over last year and 5% of U.S. GDP.

Feinberg is running the Pentagon’s procurement operation like a portfolio company, surrounding himself with former Cerberus Capital colleagues and taking equity stakes in at least five private defense firms. His team has pressured contractors to accelerate production even before formal contracts exist to fund the work.

The budget surge would replenish missile stockpiles burned through during the Iran conflict and fund projects including the Golden Dome missile shield and a naval expansion. Critics argue the money still skews toward large, complex weapons systems rather than cheaper, faster-to-produce alternatives better suited to the drone era.

Culture · Stat

The Coffee Graders Wall Street Can’t Find Morning Brew

Coffee graders who evaluate quality for the commodities market are in short supply at the NYSE—because the certification test is brutally hard.

The three-stage test includes a written exam, a three-hour grading test, and a final tasting round months later, judged in front of proctors. Fail any part and you start over. Only 5–8% of test-takers pass—compared to 64% of takers of California’s famously tough bar exam. And to qualify for the test, applicants need five years of industry experience. Being a barista—no matter how hip—doesn’t count.
