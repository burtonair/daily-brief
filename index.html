Columnist — Daily Dad

You’re Not Supposed to Be Enough

There will be days when you’re so frustrated you lose your temper and say something you regret. Days when you’re so burnt out you don’t know how to handle what’s in front of you. Days when you’re spread so thin that nobody — not even your kid — is getting the best version of you. At the root of it, it’s always the same thing.

During a disagreement with his wife, author Kurt Vonnegut realized they were expecting something impossible from each other. “When a couple has an argument,” he said, “they may think it’s about money or power or sex, or how to raise the kids, or whatever. What they’re really saying to each other, though, without realizing it, is this: ‘You are not enough people!’”

How did parents in the past do it? They did it together — as an extended family, as a village. They didn’t expect one or two people to do the job of dozens. Most of us don’t live in villages anymore, but there are real consequences to that development — ones every modern parent feels acutely, even more so if you’ve moved away from home for a job or a partner.

We are not meant to parent alone. We are not enough people to possibly handle this responsibility on our own. The good news: there are more resources, communities, and tools available to help us get better at this than any generation before. The support is there if we reach for it.

Build a Clippy-Like Desktop Pet for Codex

Create a custom animated mascot that sits on your screen and shows Codex’s progress — keeping you in the loop while your agent works in the background. No code required.

  1. Open the Codex app, update it, then go to Settings → Appearance → Pets to explore built-in pets and see how they behave while Codex is working.
  2. Ask Codex for four simple mascot concepts that are readable at small sizes and work well on transparent backgrounds. Pick one and simplify if needed.
  3. Prompt: “Use @hatch-pet to create a custom Codex pet,” describing your mascot. Codex will generate the pet pack, poses, and desktop overlay assets.
  4. Return to Settings → Appearance → Pets, click Refresh, select your new pet, and click Wake to bring it to life on your desktop.

Pro tip: Keep your mascot simple. Clear animal or creature designs generate more consistent sprite packs than complex characters with lots of details.

Claude Tag brings agentic AI into Slack — and Anthropic says Slack is just the beachhead

Claude Tag lets entire teams tag @Claude in a Slack channel to handle tasks asynchronously. It builds context over time, works through multi-step jobs using approved tools, and in “ambient mode” flags stalled threads without being asked. Anthropic says 65% of its own product team’s code now ships through an internal version of the tool.

The feature runs on Opus 4.8 and ships in beta for Enterprise and Team customers today, replacing the older Claude-in-Slack app, which retires August 3rd. The key difference from previous chatbot integrations: it’s multiplayer — a whole channel shares one Claude rather than each person querying their own private bot, and it learns the channel’s context over time so nobody re-explains the project on Monday.

Per 1% Better/Vector, the most load-bearing sentence in the announcement is Anthropic’s stated goal of expanding Claude Tag beyond Slack, so teams can tag @Claude “in the many other places they work.” What Claude Tag accumulates — a company’s institutional memory, project context, decision history — is the asset every enterprise software player is currently racing to own.

SCOTUS rules Falun Gong cannot sue Cisco for allegedly helping China persecute its members

The Supreme Court rejected the Falun Gong spiritual movement’s argument that US-based Cisco should face liability under an 18th-century statute for allegedly customizing surveillance technology the Chinese government used to target practitioners.

Cisco denied customizing any system for repression purposes. The court’s decision was based on the scope of an 18th-century statute and does not settle the underlying factual dispute. The ruling has broad implications: it will likely make it significantly harder to sue US technology companies for how foreign governments use their tools — a question that will grow more pressing as AI surveillance tech proliferates.
