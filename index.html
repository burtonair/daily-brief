You're Probably Wrong

Look, if they're not little anymore, if they're more or less on their own, if it's not about money or some real and urgent threat to their health — like addiction — you're probably wrong. If you're arguing or fighting with your kids about something, you're wrong. Maybe not about the specifics of the issue (although, again, probably), but for even making it an issue in the first place.

One of the things most parents have in common when things go sideways is how they make everything about themselves. There's also the tragedy of parents who, for whatever reason, choose religious, political, or cultural beliefs over connection with their children. The Stoic idea is worth remembering: you don't have to have an opinion about everything. Nothing and nobody — children especially — is asking to be judged by you.

So step back. Consider their perspective. Consider letting things go. Apologize. Reach back out. Try to repair. Remember what's actually important. Remember that success as a parent is raising independent adults who make their own decisions.

Check yourself before you wreck anything else. Your children need your guidance, not your judgment. Your connection with them matters more than temporary control — and the words you say and the face they see in conflict are what echo longest in their memory.

How To: Replicate ChatGPT Pulse on the $20 Plan

ChatGPT's Pulse feature — which proactively messages you each day with news and suggestions based on your chat history — is officially a $200 Pro plan feature. But you can replicate most of its value using the "Tasks" feature available on the $20 Plus plan. Here's how:

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Open ChatGPT on the web, desktop, or mobile and enter a prompt such as: "Create a daily, recurring task that briefs me on the daily stock market moves at 5 PM"
  2. To edit or delete tasks, navigate to Settings > Notifications > Tasks > Manage Tasks. Turn on push notifications for tasks and for the app on your phone.
  3. You should start receiving daily, recurring briefs in the chat where you set up the task.
  4. Pro tip: You can have up to 10 active tasks at once. Set up briefs on multiple topics, or link recurring tasks to connected services like Gmail or your Calendar.

A Phone Detox That Actually Works: 68 Days Reclaimed Per Year, Zero Cost 1%

Sahil Bloom's 30-day phone detox — built around environment design rather than willpower — delivered a 70% reduction in screen time, 50% fewer daily pickups, and 134 hours reclaimed every month.

Bloom built the plan after asking himself: what would the audience of your life be screaming at you to change? His answer led him to a structured 30-day detox, which he documented publicly. The ripple effects extended beyond himself — his wife noticed immediate attentiveness, and family members remarked on his presence within two weeks, without being told about the change.

Three Tactics That Worked
  • Grayscale mode — strips color from the screen, killing the visual dopamine pull. Takes 30 seconds to set up.
  • No-Phone Zones — kitchen, living room, family evenings, and creative mornings (5–8am) all phone-free.
  • Timed lockbox — physically impossible to access during key windows; required emailing the company to unlock early.
