US–China AI Battle Escalates: Alibaba Bans Anthropic After Distillation Attack Accusation

Anthropic accused Alibaba of using 25,000 fraudulent accounts to run nearly 29 million Claude exchanges to train its own models. Alibaba fired back by banning Anthropic tools—and Beijing is now considering restricting overseas access to China’s most powerful AI models.

In a letter to US senators, Anthropic said Alibaba ran the accounts to generate synthetic training data using Claude—a technique called distillation—in direct violation of Anthropic’s terms of service. The goal: close the AI capability gap cheaply. Chinese models already rival leading US labs at costs 60%–90% lower, per CNBC analysts.

China’s commerce officials responded by huddling with ByteDance, Alibaba, and startup Z.ai to discuss banning foreign use of their top models—a move that would make AI access a two-sided geopolitical weapon. The discussions reportedly covered both closed and open models, startup-funding limits, and penalties for leaking proprietary AI. Washington ran a similar play in June with Anthropic’s Fable and Mythos export controls.

National security nerves are raw on both sides. Chinese authorities were reportedly “deeply worried” about Anthropic’s Mythos model being weaponized against China. US lawmakers have long feared the reverse. The wild card: experts say China is only six months behind the US—and distillation crackdowns may not matter much if China can innovate independently.

Markets noticed. The Nasdaq shed more than 300 points Tuesday amid anxiety over China’s AI progress, while Rivian dropped 18% separately after announcing a share sale to satisfy an Energy Department loan.

This Is The Way We Teach

We know we teach by example. But when it comes to the things we say, the best way to reach our kids isn’t with lectures or facts and figures. It’s the way Jesus, Lincoln, and all our ancestors did it: through stories.

“Consider that for nine-tenths of all the time that anything like humans existed on this planet,” the historian David McCullough wrote, “all of their information, all they knew about who they were and how to survive, came to them through stories passed down generation after generation.”

If we want our kids to hear what we have to say—if we want to pass along real wisdom—it’s got to come via stories. They need literature. They need to know the myths. They need the riveting moments of human history, the painful mistakes, the classic pitfalls, the transcendent moments of beauty and greatness.

And they need our stories, too. The things that happened to us, what we did, what we learned, who we were, how it helped us become who we are. This is the oldest way to teach—and in a sense, the only way to teach.

Economy Morning Brew

The Global Anchovy Shortage Is About to Raise Your Grocery Bill

Even if you never touch a Caesar salad, the anchovy crisis will find you—the tiny fish is the backbone of the $500 billion farmed seafood industry.

Fishmeal prices have skyrocketed 80% after production shrank 40% in the past year. An unusually strong El Niño warmed Pacific waters, upending nutrient-rich ecosystems that anchovies need. Peru, Ecuador, and Chile—producing nearly a third of the world’s fishmeal—have seen catches collapse. The result: higher costs at fish farms that supply over half of global fish supply, and pricier lox, shrimp, and salmon at the supermarket.
