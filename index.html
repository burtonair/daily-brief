In The End, No Reason Will Suffice

You weren’t being unreasonable. You weren’t being mean. You are a lot of fun. You just couldn’t go play right then. You were tired. You knew exactly how this was going to go. Whatever.

One of the most important audits you could do as a father is counting how many times you are turning them away. How often are you saying “no” when they want to connect, when they want to do something, when they ask for attention or time together?

Because while we often have very good reasons why we can’t right this second, our kids are also tracking the aggregate. They are noticing the trend. They are getting the vibe… and that vibe is that you’re not interested. The vibe is that you’re not fun. The vibe is that you’re the wrong person to ask.

You might have your reasons for each individual “no.” But in the end, no reason really explains why you turn them away so often — why you reject the very thing you say you want. There they are, trying to spend time with you, trying to connect… and you’re saying no.

Build Any Mobile App Idea in 15 Minutes with Replit

Turn an app idea into a testable phone prototype in one session. Define one user flow, give Replit Agent a focused prompt, preview with Expo Go, then improve the weakest part.

  1. Install Replit desktop and download Expo Go on your phone for testing.
  2. Before building, define the smallest version that proves the idea. Example prompt: “A playlist-themed Pomodoro app where I dictate tasks, AI turns them into sessions, and I can skip tasks like songs.” Leave out login and payments entirely.
  3. Paste your spec into Replit, choose the mobile app option, and let Agent build the first version. When ready, scan the QR code in Expo Go and tap through the main screens.
  4. Pick the one thing that feels most unfinished and ask Replit for one targeted fix. Preview again before adding any features.

AI & Tech Rundown AI

An AI Just Dreamed Up a Fully Playable Rocket League — No Game Engine, No Physics Code

Kyutai and General Intuition released MIRA, an open-source world model built with Epic Games that runs live 2v2 Rocket League for four players, generated entirely inside a neural network.

MIRA learned the game from 10,000 hours of AI-vs-AI footage with no human gameplay in the training set. It renders boost meters, ball physics, and crashes in real time, keeping all four screens in sync on a single GPU at 20 frames per second — with zero physics engine or graphics code underneath.

Its memory spans only about four seconds, so when a goal replay triggers, MIRA hallucinates convincing footage of a goal that never actually happened. The teams open-sourced the code, training data, and a playable demo. The goal isn’t to replace games — it’s to simulate the physical world cheaply for robots that need training data.

Tencent Open-Sources Hy3 — A Small Model That Rivals Ones 2–5x Its Size

Tencent’s Hunyuan team officially released Hy3 under an Apache 2.0 license, claiming benchmark results that rival models with 2–5x the parameters.

Hy3 uses mixture-of-experts architecture that activates only a fraction of its parameters per request, letting it run on under half the hardware required for Zhipu’s much larger GLM-5.2. It leads open rivals on web research and tool use in Tencent’s testing, though GLM-5.2 still beats it on SWE-bench coding benchmarks.

The permissive Apache 2.0 license avoids the regional bans that locked EU, UK, and South Korean users out of earlier Chinese model releases — a significant accessibility improvement for the global open-source community.
