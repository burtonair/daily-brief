AI Can Fry Your Brain, Even When It's Helping

New research from Boston Consulting Group warns that overusing AI tools at work leads to a form of mental fatigue dubbed "AI brain fry" — and the sweet spot for productivity is narrower than you might think. Morning Brew

As companies push employees to dive deeper into AI, a new study published in the Harvard Business Review finds that while AI tools can boost productivity, overuse leads to mental fog that makes it harder to focus and make decisions.

Workers reported feeling more productive using one, two, or even three AI tools simultaneously. But once a fourth tool was introduced, the trend reversed. One senior engineering manager in the study likened it to having "a dozen browser tabs open in my head, all fighting for attention."

Tracking software firm ActivTrak found similarly mixed results: employees using AI tools spent twice as much time on email and messaging, but 9% less time on focused, complex work. The extra time AI saves isn't being used for rest — it's being filled with more work, accelerating burnout.

The sweet spot? Employees appeared most productive when they spent just 7% to 10% of their work hours using AI tools, according to ActivTrak data. Industries most affected include marketing, HR, operations, engineering, finance, and IT.

Your Takeaways of the Week: On Modeling, Presence, and the Passage of Time

The events unfolding around the world this winter have made things feel heavier than usual for all of us. In times like these, it can be easy to lose sight of what matters most — the small, daily moments of parenthood that quietly shape who our children become.

This week's passage is a reminder that our children aren't just observing our daily routines — they're being taught what it means to be an adult from our example. Every choice we make, every reaction we have, every value we demonstrate or fail to demonstrate, is a lesson. The question is: are you modeling what you want them to become?

The podcast this week offers a complementary warning: how easy it can be, especially when you're thinking of the next milestone, the next obligation, the next grade, to miss what is happening right now — which is to say, their childhood. We rush forward toward what's next, and in doing so, we miss the irreplaceable present. How much is already gone?

Ryan Holiday has been reading a line this week that seems to speak to all of it: "Buried violence is just a perennial bulb that is fertilized by fear and watered by insecurity." It's a reminder that what we suppress — in ourselves, in our families, in our communities — doesn't disappear. It grows. The antidote, as always, is presence, honesty, and the courage to face what is difficult rather than bury it.

Economy

GDP Revised Sharply Downward; Inflation Gauge Stays Elevated Morning Brew

New economic data highlights cracks in the economy that predated the war in Iran, as fourth-quarter GDP growth was revised down to just 0.7% annualized — half the original estimate — while the Fed's preferred inflation gauge showed prices remained stubbornly high in January.

The combination of weak growth and persistent inflation presents a difficult puzzle for the Federal Reserve, whose chair Jerome Powell is simultaneously fighting off unprecedented political pressure from the administration. The revised GDP figure of 0.7% is a significant deterioration from earlier estimates and signals the economy was slowing even before the conflict with Iran began to affect energy prices and supply chains.

The PCE inflation gauge — the Fed's favored measure — remained elevated in January, limiting the central bank's ability to cut rates even as growth slows. Markets are watching closely for signals about how the Fed will navigate this stagflationary pressure.

Protein Bar Brand David Faces Class-Action Lawsuit Over Nutrition Label Allegations Morning Brew

Consumers of the viral David protein bar are up in arms after allegations emerged that the bar contains 400% more fat and 80% more calories than its label claims — a dispute that has exploded on social media and landed in court.

A class-action lawsuit alleges that independent lab testing found David bars dramatically exceed their advertised nutritional profile of 150 calories, 2 grams of fat, and 28 grams of protein. The comparison to the Mean Girls "weight gain bar" scene quickly went viral.

Founder Peter Rahal has pushed back, responding on social media that the lab test incorrectly counted nutrition from EPG — a lab-engineered fat substitute that he says doesn't get digested by the body. The company is hiring celebrity attorney Alex Spiro to countersue.

The $725 million company is also dealing with a second controversy: a key investor, Dr. Peter Attia, stepped down as chief science officer last month after his name appeared 1,700 times in the Epstein files. David is also facing an antitrust case over its acquisition of the sole manufacturer of EPG.
