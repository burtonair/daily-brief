SK Hynix, a $1 Trillion Chipmaker, Lists on the Nasdaq Today

South Korea’s second-most-valuable company — the memory chip giant at the center of the AI supply chain — makes its US trading debut today, with preliminary orders reportedly exceeding supply by seven times.

SK Hynix’s bread and butter is supplying advanced memory chips — a vital ingredient in AI data centers — to customers like Nvidia. Because the scramble for advanced memory chips has also pushed up prices for its more basic products used in smartphones and PCs, SK Hynix’s profits surged almost fivefold in the first quarter of 2026.

The stock is still up more than 600% in the past year despite a recent dip. The company is looking to raise around $28 billion for aggressive expansion in South Korea and the US, including a chip factory in Indiana.

The IPO gives Americans another memory option beyond Micron and Samsung. Analysts warn the new factories could take years to start churning out chips — but the memory crunch is expected to persist until at least 2027. The listing will also gauge investor appetite for upcoming IPOs from Anthropic and OpenAI.

SK Hynix’s fortunes are expected to mirror the AI boom overall. Wall Street AI bulls, per Morning Brew, are “more hyped than a teen who is about to see BTS perform live.”

Don’t Dismiss These Hints

Children try to discover what they like. They try to separate themselves. It can seem like they don’t like you. But underneath the distance, there’s often something else entirely.

This defined the relationship between Franz Kafka and his stern, successful father. Franz was sensitive. He seemed aloof. Yet, as Kafka details in his heartbreaking book Letter to the Father, he really did want to connect. He was his own person, but he wanted to find something, anything, he could share with this man whose approval he craved.

But it was not to be, because Kafka’s father could not see it. Kafka once gave him a copy of Benjamin Franklin’s Autobiography, hoping Franklin’s description of his pleasant relationship with his own father would awaken Hermann to their problem. But Hermann “sarcastically dismissed the gift as a feeble defense of Kafka’s vegetarianism.”

Your kids are not going to say: Hey, I want your approval. Hey, I want you to show interest in this. Hey, I think this could be something we share. No, it’s going to be more subtle than that — hints, text messages, flickers of curiosity or attempts at conversation. It is your job to hear this. It’s your job to nurture it. It’s your opportunity to build connection.

Dismiss it at your peril.

Use 60% Less Fable Tokens with This Orchestrator Setup

Stop burning Fable tokens on grunt work. Set it up as the planner and reviewer, then send browsing, coding, and research to Codex or a lower-cost Claude model.

  1. Open the Claude app, install updates, and check your weekly Fable limits. In Claude Code, install the Codex plugin and start a session.
  2. Tell Fable to use its Codex skills to save on tokens — sending coding, browsing, and extraction to Codex, and keeping planning and final review with Fable.
  3. No Codex plan? Launch Claude Code in your terminal, choose a lower-cost worker with /model, then use /advisor skill to set Fable as the advisor.

Pro tip: Keep Fable out of routine retries. Use it for the plan, risky calls, and final review; let the worker handle the token-heavy loop. Full step-by-step video guide here.

Real Estate Morning Brew

Office-to-Apartment Boom Threatened After Pfizer Building Near-Collapse

Some financiers are rethinking their involvement in office-to-apartment conversions after the largest US project of this kind risked partial collapse earlier this week, the Wall Street Journal reported.

Officials stabilized the former Pfizer headquarters in midtown Manhattan, but enthusiasm for future conversion projects is now on shakier ground. The lead developer had added floors, a rooftop pool, and other expansions — the steel beams buckled under the added weight from widening ~15 upper levels. Experts now expect more expensive builders’ insurance, stricter regulatory reviews, and softer appetites for complex conversions.

That said, one of the project’s main lenders is still “very bullish” on office-to-residential conversions, and others consider the incident a mere anomaly. The US started this year with ~90,000 new units in progress from office-to-apartment conversions — a 28% increase from last year.

Housing Morning Brew

Home Sales Fell 2.4% in June; Median Price Hits All-Time High of $440,600

Sales of existing homes in the US slipped unexpectedly despite economists projecting a 0.7% increase. The national median home price rose to $440,600 — an all-time high.

The data dampens what had been a relatively strong spring for the housing market. Easing mortgage rates from the winter had suggested the market was emerging from a yearslong slump — but the war in Iran has contributed to rates surging again. Economists have adjusted their 2026 forecast from 4.13 million home sales down to 4.10 million, though that’s still a 1% improvement from last year.

Education & AI Morning Brew

Brown University Economics Professor Says Students Used AI to Ace His Exams

In the scandal heard ’round the Ivy League, professor Roberto Serrano accused his students of using AI to cheat on a take-home exam, and said the school was “meek” when presented with evidence.

The evidence is damning: Serrano’s class swelled from the usual 30 pupils to 86 after he announced take-home exams. The average score on the take-home midterm was 96%, when it typically fell between 65%–80%. Serrano then made the final in-person and worth 80% of the grade. The average final score: a “historic low” of 48.6%.

Broader context: In a recent survey, 29.9% of Princeton students admitted to cheating with AI on an exam or assignment. A recent Stanford graduate wrote in the NYT that AI has become a “fact of life” at the school.
